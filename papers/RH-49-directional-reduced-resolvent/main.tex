\title{Residue-Deflated Directional Resolvents at a Quadratic Critical Fold\\
\large A Quarter-Power Stable-Rank Bridge for Intrinsic Riesz Identification}
\author{Liang Wang}
\date{July 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The intrinsic Riesz-identification theorem for the small-noise
folded-Gaussian quadratic map reduces its only remaining mesh gate to a
mixed directional resolvent gain.  On each Perron or negative-parity contour
one must combine a Hilbert--Schmidt action on the detail-to-coarse coupling
with an operator-norm action on the coarse-to-detail coupling, or use the
symmetric placement.  The global reduced resolvent is neither required nor
expected to be uniformly bounded, but the mixed norm is still difficult to
certify directly.

We prove an exact stable-rank reduction.  If
\(B\) and \(C\) are the two Haar cross channels and
\(\ell_B^{(2)},\ell_C^{(2)}\) are their normalized Hilbert--Schmidt
directional gains, then
\[
 \min\{\ell_B^{(2)}\ell_C^{(\infty)},
        \ell_B^{(\infty)}\ell_C^{(2)}\}
 \le
 \ell_B^{(2)}\ell_C^{(2)}
 \min\!\left\{
 \frac{\norm{B}_{\HS}}{\norm B},
 \frac{\norm{C}_{\HS}}{\norm C}
 \right\}.
\]
For the canonical cell-average folded-Gaussian operator we then prove the
critical-endpoint estimates
\[
 \norm{B}_{\HS}=O(h\sigma^{-3/2}),
 \qquad
 \norm B\ge c h\sigma^{-5/4},
\]
uniformly once \(h/\sigma\) is sufficiently small.  Consequently the
selected square root of stable rank is
\[
 \frac{\norm B_{\HS}}{\norm B}=O(\sigma^{-1/4}).
\]
Thus a purely Hilbert--Schmidt directional product of order
\(O(\sigma^{-\delta})\) implies the previous mixed condition with
\(\gamma=1/4+\delta\).  Every strict \(n\sigma^2\to\infty\) schedule is
preserved whenever \(\delta\le1/4\); uniform or polylogarithmic
Hilbert--Schmidt gains leave a full quarter-power margin.

We also give the exact rank-one residue-deflation identity and a
primal/adjoint residual certificate for finite matrices.  A five-scale
binary64 audit reaches dimension \(40960\).  The outgoing coupling has fitted
powers \(-0.49526\) in Hilbert--Schmidt norm and \(-0.25000\) in operator
norm, giving a stable-rank exponent \(0.24526\).  The eight-node full
Hilbert--Schmidt directional sum has no observed growth, while the direct
mixed and stable-rank-transferred candidates have growth exponents
\(0.19162\) and \(0.20371\), respectively.  These computations are floating
diagnostics, not validated asymptotic upper bounds.  The remaining analytic
task is now a Hilbert--Schmidt range-action estimate and a hard-cutoff
transfer, not a global resolvent theorem.  No arithmetic trace formula,
zeta-zero identification, self-adjoint spectral realization, or
Riemann-hypothesis conclusion is claimed.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} reduced resolvent; Riesz projection; stable
rank; Hilbert--Schmidt operator; Haar coupling; critical fold; small noise;
Galerkin approximation.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Let \(E_n\) denote orthogonal averaging on the \(n\) equal cells of
\([0,1]\), and let
\[
 G_{n,\sigma}=E_n\cK_\sigma E_n
\]
be the canonical cell-average Galerkin compression of the folded-Gaussian
transfer operator associated with the first quadratic band-merging map.  The
preceding intrinsic-identification theorem compared the finite matrix's own
Perron-plus-parity weighted Riesz term with the compression of the continuum
term \citep{WangIntrinsic2026}.  Its exact Schur formula begins quadratically
in the adjacent Haar couplings and gives
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 =O(n^{-2}\sigma^{-3-\gamma})
 \label{eq:rh48-bound}
\end{equation}
provided a normalized mixed directional gain is
\(O(\sigma^{-\gamma})\), uniformly over all dyadic refinements.

The threshold in \eqref{eq:rh48-bound} is sharp at the level of that
ledger.  If \(n(\sigma)\asymp\sigma^{-p}\), every strict \(p>2\) schedule
survives exactly when \(\gamma\le1/2\).  The mixed quantity is substantially
weaker than a global resolvent norm, but one of its two factors is still an
operator norm.  Direct numerical estimation therefore produces only lower
candidates, while a deterministic upper appears to require a large sparse
singular-value certificate.

This paper removes that operator-norm factor from the analytic premise.  The
replacement is a stable-rank multiplier depending only on the two fixed Haar
couplings.  The endpoint geometry of the quadratic fold then selects the
better coupling and costs exactly one quarter power of the noise width.

\subsection*{Main results}

The paper has four analytic components.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
 \item \textbf{Exact residue deflation.}
 For a simple eigenvalue \(\lambda\), with Riesz projection \(P\),
 \[
  R^\circ(z)
  =(z-T)^{-1}-\frac{P}{z-\lambda}
  =(z-T+\lambda P)^{-1}(\Id-P).
 \]
 The same lifted shift gives the adjoint action without forming a dense
 resolvent.

 \item \textbf{Stable-rank norm placement.}
 The mixed RH-48 gain is at most the product of the two normalized
 Hilbert--Schmidt gains times
 \[
  \min\left\{\frac{\norm B_{\HS}}{\norm B},
               \frac{\norm C_{\HS}}{\norm C}\right\}.
 \]
 This statement is exact and does not contain a global resolvent norm.

 \item \textbf{Critical-fold quarter power.}
 For the outgoing detail-to-coarse channel \(B\), a packet supported in the
 target endpoint layer produces an output on the source scale
 \(x=\sqrt\sigma\,\xi\).  The resulting lower bound is
 \(\norm B\gtrsim h\sigma^{-5/4}\), while the Gaussian derivative envelope
 gives \(\norm B_{\HS}=O(h\sigma^{-3/2})\).  Hence the selected stable-rank
 factor is \(O(\sigma^{-1/4})\).

 \item \textbf{Hilbert--Schmidt closure theorem.}
 If the sum of the two branchwise Hilbert--Schmidt gain products is
 \(O(\sigma^{-\delta})\), then the RH-48 exponent is
 \(\gamma=1/4+\delta\).  The full strict \(p>2\) mesh range survives for
 \(\delta\le1/4\).
\end{enumerate}

The distinction between theorem and evidence is important.  The
quarter-power coupling estimate is analytic for the canonical cell-average
kernel.  The Hilbert--Schmidt directional gain itself is not proved uniformly
in \(\sigma\), and the stored sparse matrices retain an eight-sigma hard
cutoff that requires a separate transfer estimate.  The finite computations
show that both missing layers are plausible; they do not replace them.

\section{Folded-Gaussian operator and the RH-48 gate}
\label{sec:setup}

Let
\begin{equation}
 f(x)=1-u_{\rm c}x^2,
 \qquad 0\le x\le1,
 \label{eq:quadratic-map}
\end{equation}
where \(u_{\rm c}>0\) is the first band-merging parameter.  Put
\[
 \phi_\sigma(t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}
 e^{-t^2/(2\sigma^2)}
\]
and define the conditioned folded kernel
\begin{equation}
 k_\sigma(x,y)
 =\frac{\phi_\sigma(y-f(x))+\phi_\sigma(-y-f(x))}
 {Z_\sigma(x)},
 \label{eq:folded-kernel}
\end{equation}
where the denominator is the integral of the numerator over \(0\le y\le1\).
The Markov operator on observables is
\begin{equation}
 (\cK_\sigma v)(x)=\int_0^1k_\sigma(x,y)v(y)\,dy.
 \label{eq:markov-operator}
\end{equation}
It is compact and strongly positive for every fixed \(\sigma>0\).

Let
\[
 W_n=\operatorname{Ran}(E_{2n}-E_n),
 \qquad
 V_{2n}=V_n\oplus W_n.
\]
Relative to this orthogonal Haar split, write
\begin{equation}
 G_{2n,\sigma}
 =\begin{pmatrix}A&B\\C&D\end{pmatrix},
 \label{eq:haar-blocks}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
 A=G_{n,\sigma},\quad
 B=E_n\cK_\sigma(E_{2n}-E_n),\quad
 C=(E_{2n}-E_n)\cK_\sigma E_n.
 \label{eq:cross-couplings}
\end{equation}
The identities in \eqref{eq:cross-couplings} use the exact nesting
\(E_nE_{2n}=E_n\).

For a branch \(s\in\{+,-\}\), let \(\Gamma_s\) be its fixed peripheral
contour.  The top-left fine resolvent is the Schur inverse
\[
 S_s(z)=E_n(z-G_{2n,\sigma})^{-1}E_n
       =(z-A-B(z-D)^{-1}C)^{-1},
\]
and put \(R_{A,s}(z)=(z-A)^{-1}\).  Define
\begin{align}
 \ell_{B,s}^{(2)}
 &=\sup_{z\in\Gamma_s}
   \frac{\norm{S_s(z)B}_{\HS}}{\norm B_{\HS}},
 &
 \ell_{B,s}^{(\infty)}
 &=\sup_{z\in\Gamma_s}
   \frac{\norm{S_s(z)B}}{\norm B},
 \label{eq:left-gains}\\
 \ell_{C,s}^{(2)}
 &=\sup_{z\in\Gamma_s}
   \frac{\norm{CR_{A,s}(z)}_{\HS}}{\norm C_{\HS}},
 &
 \ell_{C,s}^{(\infty)}
 &=\sup_{z\in\Gamma_s}
   \frac{\norm{CR_{A,s}(z)}}{\norm C}.
 \label{eq:right-gains}
\end{align}
Zero couplings may be assigned zero gain.  The mixed and purely
Hilbert--Schmidt sums are
\begin{align}
 \cL_{n,\sigma}
 &:=\sum_{s\in\{+,-\}}
 \min\left\{
 \ell_{B,s}^{(2)}\ell_{C,s}^{(\infty)},
 \ell_{B,s}^{(\infty)}\ell_{C,s}^{(2)}
 \right\},
 \label{eq:mixed-gain}\\
 \cF_{n,\sigma}
 &:=\sum_{s\in\{+,-\}}
 \ell_{B,s}^{(2)}\ell_{C,s}^{(2)}.
 \label{eq:hs-gain}
\end{align}
The condition in the preceding paper was a dyadically uniform upper for
\(\cL_{n,\sigma}\).  Our purpose is to replace it by an upper for
\(\cF_{n,\sigma}\).

\section{Exact rank-one residue deflation}
\label{sec:deflation}

The peripheral projection norm grows logarithmically in the small-noise
limit \citep{WangLogConditioning2026}.  It is therefore essential to remove
the selected pole exactly before interpreting a reduced solve.

\begin{proposition}[Exact deflated resolvent]
\label{prop:deflated-resolvent}
Let \(T\) be a bounded operator, let \(\lambda\ne0\) be a simple isolated
eigenvalue, and let \(P\) be its Riesz projection.  Put \(Q=\Id-P\).  For
\(z\ne0,\lambda\) in the relevant resolvent sets,
\begin{equation}
 R^\circ(z)
 :=(z-T)^{-1}-\frac{P}{z-\lambda}
 =(z-T+\lambda P)^{-1}Q
 =Q(z-T+\lambda P)^{-1}.
 \label{eq:deflated-identity}
\end{equation}
Moreover,
\begin{equation}
 R^\circ(z)^*
 =Q^*(\overline z-T^*+\overline\lambda P^*)^{-1}.
 \label{eq:deflated-adjoint}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(TP=PT=\lambda P\), the space splits algebraically into the invariant
ranges of \(P\) and \(Q\).  On \(PH\), the operator \(T-\lambda P\)
vanishes, while on \(QH\) it equals \(T\).  Therefore
\[
 (z-T+\lambda P)^{-1}
 =z^{-1}P+(z-T)^{-1}Q.
\]
Multiplication by \(Q\) gives \eqref{eq:deflated-identity}; taking adjoints
gives \eqref{eq:deflated-adjoint}.
\end{proof}

For a normalized right/left eigenpair \(Tr=\lambda r\),
\(T^*\ell=\overline\lambda\ell\), \(\langle\ell,r\rangle=1\), one has
\(P=r\otimes\ell\).  Thus a matrix-free primal action is obtained from
\begin{equation}
 (z-T+\lambda r\otimes\ell)x
 =b-r\langle\ell,b\rangle,
 \label{eq:primal-deflated-solve}
\end{equation}
and the adjoint action from
\begin{equation}
 (\overline z-T^*+\overline\lambda\ell\otimes r)y
 =c-\ell\langle r,c\rangle.
 \label{eq:adjoint-deflated-solve}
\end{equation}
These are the systems used in the numerical audit.

\begin{remark}[One pole versus both peripheral poles]
The branchwise deflation removes the pole enclosed by the current contour.
The opposite peripheral eigenvalue remains in the complement but stays a
fixed distance from that contour.  Rank-two deflation is possible, but it is
not required for the identities or computations below.
\end{remark}

\section{Stable-rank transfer of the norm placement}
\label{sec:stable-rank}

For a nonzero Hilbert--Schmidt operator \(X\), define its square root of
stable rank by
\begin{equation}
 \kappa(X):=\frac{\norm X_{\HS}}{\norm X}
 =\sqrt{\sr(X)}.
 \label{eq:sqrt-stable-rank}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Stable-rank norm-placement bridge]
\label{thm:stable-rank-bridge}
For every adjacent Haar split for which \(B,C\ne0\),
\begin{equation}
 \boxed{
 \cL_{n,\sigma}
 \le
 \cF_{n,\sigma}\min\{\kappa(B),\kappa(C)\}.}
 \label{eq:stable-rank-bridge}
\end{equation}
The inequality applies equally to full or residue-reduced directional
resolvents.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each branch and every contour node,
\[
 \norm{S_s(z)B}
 \le\norm{S_s(z)B}_{\HS}.
\]
After dividing by \(\norm B\) and taking the supremum,
\begin{equation}
 \ell_{B,s}^{(\infty)}
 \le\ell_{B,s}^{(2)}\kappa(B).
 \label{eq:left-stable-transfer}
\end{equation}
Likewise,
\begin{equation}
 \ell_{C,s}^{(\infty)}
 \le\ell_{C,s}^{(2)}\kappa(C).
 \label{eq:right-stable-transfer}
\end{equation}
Insert \eqref{eq:left-stable-transfer} and
\eqref{eq:right-stable-transfer} into the two alternatives in
\eqref{eq:mixed-gain}, take their minimum, and sum over the two branches.
\end{proof}

The theorem contains no bound for \(S_s\), \(R_{A,s}\), or either reduced
resolvent on the whole ambient space.  All nonnormality remains inside the
two Hilbert--Schmidt range actions.  The price is the stable rank of one
fixed coupling, which can be studied geometrically.

\section{Critical endpoint packet and the quarter power}
\label{sec:endpoint}

The better coupling is \(B\), which differentiates in the target variable
and exits through the quadratic critical source.  We first record the
endpoint scaling of the conditioned kernel.

\subsection{The endpoint profile}

Let \(x=\sqrt\sigma\,\xi\) and \(y=1-\sigma s\).  Since
\[
 f(\sqrt\sigma\,\xi)=1-u_{\rm c}\sigma\xi^2,
\]
the rescaled first Gaussian in \eqref{eq:folded-kernel} has center clearance
\(u_{\rm c}\xi^2\).  Put
\begin{equation}
 q(\xi,s)
 :=\frac{\phi_1(u_{\rm c}\xi^2-s)}
 {\Phi(u_{\rm c}\xi^2)},
 \qquad \xi,s\ge0,
 \label{eq:endpoint-profile}
\end{equation}
where \(\Phi\) is the standard normal distribution function.

\begin{lemma}[Conditioned critical-fold scaling]
\label{lem:endpoint-scaling}
On every compact subset of \([0,\infty)^2\),
\begin{align}
 \sigma k_\sigma(\sqrt\sigma\,\xi,1-\sigma s)
 &\longrightarrow q(\xi,s),
 \label{eq:kernel-scaling}\\
 \sigma^2\partial_y k_\sigma(
 \sqrt\sigma\,\xi,1-\sigma s)
 &\longrightarrow-\partial_s q(\xi,s).
 \label{eq:kernel-derivative-scaling}
\end{align}
The convergence admits Gaussian majorants after multiplication by any fixed
compactly supported function of \(s\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The integral of the first Gaussian in \eqref{eq:folded-kernel} tends to
\(\Phi(u_{\rm c}\xi^2)\).  The folded companion has center near \(-1\) in
the physical \(y\)-coordinate and is \(O(e^{-c/\sigma^2})\) on the displayed
scale.  Thus the denominator converges locally uniformly to the denominator
in \eqref{eq:endpoint-profile}.  The numerator gives
\[
 \sigma\phi_\sigma(
 1-\sigma s-(1-u_{\rm c}\sigma\xi^2))
 =\phi_1(u_{\rm c}\xi^2-s).
\]
Differentiation in \(y=1-\sigma s\) contributes the second factor
\(\sigma^{-1}\), proving \eqref{eq:kernel-derivative-scaling}.  Since
\(\Phi(u_{\rm c}\xi^2)\ge1/2\), the Gaussian numerator and its polynomial
derivatives provide the claimed majorants.
\end{proof}

\subsection{A normalized Haar packet}

Let \(h=1/n\), and for the \(j\)-th coarse target cell put
\begin{equation}
 \psi_{j,h}(y)
 =h^{-1/2}
 \left(mathbf1_{[jh,jh+h/2)}(y)
 -\mathbf1_{[jh+h/2,(j+1)h)}(y)\right).
 \label{eq:haar-wavelet}
\end{equation}
The functions \(\psi_{j,h}\) form an orthonormal basis of \(W_n\).

Choose a nonnegative \(A\in C_c^\infty((0,\infty))\) with \(\norm A_2=1\),
and define
\begin{equation}
 a_\sigma(y)=\sigma^{-1/2}
 A\!\left(\frac{1-y}{\sigma}\right).
 \label{eq:target-envelope}
\end{equation}
If \(y_j=(j+1/2)h\), set
\begin{equation}
 c_{j,h,\sigma}=\sqrt h\,a_\sigma(y_j),
 \qquad
 w_{h,\sigma}
 =\frac{\sum_jc_{j,h,\sigma}\psi_{j,h}}
 {\left(\sum_j|c_{j,h,\sigma}|^2\right)^{1/2}}.
 \label{eq:haar-packet}
\end{equation}
When \(h/\sigma\to0\), the denominator tends to one by a Riemann sum.

Define the limiting output profile
\begin{equation}
 F_A(\xi)
 :=\int_0^\infty A(s)\partial_s q(\xi,s)\,ds.
 \label{eq:limit-output}
\end{equation}
At \(\xi=0\),
\[
 \partial_s q(0,s)=-2s\phi_1(s),
\]
so \(F_A(0)<0\).  Gaussian decay gives
\(F_A\in L^2(0,\infty)\), and hence \(\norm{F_A}_2>0\).

\begin{theorem}[Critical endpoint lower bound]
\label{thm:endpoint-lower}
There are \(a_0,c,\sigma_0>0\) such that, whenever
\(0<\sigma<\sigma_0\) and \(0<h/\sigma<a_0\), the outgoing Haar coupling
\[
 B_{n,\sigma}=E_n\cK_\sigma(E_{2n}-E_n)
\]
satisfies
\begin{equation}
 \boxed{
 \norm{B_{n,\sigma}}
 \ge c h\sigma^{-5/4}.}
 \label{eq:B-operator-lower}
\end{equation}
More precisely, the packets in \eqref{eq:haar-packet} obey
\begin{equation}
 \frac{\sigma^{5/4}}{h}
 \norm{B_{n,\sigma}w_{h,\sigma}}
 \longrightarrow\frac14\norm{F_A}_{L^2(0,\infty)}
 \label{eq:packet-limit}
\end{equation}
as \(\sigma\to0\) and \(h/\sigma\to0\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For a smooth function \(g\), the symmetric Haar difference on one cell is
\begin{equation}
 \int_{jh}^{jh+h/2}g(y)\,dy
 -\int_{jh+h/2}^{(j+1)h}g(y)\,dy
 =-\frac{h^2}{4}g'(y_j)
 +O\!\left(h^4\sup_{I_j}|g'''|\right).
 \label{eq:haar-first-moment}
\end{equation}
Apply this with \(g(y)=k_\sigma(x,y)\).  Because the coefficient
\(c_{j,h,\sigma}h^{-1/2}\) equals \(a_\sigma(y_j)\), summing
\eqref{eq:haar-first-moment} gives
\begin{equation}
 (\cK_\sigma w_{h,\sigma})(x)
 =-\frac h4\int_0^1
 a_\sigma(y)\partial_yk_\sigma(x,y)\,dy
 +r_{h,\sigma}(x),
 \label{eq:packet-expansion}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
 \norm{r_{h,\sigma}}_2
 =o(h\sigma^{-5/4}).
 \label{eq:packet-remainder}
\end{equation}
Indeed, on the compact support selected by \(A\), target derivatives of
order three have scale \(O(\sigma^{-4})\), the number of active cells is
\(O(\sigma/h)\), and the source output is concentrated on a layer of width
\(O(\sqrt\sigma)\).  The Taylor remainder relative to the main term is
\(O((h/\sigma)^2)\).  Midpoint replacement in the sum has the same relative
order.  Outside the critical source layer the Gaussian majorant is
exponentially small.

Now use \(y=1-\sigma s\), \(x=\sqrt\sigma\,\xi\), and
\cref{lem:endpoint-scaling}.  Dominated convergence gives
\begin{equation}
 \sigma^{3/2}
 \int_0^1a_\sigma(y)\partial_yk_\sigma(
 \sqrt\sigma\,\xi,y)\,dy
 \longrightarrow- F_A(\xi)
 \label{eq:packet-profile-convergence}
\end{equation}
in the rescaled \(L^2(d\xi)\) space.  Since \(dx=\sqrt\sigma\,d\xi\),
the leading term in \eqref{eq:packet-expansion} has physical \(L^2(dx)\)
norm
\[
 \frac h4\sigma^{-3/2}\sigma^{1/4}\norm{F_A}_2
 =\frac h4\sigma^{-5/4}\norm{F_A}_2.
\]
Finally, cell averaging in the source changes this profile by relative size
\(O(h/\sqrt\sigma)=o(1)\).  This proves \eqref{eq:packet-limit}; positivity
of its limit yields \eqref{eq:B-operator-lower} for sufficiently small
\(h/\sigma\) and \(\sigma\).
\end{proof}

The lower bound is a critical-fold effect.  A normalized target packet has
width \(\sigma\); Gaussian differentiation costs \(\sigma^{-1}\), while
the quadratic preimage of the endpoint has source width \(\sqrt\sigma\).
The resulting \(L^2\) gain is the quarter power in
\eqref{eq:B-operator-lower}.

\subsection{Hilbert--Schmidt upper and stable rank}

The target derivative envelope proved previously is
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\partial_yk_\sigma}_{L^2([0,1]^2)}
 =O(\sigma^{-3/2})
 \label{eq:kernel-y-envelope}
\end{equation}
\citep{WangSmallNoiseMesh2026}.  Cellwise Poincar\'e--Wirtinger now gives the
matching Hilbert--Schmidt estimate.

\begin{proposition}[Outgoing Hilbert--Schmidt upper]
\label{prop:B-hs-upper}
For every \(n\ge1\),
\begin{equation}
 \norm{B_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 \le\frac{h}{\pi}
 \norm{\partial_yk_\sigma}_{L^2([0,1]^2)}
 =O(h\sigma^{-3/2}).
 \label{eq:B-hs-upper}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(E_{2n}-E_n\) is an orthogonal subprojection of \(\Id-E_n\),
\[
 \norm{B_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 \le\norm{\cK_\sigma(\Id-E_n)}_{\HS}.
\]
The kernel on the right is the target-variable cell-averaging error.
Applying the one-dimensional Poincar\'e inequality on every target cell and
then integrating over the source proves the first inequality.  Use
\eqref{eq:kernel-y-envelope} for the second.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Quarter-power stable-rank bridge]
\label{cor:quarter-stable-rank}
There are \(C,a_0,\sigma_0>0\) such that
\begin{equation}
 \boxed{
 \kappa(B_{n,\sigma})
 =\frac{\norm{B_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}}
 {\norm{B_{n,\sigma}}}
 \le C\sigma^{-1/4}}
 \label{eq:B-stable-rank}
\end{equation}
whenever \(0<\sigma<\sigma_0\) and \(h/\sigma<a_0\).  The same constant is
valid at every finer dyadic level.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Divide \eqref{eq:B-hs-upper} by \eqref{eq:B-operator-lower}.  Replacing
\(h\) by \(2^{-j}h\) only decreases \(h/\sigma\), so the estimate is uniform
over all \(j\ge0\).
\end{proof}

No estimate for \(\kappa(C)\) is needed.  The minimum in
\cref{thm:stable-rank-bridge} automatically selects the outgoing channel.

\section{Quarter-power closure of intrinsic identification}
\label{sec:closure}

We can now replace the mixed directional premise of RH-48 by a purely
Hilbert--Schmidt premise.

\begin{condition}[Dyadic Hilbert--Schmidt directional gain]
\label{cond:hs-gain}
There are \(C<\infty\), \(\delta\ge0\), \(\sigma_0>0\), and
\(n_0(\sigma)\) such that the two contours lie in the required resolvent sets
and
\begin{equation}
 \sup_{j\ge0}\cF_{2^jn,\sigma}
 \le C\sigma^{-\delta}
 \label{eq:hs-gain-condition}
\end{equation}
for \(0<\sigma<\sigma_0\) and \(n\ge n_0(\sigma)\).
\end{condition}

\begin{theorem}[Hilbert--Schmidt reduction of the RH-48 gate]
\label{thm:hs-closure}
Assume \cref{cond:hs-gain}, and suppose \(n\) is large enough that
\(h/\sigma<a_0\) from \cref{cor:quarter-stable-rank}.  Then
\begin{equation}
 \sup_{j\ge0}\cL_{2^jn,\sigma}
 =O(\sigma^{-1/4-\delta}).
 \label{eq:mixed-from-hs}
\end{equation}
Consequently the intrinsic identification defect satisfies
\begin{equation}
 \boxed{
 \norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 =O(n^{-2}\sigma^{-13/4-\delta}).}
 \label{eq:identification-quarter-bound}
\end{equation}
Every strict schedule \(n(\sigma)\sigma^2\to\infty\) retains the anchored
bulk-square theorem whenever \(\delta\le1/4\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Apply \cref{thm:stable-rank-bridge} and
\cref{cor:quarter-stable-rank} at each dyadic level:
\[
 \cL_{2^jn,\sigma}
 \le C\sigma^{-1/4}\cF_{2^jn,\sigma}.
\]
This proves \eqref{eq:mixed-from-hs}.  The exponent in the notation of
\eqref{eq:rh48-bound} is
\begin{equation}
 \gamma=\frac14+\delta.
 \label{eq:gamma-delta}
\end{equation}
Insert \eqref{eq:gamma-delta} into \eqref{eq:rh48-bound} to obtain
\eqref{eq:identification-quarter-bound}.  The previous threshold
\(\gamma\le1/2\) is equivalent to \(\delta\le1/4\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Uniform and polylogarithmic HS gains]
\label{cor:polylog}
If, for a fixed \(m\),
\begin{equation}
 \sup_{j\ge0}\cF_{2^jn,\sigma}
 =O((\log(1/\sigma))^m),
 \label{eq:polylog-hs}
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 =O\!\left(
 n^{-2}\sigma^{-13/4}(\log(1/\sigma))^m
 \right),
 \label{eq:polylog-identification}
\end{equation}
and every schedule \(n\sigma^2\to\infty\) makes this lower order than the
anchored one-step Hilbert--Schmidt clock
\(n^{-1}\sigma^{-3/2}\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The ratio of \eqref{eq:polylog-identification} to the anchored clock is
\[
 \frac{\sigma^{1/4}(\log(1/\sigma))^m}{n\sigma^2},
\]
which tends to zero.
\end{proof}

The significance of \cref{thm:hs-closure} is not merely a relaxed numerical
test.  Hilbert--Schmidt actions can be audited by block residuals, trace
estimators, and low-rank tail bounds, whereas an operator-norm upper requires
control of every input direction.  The difficult nonnormal geometry has
been moved into an average-energy gate with a quantified quarter-power
allowance.

\section{A posteriori primal and adjoint certificates}
\label{sec:certificate}

We record the deterministic finite-matrix certificate suggested by the
deflated systems.  It is elementary but useful because it separates the
computed directional action from the remaining reduced-inverse budget.

\begin{theorem}[Residual upper for a deflated range action]
\label{thm:residual-upper}
Let
\[
 A_\lambda(z)=z-T+\lambda P,
 \qquad Q=\Id-P,
\]
and suppose \(\norm{A_\lambda(z)^{-1}}\le M\).  For a Hilbert--Schmidt source
operator \(E\) and any approximation \(X\), define
\[
 \mathcal E=QE-A_\lambda(z)X.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
 \norm{R^\circ(z)E}_{\HS}
 \le\norm X_{\HS}+M\norm{\mathcal E}_{\HS}.
 \label{eq:residual-upper}
\end{equation}
For an observation \(F\), solve the adjoint problem with approximation
\(Y\) and residual
\[
 \mathcal D=Q^*F^*-A_\lambda(z)^*Y.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
 \norm{FR^\circ(z)}_{\HS}
 \le\norm Y_{\HS}+M\norm{\mathcal D}_{\HS}.
 \label{eq:dual-residual-upper}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \cref{prop:deflated-resolvent},
\[
 A_\lambda(z)(R^\circ(z)E-X)=\mathcal E.
\]
Apply the ideal property of the Hilbert--Schmidt norm.  The adjoint statement
follows from \eqref{eq:deflated-adjoint}; moreover
\(FR^\circ(z)=((R^\circ(z))^*F^*)^*\).
\end{proof}

After division by certified lower bounds for \(\norm B_{\HS}\) and
\(\norm C_{\HS}\), \eqref{eq:residual-upper} and
\eqref{eq:dual-residual-upper} bound the two factors in
\(\cF_{n,\sigma}\).  A validated reduced-inverse upper \(M\) may grow with
\(\sigma\); sufficiently small outward residuals still make its contribution
negligible.  Sparse Grushin and primal--dual residual machinery from the
preceding finite-matrix work can supply this layer
\citep{WangSparseGrushin2026,WangPrimalDual2026}.

For a continuous contour, node bounds can be transported over an arc by the
usual resolvent identity.  If \(M_0\ge\norm{A_\lambda(z_0)^{-1}}\) and
\(|z-z_0|M_0<1\), then
\begin{equation}
 \norm{A_\lambda(z)^{-1}}
 \le\frac{M_0}{1-|z-z_0|M_0}.
 \label{eq:arc-transport}
\end{equation}
Thus the remaining computer-assisted proof has a finite checklist: enclose
the eigenfactors, certify one deflated inverse budget at the contour nodes,
bound primal and adjoint Hilbert--Schmidt residual blocks, and transport over
the intervening arcs.

\section{Five-scale numerical audit}
\label{sec:numerics}

The computation tests three distinct statements.  They must not be merged
into one evidence level.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
 \item The sparse Haar blocks \(B\) and \(C\) are materialized exactly from
 the stored binary64 matrix.  Their Frobenius norms are direct sums of
 squares.  Matrix-free power iteration gives operator-norm candidates.

 \item Four deterministic Rademacher probes estimate the normalized
 Hilbert--Schmidt actions on eight nodes of each Perron and parity contour.
 Each branch pole is removed by \eqref{eq:primal-deflated-solve}.

 \item Matrix-free primal/adjoint singular iteration estimates the two mixed
 placements at the empirically worst real contour node.  These ratios are
 diagnostics, not certified singular-value uppers.
\end{enumerate}

At every noise level the fine resolution is fixed at
\(N\sigma=20.48\), so the coarse resolution is \(n\sigma=10.24\).  The
largest matrix has dimension \(40960\).  GMRES uses relative tolerance
\(2\times10^{-10}\).

The maximum log residual in the fit for \(\kappa_B\) is

The operator statement remains floating: evaluating \(\norm{Bv}\) on a
power iterate gives a lower candidate for \(\norm B\), and binary64
rounding has not been enclosed.  The analytic theorem, not the regression,

\subsection{Hilbert--Schmidt and mixed gains}

 4.4076,\quad4.6473,\quad4.2154,\quad4.1833,\quad3.9630.

\section{What is proved and what remains}
\label{sec:boundary}

The present result changes the next gate in a material way.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=4.1cm,style=nextline]
 \item[Proved operator algebra]
 Exact rank-one residue deflation, primal/adjoint range-action identities,
 the stable-rank norm-placement inequality, and the a posteriori residual
 upper.

 \item[Proved endpoint analysis]
 For the canonical cell-average folded-Gaussian family, the outgoing Haar
 coupling has Hilbert--Schmidt upper \(O(h\sigma^{-3/2})\), operator lower
 \(\Omega(h\sigma^{-5/4})\), and square-root stable rank
 \(O(\sigma^{-1/4})\).

 \item[Conditional small-noise theorem]
 A dyadically uniform Hilbert--Schmidt directional gain of order
 \(\sigma^{-\delta}\) implies the RH-48 mixed condition with
 \(\gamma=1/4+\delta\).  The entire strict \(p>2\) range survives for
 \(\delta\le1/4\).

 \item[Floating evidence]
 Five-scale exact-Haar Frobenius sums, Hutchinson-GMRES range actions, and
 power-iteration operator candidates.  None is an interval enclosure.
\end{description}

Two analytic tasks remain.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
 \item \textbf{Hilbert--Schmidt range-action upper.}
 Prove \cref{cond:hs-gain}, ideally with \(\delta=0\) or a polylogarithmic
 loss.  The residue-deflated formulation suggests combining a two-pole
 decomposition with smoothing on the coupling ranges rather than controlling
 the global reduced resolvent.

 \item \textbf{Stored sparse transfer.}
 Transfer the canonical endpoint packet and Hilbert--Schmidt estimates to
 the eight-sigma cutoff, row-renormalized sparse matrices with validated
 error.  Previous cutoff and Euclidean bridge bounds provide the natural
 starting point \citep{WangHaar2026,WangSmallNoiseMesh2026}.
\end{enumerate}

If either step fails, the failure is now localized.  A negative result for
the Hilbert--Schmidt gate would show genuine energy growth on the two coupling
ranges; it would not be confused with the already known global residue
conditioning.  A failure of sparse transfer would identify the hard cutoff,
not the underlying critical-fold operator, as the obstruction.

\subsection{No Hilbert--P\'olya or arithmetic claim}

All objects in this paper arise from one explicit noisy quadratic transfer
operator and its Galerkin compressions.  The result does not construct a
self-adjoint Hilbert--P\'olya operator, identify any eigenvalue with a zero of
the Riemann zeta function, prove a prime-power trace identity, derive a
\(T\log T\) counting law, or imply the Riemann hypothesis.  It advances one
operator-theoretic mesh gate inside the existing dynamical program.

\section{Conclusion}

The mixed directional gate from intrinsic Riesz identification admits a
strictly simpler sufficient condition.  An exact stable-rank inequality
replaces the operator-norm range action by a second Hilbert--Schmidt action,
and the quadratic critical endpoint charges only
\(\sigma^{-1/4}\) for that replacement.  Therefore a Hilbert--Schmidt gain
may itself grow by another quarter power before the strict \(p>2\) mesh range
is lost.

The numerical evidence is unusually coherent: the two coupling norms split
into the predicted half- and quarter-power exponents, the full
Hilbert--Schmidt directional product stays flat, and two independent mixed
diagnostics remain well below the critical half-power slope.  The maze has
therefore narrowed from a global nonnormal resolvent problem to a concrete
Hilbert--Schmidt range-action theorem plus a sparse cutoff transfer.  Those
are the next gates; they are not silently assumed here.
