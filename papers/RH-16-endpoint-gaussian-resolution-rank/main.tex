\begin{abstract}
At the first algebraic band-merging parameter of
$f_u(x)=1-u x^2$, parity extraction leaves a small-noise bulk resonance cloud
whose observed degree grows on the scale
$\log(1/\sigma)/(2\log\lambda)$.  The preceding determinant analysis derived
this scale geometrically but did not realize it as an operator rank.  We give
such a realization for the canonical endpoint row family.

Let $p_{2k}\to1$ be the distinguished boundary cycles and put
$\delta_k=1-p_{2k}$.  Their exact inverse-branch construction gives
\[
 \delta_k=C_{\mathrm b}\lambda^{-2k}(1+o(1)),
 \qquad C_{\mathrm b}>0.
\]
For a Gaussian transition row with mean $1-\delta$, conditioned not to cross
the upper state boundary, let $\psi_{\sigma,\delta}$ be its unit Hellinger
fingerprint.  If $t=\delta/\sigma$ and $s=\varepsilon/\sigma$, their affinity
is exactly
\[
 \langle\psi_{\sigma,\delta},\psi_{\sigma,\varepsilon}\rangle
 =A(t,s)
 =e^{-(t-s)^2/8}
 \frac{\Phi((t+s)/2)}{\sqrt{\Phi(t)\Phi(s)}}.
\]
The uncentered synthesis of these rows is unbounded because every fingerprint
converges to the same endpoint vector.  Orthogonally removing that vector
defines the compact resolution operator
\[
 \cR_\sigma e_k
 =\bigl(\Id-|\psi_{\sigma,0}\rangle
 \langle\psi_{\sigma,0}|\bigr)\psi_{\sigma,\delta_k}.
\]
We prove
\[
 \|\cR_\sigma\|_{S_2}^2
 =\frac{\log(1/\sigma)}{2\log\lambda}+O(1)
\]
and, for every fixed $0<\eta<1$,
\[
 \boxed{
 \#\{j:s_j(\cR_\sigma)>\eta\}
 =\frac{\log(1/\sigma)}{2\log\lambda}+O_\eta(1).}
\]
The same law holds for the finite-interval and folded Gaussian row families;
it also holds for $L^2$-normalized linear kernel rows.  The omitted
lower-boundary and reflected masses are exponentially small.
Thus the half-logarithmic clock is a singular-value theorem, not a fitted
resonance count.

High-precision boundary cycles and exact Gram matrices audit the result over
$10^{-14}\le\sigma\le10^{-2}$.  At the parameter-free half-energy threshold
$s_j^2>1/2$, the endpoint rank agrees with the independently selected bulk
cloud degree at six of the seven archived noise levels and differs by one at
the coarsest level.  This agreement is numerical evidence only.  Identifying
the endpoint row rank with the actual Markov resonance count still requires
a dynamical block factorization and the finite-shift reduction; neither is
claimed here.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} transfer operator; Gaussian perturbation;
Hellinger affinity; singular values; effective rank; endpoint boundary
layer; resonance cloud; quadratic map.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

An entire finite-noise determinant can approach a deterministic meromorphic
germ only by developing an increasing spectral complexity.  At the quadratic
band-merging map, the deterministic parity-extracted bulk determinant has the
exact factor
\begin{equation}\label{eq:previous-pole}
 \frac1{1-z^2/\lambda}.
\end{equation}
Its negative leading moments rule out every fixed finite set of limiting
resonances.  Sparse spectra show a growing cloud near
$|\mu|=\lambda^{-1/2}$, with phases close to the roots-of-unity grid of a
geometric pole section \citep{WangBulkScattering2026}.

The missing integer in that picture is the degree of the section.  A boundary
cycle approaches the state endpoint like $\lambda^{-m}$ at original time
$m$.  At two-step component time $k=m/2$, the equality
$\lambda^{-2k}\asymp\sigma$ suggests
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heuristic-clock}
 k\asymp\frac{\log(1/\sigma)}{2\log\lambda}.
\end{equation}
This argument locates a scale but does not define a rank.  In particular, a
count of visually separated eigenvalues would be circular: it would insert
the numerical cloud into the definition one is trying to explain.

We instead ask how many boundary contacts remain distinguishable under the
actual Gaussian resolution.  Transition probabilities are compared in
Hellinger geometry, where square-root densities are unit vectors and their
inner product is the Hellinger affinity \citep{LeCamYang2000}.  The endpoint
rows share a nonzero limiting vector, which must be extracted before a compact
operator appears.  After that projection, the geometric boundary ladder
becomes a Hilbert--Schmidt synthesis operator with a growing plateau of
singular values near one.

This distinction is central:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:three-levels}
 \boxed{
 \begin{gathered}
 \text{endpoint row rank}
 \quad\Longrightarrow?\quad
 \text{dynamical endpoint-block rank}\\
 \phantom{\text{endpoint row rank}}\quad\Longrightarrow?\quad
 \text{bulk resonance-cloud degree}
 \end{gathered}}
\end{equation}
The first quantity is constructed and analyzed here.  The two arrows remain
proof obligations.  The paper therefore advances the effective-rank step
without silently promoting a row-resolution theorem into an eigenvalue
theorem.

\subsection*{Main results and logical status}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{R\arabic*.},leftmargin=3.2em]
 \item The boundary-cycle asymptotic is sharpened to a ratio and gap law:
 \[
  \frac{\delta_{k+1}}{\delta_k}\to\lambda^{-2},
  \qquad
  \delta_k-\delta_{k+1}
  \sim C_{\mathrm b}(1-\lambda^{-2})\lambda^{-2k}.
 \]

 \item Conditioned Gaussian rows have the exact scale-free Hellinger kernel
 $A(t,s)$ displayed in the abstract.  Endpoint projection leaves energy
 \[
  F(t)=\|\cQ\psi_t\|_2^2
  =\frac{\pi-2}{4\pi}t^2+O(t^3)
 \]
 as $t\downarrow0$, while $F(t)\to1$ exponentially as $t\to\infty$.

 \item The unprojected row synthesis has no bounded extension from
 $\ell^2$.  The endpoint-projected synthesis is Hilbert--Schmidt and has an
 explicit Gram matrix.

 \item The squared Hilbert--Schmidt norm and every fixed subunit threshold
 rank obey the half-logarithmic law in \eqref{eq:heuristic-clock}.  These are
 analytic theorems proved without finite matrices.

 \item The theorem extends from Hellinger fingerprints to every fixed positive
 power of the Gaussian density.  In particular it holds for
 $L^2$-normalized linear kernel rows.  Conditioning on $[-1,1]$ and folding
 by $y\mapsto|y|$ preserve the same rank law.

 \item High-precision computations evaluate
 $C_{\mathrm b}=0.4608051492\ldots$, test the exact Gram kernel and show a
 bounded staircase defect down to $\sigma=10^{-14}$.  These decimals and
 finite spectra are numerical diagnostics.

 \item With the fixed half-energy threshold $\eta=2^{-1/2}$, the model ranks
 are $2,3,4,5,5,6,7$ on the seven RH-15 noise levels.  The archived cloud
 degrees are $3,3,4,5,5,6,7$.  This close match does not prove either arrow
 in \eqref{eq:three-levels}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{The geometric boundary ladder}
\label{sec:boundary-ladder}

Let $\uc$ be the root in $(1,2)$ of
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cubic}
 u^3-2u^2+2u-2=0,
\end{equation}
and put
\begin{equation}\label{eq:constants}
 r=\uc-1,
 \qquad
 \lambda=2\uc r=1.678573510428322\ldots,
 \qquad
 a=\lambda^{-2}=0.354910844401770\ldots.
\end{equation}
For $f(x)=1-\uc x^2$, the postcritical orbit is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:critical-orbit}
 0\longmapsto1\longmapsto-r\longmapsto r\longmapsto r.
\end{equation}

The two positive inverse-branch compositions used by the boundary word are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h-q}
 h=g_+\circ g_+,
 \qquad
 q=g_+\circ g_-,
 \qquad
 g_\pm(y)=\pm\sqrt{\frac{1-y}{\uc}}.
\end{equation}
They satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h-q-data}
 h(r)=r,
 \quad h'(r)=a,
 \quad q(r)=1,
 \quad q'(r)=-\frac1{2\uc\lambda}.
\end{equation}
For $k\ge1$, let $p_{2k}$ be the unique fixed point of
\begin{equation}\label{eq:boundary-fixed-point}
 p_{2k}=q\bigl(h^{k-1}(p_{2k})\bigr),
 \qquad
 \delta_k=1-p_{2k}.
\end{equation}
It is the primitive orbit coded by $CA(CB)^{k-1}$.

The boundary-crowding theorem of \citet{WangLongCycle2026}, proved using the
Koenigs coordinate of $h$ at $r$, gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:boundary-asymptotic}
 \delta_k=C_{\mathrm b}a^k(1+o(1)),
 \qquad C_{\mathrm b}>0.
\end{equation}
Only this analytic asymptotic, not its decimal constant, is needed below.

\begin{corollary}[Ratio and spacing law]
\label{cor:ratio-spacing}
As $k\to\infty$,
\begin{align}
 \frac{\delta_{k+1}}{\delta_k}&\longrightarrow a,
 \label{eq:ratio-law}\\
 p_{2(k+1)}-p_{2k}
 =\delta_k-\delta_{k+1}
 &=C_{\mathrm b}(1-a)a^k(1+o(1)).
 \label{eq:spacing-law}
\end{align}
In particular, for some $k_0$, constants $c_\pm>0$ and $0<q<1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:geometric-bounds}
 c_-a^k\le\delta_k\le c_+a^k,
 \qquad
 \frac{\delta_{k+1}}{\delta_k}\le q
 \quad(k\ge k_0).
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The first two statements follow by dividing and subtracting
\eqref{eq:boundary-asymptotic}.  The uniform bounds follow by increasing
$k_0$ and absorbing the finitely many earlier indices into the constants.
\end{proof}

The deterministic ladder therefore has one new scale whenever $\sigma$ is
multiplied by $a$.  Define its continuous clock
\begin{equation}\label{eq:clock}
 H_\sigma
 :=\frac{\log(1/\sigma)}{|\log a|}
 =\frac{\log(1/\sigma)}{2\log\lambda}.
\end{equation}
The purpose of the next sections is to show that $H_\sigma$ counts singular
directions of a canonical Gaussian operator up to a bounded error.

\section{Exact conditioned-Gaussian geometry}
\label{sec:gaussian-geometry}

Let
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-density}
 \phi_\sigma(x)=\frac1{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}
 e^{-x^2/(2\sigma^2)},
\end{equation}
and let $\Phi$ be the standard normal distribution function.  A Gaussian row
with mean $1-\delta$, conditioned not to cross the upper boundary, is the
probability measure on $(-\infty,1]$ given by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:conditioned-row}
 \frac{d\nu_{\sigma,\delta}}{dy}(y)
 =\frac{\phi_\sigma(y-(1-\delta))}{\Phi(\delta/\sigma)}
 \mathbf1_{(-\infty,1]}(y),
 \qquad \delta\ge0.
\end{equation}
Its Hellinger fingerprint is the unit vector
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fingerprint}
 \psi_{\sigma,\delta}(y)
 =\sqrt{\frac{d\nu_{\sigma,\delta}}{dy}(y)}
 \in\Hilbert.
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Exact Hellinger affinity]
\label{thm:affinity}
For $\delta,\varepsilon\ge0$, put
$t=\delta/\sigma$ and $s=\varepsilon/\sigma$.  Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:affinity}
 \boxed{
 \left\langle\psi_{\sigma,\delta},
 \psi_{\sigma,\varepsilon}\right\rangle
 =A(t,s)
 :=e^{-(t-s)^2/8}
 \frac{\Phi((t+s)/2)}{\sqrt{\Phi(t)\Phi(s)}}.}
\end{equation}
In particular, the row geometry depends on $\delta$ and $\sigma$ only through
their ratio.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Use the boundary coordinate $x=(y-1)/\sigma\le0$.  Taking square roots in
\eqref{eq:conditioned-row} gives
\begin{align*}
 \left\langle\psi_{\sigma,\delta},
 \psi_{\sigma,\varepsilon}\right\rangle
 &=\frac1{\sqrt{2\pi\Phi(t)\Phi(s)}}
 \int_{-\infty}^0
 \exp\left[-\frac{(x+t)^2+(x+s)^2}{4}\right]dx.
\end{align*}
Completing the square separates the factor $e^{-(t-s)^2/8}$; the remaining
integral is $\sqrt{2\pi}\Phi((t+s)/2)$.  This proves
\eqref{eq:affinity}.
\end{proof}

Let $\psi_{\sigma,0}$ be the limiting endpoint fingerprint and define the
orthogonal projection
\begin{equation}\label{eq:endpoint-projection}
 \cQ_\sigma
 =\Id-|\psi_{\sigma,0}\rangle\langle\psi_{\sigma,0}|.
\end{equation}
The energy that remains after endpoint extraction is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:F-definition}
 F(t)
 :=\|\cQ_\sigma\psi_{\sigma,\sigma t}\|_2^2
 =1-A(t,0)^2.
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[Resolved and unresolved endpoint regimes]
\label{lem:F-bounds}
The function $F$ is continuous, $0\le F(t)<1$, and
\begin{align}
 F(t)
 &=\frac{\pi-2}{4\pi}t^2+O(t^3)
 &&(t\downarrow0),
 \label{eq:F-small}\\
 1-F(t)
 &=2e^{-t^2/4}(1+o(1))
 &&(t\to\infty).
 \label{eq:F-large}
\end{align}
Consequently there are $C,c>0$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:F-global-bounds}
 F(t)\le Ct^2\quad(0\le t\le1),
 \qquad
 1-F(t)\le Ce^{-ct^2}\quad(t\ge1).
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
From \eqref{eq:affinity},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:endpoint-overlap}
 A(t,0)=e^{-t^2/8}
 \frac{\Phi(t/2)}{\sqrt{\Phi(t)\Phi(0)}}.
\end{equation}
Taylor expansion of $\log A(t,0)$ at zero gives
\[
 \log A(t,0)
 =-\frac{\pi-2}{8\pi}t^2+O(t^3).
\]
Substitution in \eqref{eq:F-definition} proves \eqref{eq:F-small}.  As
$t\to\infty$, both distribution functions in the numerator and the
nonconstant denominator tend to one, while $\Phi(0)=1/2$.  Thus
$A(t,0)^2=2e^{-t^2/4}(1+o(1))$, proving \eqref{eq:F-large}.  Compactness of
the transition region supplies the global bounds.
\end{proof}

The coefficient in \eqref{eq:F-small} is not fitted.  It quantifies the loss
of one endpoint row against another when their means differ by much less than
one noise width.

\subsection{Power fingerprints and linear kernel rows}

Hellinger geometry is useful for comparing probability rows, but the square
root is nonlinear.  To connect the resolution count to the linear kernel row
space, fix $\beta>0$ and define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:power-fingerprint}
 \psi^{(\beta)}_{\sigma,\delta}
 =\frac{(d\nu_{\sigma,\delta}/dy)^\beta}
 {\|(d\nu_{\sigma,\delta}/dy)^\beta\|_2}.
\end{equation}
The Hellinger vector is $\beta=1/2$, while $\beta=1$ is the
$L^2$-normalized linear Gaussian kernel row.  The $L^1$ conditioning
normalizer cancels from the latter direction.

\begin{proposition}[Exact powered-row affinity]
\label{prop:powered-affinity}
For every fixed $\beta>0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:powered-affinity}
 \left\langle
 \psi^{(\beta)}_{\sigma,\delta},
 \psi^{(\beta)}_{\sigma,\varepsilon}
 \right\rangle
 =A_\beta(t,s)
 :=e^{-\beta(t-s)^2/4}
 \frac{\Phi(\sqrt{\beta/2}\,(t+s))}
 {\sqrt{\Phi(\sqrt{2\beta}\,t)
 \Phi(\sqrt{2\beta}\,s)}}.
\end{equation}
For $\beta=1/2$, this is \eqref{eq:affinity}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In the boundary coordinate, the normalized vector is proportional to
$e^{-\beta(x+t)^2/2}\mathbf1_{x\le0}$.  Complete the square in the product of
the two vectors.  Its normalization integrals are
$\sqrt{\pi/\beta}\,\Phi(\sqrt{2\beta}\,t)$ and the corresponding expression
with $s$, which gives \eqref{eq:powered-affinity}.
\end{proof}

For each fixed $\beta$, the projected energy
$F_\beta(t)=1-A_\beta(t,0)^2$ is $O_\beta(t^2)$ at zero and approaches one
with a Gaussian tail.  These are exactly the two properties used in the rank
proof.

\subsection{The physical interval and folding}

The normal form above omits the lower state boundary.  For the present ladder
this omission is exponentially accurate because
$p_{2k}\ge p_2=0.9010184\ldots$.  Let $\nu^I_{\sigma,\delta}$ be the Gaussian
with mean $1-\delta$, conditioned on $[-1,1]$, and let
$\psi^I_{\sigma,\delta}$ be its Hellinger fingerprint, extended by zero to
$(-\infty,1]$.

\begin{proposition}[Physical-row robustness]
\label{prop:physical-rows}
There are $C,c,\sigma_0>0$ such that, uniformly for
$0\le\delta\le\delta_1$ and $0<\sigma<\sigma_0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:interval-row-error}
 \|\psi^I_{\sigma,\delta}-\psi_{\sigma,\delta}\|_2
 \le Ce^{-c/\sigma^2}.
\end{equation}
After folding the conditioned row by $y\mapsto|y|$, its Hellinger affinity
matrix differs from the positive half-line affinity matrix by the same type
of exponentially small bound on every block of $O(H_\sigma)$ resolved rows.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Under the upper-conditioned law, the omitted mass below $-1$ is bounded by
the Gaussian tail at distance $1+p_2>1.9$ from the mean.  It is therefore
$O(e^{-c/\sigma^2})$, uniformly in the ladder.  Conditioning once more on
$y\ge-1$ changes the square-root density by the square root of this tail,
which is still of the stated form after changing $c$.  Folding adds the
reflected density from $y<0$.  Its mass is bounded by the Gaussian tail at
distance $p_2$ and is again exponentially small.  Hellinger distance controls
the change of affinity, and a block with $O(H_\sigma)$ entries retains an
exponentially small total error.
\end{proof}

This proposition will let the rank theorem transfer to the physical row
family.  It does not insert the deterministic Jacobian or time ordering of
the transfer operator; those are dynamical data beyond row resolution.

\section{Endpoint extraction and the compact resolution operator}
\label{sec:resolution-operator}

One might first try the row synthesis
\begin{equation}\label{eq:uncentered-synthesis}
 \cU_\sigma c
 =\sum_{k\ge1}c_k\psi_{\sigma,\delta_k}
\end{equation}
on finitely supported sequences.  This is the wrong object.

\begin{proposition}[Why endpoint extraction is necessary]
\label{prop:unbounded}
The map in \eqref{eq:uncentered-synthesis} has no bounded extension from
$\ellspace$ to $\Hilbert$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $\delta_k\to0$, \cref{thm:affinity} gives
$\psi_{\sigma,\delta_k}\to\psi_{\sigma,0}$ in $L^2$.  Given $N$, choose a
block of $N$ sufficiently late rows, and put $c_k=N^{-1/2}$ on that block.
Then $\|c\|_{\ell^2}=1$, while
\[
 \cU_\sigma c
 =\sqrt N\,\psi_{\sigma,0}+o(1)
\]
after choosing the block far enough along the ladder.  The operator norm on
finitely supported sequences is therefore unbounded.
\end{proof}

The common endpoint vector is the row-space analogue of a peripheral mode:
it must be removed before a compact residual object can exist.

\begin{definition}[Endpoint Gaussian resolution operator]
\label{def:resolution-operator}
For $\sigma>0$, define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:R-definition}
 \cR_\sigma:\ellspace\longrightarrow\Hilbert,
 \qquad
 \cR_\sigma e_k
 =\cQ_\sigma\psi_{\sigma,\delta_k}.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Hilbert--Schmidt property and exact Gram kernel]
\label{prop:gram}
The operator $\cR_\sigma$ is Hilbert--Schmidt.  If
$t_k=\delta_k/\sigma$, then
\begin{align}
 \|\cR_\sigma\|_{S_2}^2
 &=\sum_{k\ge1}F(t_k),
 \label{eq:HS-exact}\\
 (\cR_\sigma^*\cR_\sigma)_{jk}
 &=A(t_j,t_k)-A(t_j,0)A(0,t_k).
 \label{eq:Gram-exact}
\end{align}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The Gram identity follows from the rank-one projection
\eqref{eq:endpoint-projection}.  For fixed $\sigma$, the geometric bound
\eqref{eq:geometric-bounds} and \eqref{eq:F-global-bounds} give
\[
 \sum_{k\ge1}\|\cR_\sigma e_k\|_2^2
 =\sum_{k\ge1}F(\delta_k/\sigma)<\infty.
\]
This is the Hilbert--Schmidt criterion \citep{Simon2005} and proves
\eqref{eq:HS-exact}.
\end{proof}

\section{Hilbert--Schmidt energy and the half-logarithmic clock}
\label{sec:HS-law}

The first rank law is already visible in the trace of the Gram operator.

\begin{theorem}[Half-logarithmic Hilbert--Schmidt law]
\label{thm:HS-law}
As $\sigma\downarrow0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:HS-law}
 \boxed{
 \|\cR_\sigma\|_{S_2}^2
 =H_\sigma+O(1)
 =\frac{\log(1/\sigma)}{2\log\lambda}+O(1).}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let
\begin{equation}\label{eq:J-definition}
 J_\sigma=\max\{k:\delta_k\ge\sigma\}.
\end{equation}
The two-sided bounds in \eqref{eq:geometric-bounds} give
\begin{equation}\label{eq:J-clock}
 J_\sigma=H_\sigma+O(1).
\end{equation}
Choose $0<a_-<a<a_+<1$ so that, after increasing $k_0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ratio-bracket}
 a_-\le\frac{\delta_{k+1}}{\delta_k}\le a_+
 \qquad(k\ge k_0).
\end{equation}
Maximality in \eqref{eq:J-definition} makes
$1\le t_{J_\sigma}<a_-^{-1}$.  Moving backward and forward from that index
therefore gives, up to finitely many initial terms,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:t-geometric}
 t_{J_\sigma-m}\ge a_+^{-m},
 \qquad
 t_{J_\sigma+m}\le a_-^{-1}a_+^m.
\end{equation}

By \eqref{eq:F-global-bounds},
\begin{align*}
 \left|
 \sum_{k\ge1}F(t_k)-J_\sigma
 \right|
 &\le
 \sum_{k\le J_\sigma}(1-F(t_k))
 +\sum_{k>J_\sigma}F(t_k)\\
 &\le C\sum_{m\ge0}e^{-c a_+^{-2m}}
 +C\sum_{m\ge1}a_+^{2m}+O(1).
\end{align*}
Both series are finite independently of $\sigma$.  Combine this bound with
\eqref{eq:HS-exact} and \eqref{eq:J-clock}.
\end{proof}

The bounded remainder need not converge.  An exact geometric ladder already
has a discrete scaling phase.

\begin{proposition}[Exact geometric renormalization identity]
\label{prop:geometric-renormalization}
If $\delta_k=C a^k$ exactly and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:geometric-energy}
 E(\sigma)=\sum_{k\ge1}F(Ca^k/\sigma),
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:energy-renormalization}
 E(a\sigma)-E(\sigma)=F(C/\sigma)\longrightarrow1.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Shift the summation index in $E(a\sigma)$ and use $F(t)\to1$.
\end{proof}

Thus one unit of Hilbert--Schmidt energy is released whenever the noise is
reduced by the deterministic factor $a=\lambda^{-2}$.  The bounded staircase
seen numerically is a consequence of discrete endpoint scaling, not an
anomalous fitted exponent.

\section{The threshold singular-value theorem}
\label{sec:rank-law}

Let
\begin{equation}\label{eq:singular-values}
 s_1(\cR_\sigma)\ge s_2(\cR_\sigma)\ge\cdots\ge0
\end{equation}
be the singular values, repeated with multiplicity, and for $\eta>0$ put
\begin{equation}\label{eq:threshold-count}
 \ranketa(\sigma)
 =\#\{j:s_j(\cR_\sigma)>\eta\}.
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Half-logarithmic effective rank]
\label{thm:rank-law}
For every fixed $0<\eta<1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rank-law}
 \boxed{
 \ranketa(\sigma)
 =H_\sigma+O_\eta(1)
 =\frac{\log(1/\sigma)}{2\log\lambda}+O_\eta(1)}
 \qquad(\sigma\downarrow0).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We prove the upper and lower bounds separately.

\emph{Upper bound.}
Split $\cR_\sigma=R_{\le J}+R_{>J}$ at $J=J_\sigma$ from
\eqref{eq:J-definition}.  The first operator has rank at most $J$.  The tail
estimate in the proof of \cref{thm:HS-law} gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tail-HS}
 \|R_{>J}\|_{S_2}^2\le C.
\end{equation}
The approximation-number inequality followed by the Hilbert--Schmidt
Chebyshev bound yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rank-upper}
 \ranketa(\sigma)
 \le J+\eta^{-2}\|R_{>J}\|_{S_2}^2
 \le H_\sigma+O_\eta(1).
\end{equation}

\emph{Lower bound.}
Fix $q<1$ and $k_0$ as in \eqref{eq:geometric-bounds}.  For a constant $K>1$
to be chosen, let
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M-definition}
 M_\sigma=\max\{k:\delta_k\ge K\sigma\}.
\end{equation}
Again $M_\sigma=H_\sigma+O_K(1)$.  For
$k_0\le k<M_\sigma$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:resolved-spacing}
 \frac{\delta_k-\delta_{k+1}}\sigma
 \ge(1-q)K=:L.
\end{equation}
Hence the dimensionless centers $t_k$ in this block are ordered with spacing
at least $L$.

For $t,s\ge K$, the exact affinity obeys
\begin{equation}\label{eq:affinity-majorants}
 A(t,s)\le\Phi(K)^{-1}e^{-(t-s)^2/8},
 \qquad
 A(t,0)\le
 \sqrt{\frac2{\Phi(K)}}e^{-t^2/8}.
\end{equation}
Let $G$ be the Gram matrix \eqref{eq:Gram-exact} restricted to
$k_0\le k\le M_\sigma$.  The off-diagonal row sums of the first term are
bounded by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:off-diagonal-bound}
 \frac2{\Phi(K)}\sum_{m\ge1}e^{-L^2m^2/8},
\end{equation}
while the operator norm of the endpoint rank-one subtraction is bounded by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:endpoint-vector-bound}
 C\sum_{m\ge0}e^{-(K+mL)^2/4}.
\end{equation}
Both bounds tend to zero as $K\to\infty$, because $L=(1-q)K$.
Choose $K=K(\eta)$ so large that their sum is less than $1-\eta^2$.
Gershgorin's theorem, or equivalently the Schur bound, then gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:riesz-lower}
 G\ge\eta^2\Id.
\end{equation}
Thus $\cR_\sigma$ is bounded below by $\eta$ on a subspace of dimension
$M_\sigma-k_0+1$.  The min--max characterization of singular values gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rank-lower}
 \ranketa(\sigma)
 \ge M_\sigma-k_0+1
 =H_\sigma-O_\eta(1).
\end{equation}
Together with \eqref{eq:rank-upper}, this proves \eqref{eq:rank-law}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Half-energy rank]
\label{cor:half-energy}
At the fixed threshold
\begin{equation}\label{eq:half-energy-threshold}
 \eta_*=2^{-1/2},
\end{equation}
the number of singular directions retaining more than half their squared
energy satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:half-energy-law}
 \mathfrak n_{\eta_*}(\sigma)
 =\frac{\log(1/\sigma)}{2\log\lambda}+O(1).
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

The special threshold is used only for a parameter-free numerical audit.  The
theorem itself holds at every fixed threshold strictly between zero and one.

\begin{corollary}[Power-row universality]
\label{cor:power-universality}
Let $\cR_\sigma^{(\beta)}$ be defined as in
\eqref{eq:R-definition}, using the power fingerprints
\eqref{eq:power-fingerprint} and projection away from
$\psi^{(\beta)}_{\sigma,0}$.  For every fixed $\beta>0$,
\begin{align}
 \|\cR_\sigma^{(\beta)}\|_{S_2}^2
 &=H_\sigma+O_\beta(1),
 \label{eq:power-HS-law}\\
 \#\{j:s_j(\cR_\sigma^{(\beta)})>\eta\}
 &=H_\sigma+O_{\beta,\eta}(1),
 \qquad 0<\eta<1.
 \label{eq:power-rank-law}
\end{align}
In particular, the half-logarithmic law holds for the linear kernel-row
directions $\beta=1$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The exact kernel \eqref{eq:powered-affinity} gives
$F_\beta(t)\le C_\beta t^2$ near zero and
$1-F_\beta(t)\le C_\beta e^{-c_\beta t^2}$ at infinity.  For separated
resolved rows, its off-diagonal affinity is bounded by
$C_{\beta,K}e^{-\beta(t-s)^2/4}$.  The proofs of
\cref{thm:HS-law,thm:rank-law} therefore apply verbatim with constants
depending on $\beta$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Physical and folded row ranks]
\label{cor:physical-rank}
Define the endpoint-projected resolution operators from either the Hellinger
fingerprints or the $L^2$-normalized linear Gaussian rows conditioned on
$[-1,1]$, and likewise from their folded versions on $[0,1]$.  Their
Hilbert--Schmidt energy and threshold rank obey the corresponding laws above.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
For unresolved rows $\delta_k\le\sigma$, smooth dependence of the normalized
finite-interval fingerprint on $t=\delta/\sigma$ gives the same bound
$\|\cQ\psi_t\|^2\le Ct^2$.  This supplies the Hilbert--Schmidt tail and the
upper rank bound.  The $O(H_\sigma)$ resolved Gram block differs from the
half-line block by $O(H_\sigma e^{-c/\sigma^2})$ in row-sum norm by
\cref{prop:physical-rows}.  Hence the Riesz lower bound
\eqref{eq:riesz-lower} persists for small $\sigma$.  The finitely many
remaining noise values are absorbed into the $O(1)$ constants.
\end{proof}

\section{What the theorem supplies to bulk scattering}
\label{sec:bulk-bridge}

The pole section in the preceding paper was
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pole-section}
 \Pi_N(q)=1+q+\cdots+q^N,
 \qquad q=z^2/\lambda.
\end{equation}
It resolves the deterministic pole through $2N$ reciprocal resonances.  The
integer $N$ was numerically selected from the noisy spectrum and conjectured
to obey the half-logarithmic clock.  The present theorem provides an intrinsic
candidate that is independent of eigenvalue selection:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:intrinsic-degree}
 N_\sigma^{\mathrm{row}}
 :=\mathfrak n_{2^{-1/2}}(\sigma).
\end{equation}

Three conclusions are now rigorous.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),leftmargin=2.5em]
 \item The factor $1/2$ in the logarithmic clock comes from the deterministic
 component contraction $a=\lambda^{-2}$, not from fitting the cloud.
 \item The integer counts stable Gaussian row directions, rather than merely
 counting cycle clearances above a manually chosen distance.
 \item Endpoint extraction is structurally necessary: without it the row
 synthesis is not even bounded, whereas after extraction it is compact with
 the exact rank law.
\end{enumerate}

What is not yet rigorous is equally important.  Singular values of a row
dictionary are not eigenvalues of the Markov operator.  The deterministic
Jacobians, transitions between successive contacts, and coupling to the
interior component have not entered $\cR_\sigma$.  Consequently this paper
does not prove
\begin{equation}\label{eq:not-proved}
 N_\sigma^{\mathrm{cloud}}
 =N_\sigma^{\mathrm{row}},
\end{equation}
nor does it prove the roots-of-unity phases of the cloud.

The next operator proof can now be stated more precisely:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{B\arabic*.},leftmargin=3.0em]
 \item Construct a Feshbach/Grushin endpoint block after the RH-14 peripheral
 boundary layers are removed \citep{WangBoundaryLayer2026}.
 \item Compare its linear row-resolution part with
 $\cR_\sigma^{(1)}$ using pre- and
 post-maps whose condition numbers are uniform on the
 $H_\sigma+O(1)$ transition subspace.
 \item Prove that time ordering on those resolved directions is a unilateral
 shift plus an operator-norm remainder small on the $H_\sigma^{-1}$ spectral
 scale.
\end{enumerate}
The first two items would transfer the linear-row rank law to the actual dynamical block;
the third would turn rank into the characteristic polynomial
$\Pi_{N_\sigma}$.  This separates the remaining dynamical problem from the
Gaussian resolution problem solved here.

\section{Numerical audit}
\label{sec:numerics}

The computations use no large transfer matrix.  Boundary points are evaluated
from \eqref{eq:boundary-fixed-point} with 110-decimal arithmetic.  Singular
values come from the exact Gram matrix \eqref{eq:Gram-exact}.  NumPy, SciPy,
mpmath and Matplotlib provide the implementation
\citep{HarrisEtAl2020,VirtanenEtAl2020,Mpmath2026,Hunter2007}.

\subsection{Boundary constant and singular-value staircase}

The scaled clearances stabilize to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:boundary-constant-numerical}
 \delta_k\lambda^{2k}\longrightarrow
 C_{\mathrm b}=0.4608051492217\ldots.
\end{equation}
This is a high-precision evaluation of the analytic constant in
\eqref{eq:boundary-asymptotic}, not an interval enclosure.

\Cref{fig:rank-audit} shows the two mechanisms in the proof.  The boundary
clearances form a geometric ladder.  The exact projected energy is quadratic
for $\delta\ll\sigma$ and saturates for $\delta\gg\sigma$.  The resulting
Gram spectra contain a plateau near one whose length increases by roughly one
whenever the noise is multiplied by $a$.

\subsection{Numerical status}

The boundary fixed points are ordinary high-precision computations and the
Gram eigenvalues are ordinary double-precision computations.  The test suite
checks the first seven clearances against the independent RH-10 archive,
verifies the affinity by direct quadrature, checks the exact small-$t$
coefficient, the $\beta=1$ linear-row kernel, Gram positivity, tail stability,
and the two logarithmic laws.
No finite computation is used to certify a theorem constant or an actual
Markov resonance.

\section{Conclusion}

The half-logarithmic endpoint clock now has an intrinsic operator meaning.
The geometric boundary cycles supply conditioned Gaussian transition rows;
after their common endpoint vector is removed, those rows form a
Hilbert--Schmidt resolution operator.  Its exact Hellinger Gram kernel yields
both
\[
 \|\cR_\sigma\|_{S_2}^2
 =\frac{\log(1/\sigma)}{2\log\lambda}+O(1)
\]
and
\[
 \#\{j:s_j(\cR_\sigma)>\eta\}
 =\frac{\log(1/\sigma)}{2\log\lambda}+O_\eta(1),
 \qquad 0<\eta<1.
\]
The proof explains the factor $1/2$, the bounded staircase defect, and the
necessity of endpoint extraction.  Power-row universality includes the
$L^2$-normalized linear kernel rows, and the result also survives the finite
state interval and folding used by the physical Gaussian operator.

The numerical agreement with the resonance-cloud degree is unusually clean,
but the logical boundary remains firm: row singular values are not Markov
eigenvalues.  The next gate is to factor the actual endpoint transfer block
through this resolution operator and then recover the unilateral shift.  If
that factorization fails, the rank theorem remains valid while the geometric
cloud mechanism must be revised; if it succeeds, the degree of the pole
section will no longer be an empirical input.
