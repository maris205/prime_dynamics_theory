\begin{abstract}
Directional Feshbach closure replaces a global complement-resolvent norm by
the resolvent action on a low-rank primal residual, but a floating
directional solve still leaves an error proportional to one residual and an
unknown inverse norm.  We introduce a goal-oriented primal--dual identity
that squares this residual mechanism.  Let
\(A_z=z\Id-B\), let \(R\) be a primal block right-hand side, and let
\(E\) be the packet observation.  For arbitrary approximations
\(\widehat Y\) and \(\widehat Z\), define
\[
 r=R-A_z\widehat Y,
 \qquad s=E^*-A_z^*\widehat Z.
\]
We prove the exact identity
\[
 -EA_z^{-1}R
 =-E\widehat Y-\widehat Z^*r-s^*A_z^{-1}r.
\]
Thus the computable correction is \(-E\widehat Y-\widehat Z^*r\), while
the only unresolved term contains both the primal and adjoint residuals.  If
\(F_J\) is the base Feshbach matrix and
\(M\geq\norm{A_z^{-1}}_2\), the remaining matrix-Rouch\'e contribution is
bounded by
\[
 M\,\norm{F_J^{-1}s^*}_2\,\norm{r}_2.
\]
This gives an explicit admissible resolvent budget \(M_*(z)\).  We also
prove a sharp no-free-lunch statement: unit primal and dual residuals can
produce an arbitrarily large bilinear remainder even when
\(\norm{A_z}_2\leq1\).  Primal--dual residuals greatly relax, but cannot
logically eliminate, the need for some inverse information unless one
residual vanishes exactly.

The construction is tested on the seven finite noisy transfer matrices and
contours of the quadratic band-merging Feshbach program.  The primal
approximation is the depth increment from \(J\) to \(J+16\); independent
shifted Arnoldi families for \(B^*\), driven by the columns of \(E^*\),
provide dual approximations at depths \(J,J+8,J+16\).  Across
\(10^{-2}\geq\sigma\geq10^{-4}\), dimensions
\(2048\leq n\leq204800\), and packet ranks \(4\leq m\leq9\), every
32-node primal--dual corrected determinant has sampled winding one.  The
maximum computed Rouch\'e ratio is \(4.97\times10^{-11}\).  The minimum
one-sided resolvent budget over all scales is \(4.22\times10^{10}\); after
dual weighting the minimum is \(3.35\times10^{24}\).  The worst
seven-scale budget gain is \(5.48\times10^{13}\).  Deep dual true residuals
are at most \(2.63\times10^{-14}\), and changing the dual depth across the
three prescribed levels changes the minimum budget by at most \(2.83\%\).

These are exact finite-dimensional identities and reproducible
floating-point stress tests.  The large budgets are not themselves
certificates: the residuals are not interval-enclosed, no rigorous upper
bound for \(\norm{A_z^{-1}}\) is supplied, and contour arcs remain
unvalidated.  The result changes the next requirement from a sharp global
resolvent estimate to any sufficiently crude validated bound compatible
with rigorously inflated primal and dual residuals.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Feshbach map; dual-weighted residual; matrix
Rouch\'e theorem; Arnoldi method; a posteriori error; nonnormal resolvent;
Gaussian transfer operator; quadratic map.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

A posteriori error analysis is most effective when it estimates the
quantity of interest rather than the full state error.  In finite-element
and iterative-solver settings this principle leads naturally to adjoint or
dual-weighted residuals \citep{BeckerRannacher2001,GilesSuli2002}.  The same
idea is particularly well matched to a Feshbach reduction: the desired
quantity is a small packet matrix, whereas the discarded complement may
have hundreds of thousands of degrees of freedom and be strongly
nonnormal.

The preceding contour calculation isolated the relevant one-sided
quantity \citep{WangDirectionalRouche2026}.  If \(X_J\) approximately
solves \((z\Id-B)X=C\), then with
\[
 R_J=C-(z\Id-B)X_J,
 \qquad F_J=z\Id-D-EX_J,
\]
the exact Feshbach error is
\begin{equation}
 F-F_J=-E(z\Id-B)^{-1}R_J.
 \label{eq:rh25-directional}
\end{equation}
Consequently, matrix Rouch\'e theory requires only the resolvent action on
the at-most-rank-\(m\) residual block.  Extending the primal Arnoldi spaces
by 16 vectors and directly solving the worst residual direction gave
consistent perturbation ratios below \(5\times10^{-11}\) on seven finite
physical discretizations.  This bypassed the computationally expensive
search for the worst response over the entire complement.

One logical gap remained.  If a computed directional solve
\(\widehat Y\) has residual \(r\), then
\[
 -E(z\Id-B)^{-1}R_J
 =-E\widehat Y-E(z\Id-B)^{-1}r.
\]
Bounding the second term by norms reintroduces
\(\norm{(z\Id-B)^{-1}}\), albeit multiplied by a much smaller residual.
At the finest scales, the selected global smallest-singular-value algorithm
was already unattractive \citep{WangDirectionalRouche2026}.

This paper applies a dual residual before taking that final norm.  An
approximate solution of
\[
 (\overline z\Id-B^*)Z=E^*
\]
weights the primal residual directly.  The remaining error then contains
the product of the primal and dual residuals.  This has four consequences.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{P\arabic*.},leftmargin=2.8em]
 \item \textbf{Exact goal-oriented identity.}  The full packet correction
 decomposes into a computed primal term, a computed dual-weighted term, and
 one bilinear remainder.
 \item \textbf{An explicit resolvent budget.}  The matrix-Rouch\'e margin
 determines the largest external inverse norm compatible with the computed
 residual factors.  The dual residual enlarges this budget by at least 13
 orders of magnitude on the tested contours.
 \item \textbf{A precise no-go boundary.}  Residual norms alone cannot
 uniformly bound the bilinear remainder.  Some inverse information remains
 mathematically necessary unless a primal or dual solve is exact.
 \item \textbf{A seven-scale matrix-free audit.}  Shift-invariant primal and
 adjoint Arnoldi families evaluate all 32 contour nodes while true residuals
 are checked by fresh sparse operator calls.
\end{enumerate}

The evidence hierarchy is essential.  The primal--dual identity, budget
criterion, and no-go theorem are exact finite-dimensional statements.  The
reported budgets use floating matrices and floating residuals.  They
measure how much room a later validated calculation would have; they do not
constitute that validated calculation.  No continuum or small-noise theorem
is asserted.

\section{Packet--complement setting}
\label{sec:setting}

Let \(A\in\C^{n\times n}\) be the Perron/parity-extracted physical
two-step matrix at one noise scale.  Let \(V\in\C^{n\times m}\) and
\(W\in\C^{m\times n}\) be the canonical packet pair with \(WV=\Id_m\),
and put
\[
 P=VW,\qquad Q=\Id-P.
\]
In packet--complement coordinates define
\begin{equation}
 D=WAV,\qquad C=QAV,\qquad E=WAQ,
 \qquad B=QAQ\big|_{\Ran Q}.
 \label{eq:blocks}
\end{equation}
For \(z\notin\spec B\), write
\begin{equation}
 A_z=z\Id_{\Ran Q}-B,
 \qquad
 F(z)=z\Id_m-D-EA_z^{-1}C.
 \label{eq:az-f}
\end{equation}
The oblique Schur factorization and argument principle give
\begin{align}
 \det(z\Id_n-A)&=\det(A_z)\det F(z),
 \label{eq:det-factorization}\\
 \wind_\Gamma\det F&=N_\Gamma(A)-N_\Gamma(B)
 \label{eq:winding-count}
\end{align}
on admissible contours.  The blind contour construction, shifted Arnoldi
realization, and projected zero-minus-pole audit are developed in
\citet{WangContourFeshbach2026}; the growing physical one-vector resolvent
lower bounds appear in \citet{WangPhysicalFeshbach2026}.

At base Arnoldi depth \(J\), let \(X_J(z)\) be the FOM approximation and
\begin{equation}
 R_J=C-A_zX_J,
 \qquad F_J=z\Id_m-D-EX_J.
 \label{eq:base-residual}
\end{equation}
The present goal is to transfer the sampled winding of \(F_J\) toward the
exact \(F\) without first computing a sharp global norm of \(A_z^{-1}\).

\section{Exact primal--dual correction}
\label{sec:identity}

\subsection{Goal-oriented residual identity}

The algebra is stated for a general primal right-hand side
\(R\in\C^{n\times m}\).  In the application, \(R=R_J\).  Let
\(\widehat Y,\widehat Z\in\C^{n\times m}\) be arbitrary approximations
and define their true residual blocks
\begin{equation}
 r=R-A_z\widehat Y,
 \qquad
 s=E^*-A_z^*\widehat Z.
 \label{eq:primal-dual-residuals}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Primal--dual Feshbach identity]
\label{thm:primal-dual}
For every invertible \(A_z\),
\begin{equation}
 -EA_z^{-1}R
 =-E\widehat Y-\widehat Z^*r-s^*A_z^{-1}r.
 \label{eq:primal-dual-identity}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
From \cref{eq:primal-dual-residuals},
\[
 R=A_z\widehat Y+r,
 \qquad
 E=\widehat Z^*A_z+s^*.
\]
Therefore
\[
 EA_z^{-1}R
 =E\widehat Y+EA_z^{-1}r
 =E\widehat Y+\widehat Z^*r+s^*A_z^{-1}r.
\]
Multiplication by \(-1\) proves the claim.
\end{proof}

Define the computable correction and exact remainder by
\begin{align}
 \widehat\Delta(z)&=-E\widehat Y-\widehat Z^*r,
 \label{eq:computed-delta}\\
 \mathcal R_{\rm pd}(z)&=-s^*A_z^{-1}r.
 \label{eq:pd-remainder}
\end{align}
The second term in \cref{eq:computed-delta} is a standard dual-weighted
residual.  It is an \(m\times m\) matrix even though both approximations
live in the ambient space.

\begin{corollary}[One exact side closes the goal error]
\label{cor:exact-side}
If either \(r=0\) or \(s=0\), then
\(\mathcal R_{\rm pd}=0\), so \(\widehat\Delta=-EA_z^{-1}R\) exactly.
In particular, an exact dual solve recovers the packet correction for any
primal approximation.
\end{corollary}

This corollary concerns the small goal matrix, not the full primal state.
It explains why adjoint accuracy can be more valuable than another global
primal norm estimate.

\subsection{A conditional matrix-Rouch\'e certificate}

Precondition the computed and remaining corrections by the base packet
matrix:
\begin{align}
 \widehat\eta(z)&=\norm{F_J(z)^{-1}\widehat\Delta(z)}_2,
 \label{eq:eta-hat}\\
 c_{\rm pd}(z)&=
 \norm{F_J(z)^{-1}s(z)^*}_2\,\norm{r(z)}_2.
 \label{eq:pd-coefficient}
\end{align}

\begin{theorem}[Primal--dual Rouch\'e condition]
\label{thm:pd-rouche}
Suppose \(F_J\) and \(A_z\) are invertible on a closed contour \(\Gamma\).
Let \(M(z)\) satisfy
\(M(z)\geq\norm{A_z^{-1}}_2\).  If
\begin{equation}
 \sup_{z\in\Gamma}
 \left[\widehat\eta(z)+M(z)c_{\rm pd}(z)\right]<1,
 \label{eq:pd-condition}
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
 \wind_\Gamma\det F=\wind_\Gamma\det F_J.
 \label{eq:pd-winding-transfer}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \cref{eq:pd-remainder},
\[
 \norm{F_J^{-1}\mathcal R_{\rm pd}}_2
 \leq
 \norm{F_J^{-1}s^*}_2\norm{A_z^{-1}}_2\norm r_2
 \leq M c_{\rm pd}.
\]
Hence \(\norm{F_J^{-1}(F-F_J)}_2<1\) on \(\Gamma\).  The homotopy
\(F_J+t(F-F_J)\) remains invertible there by the Neumann lemma, so its
determinant winding is constant \citep{GohbergSigal1971}.
\end{proof}

Whenever \(\widehat\eta(z)<1\) and \(c_{\rm pd}(z)>0\), define the
pointwise admissible resolvent budget
\begin{equation}
 M_*^{\rm pd}(z)=
 \frac{1-\widehat\eta(z)}{c_{\rm pd}(z)}.
 \label{eq:pd-budget}
\end{equation}
Any rigorous inverse bound strictly below
\(\inf_{z\in\Gamma}M_*^{\rm pd}(z)\), combined with rigorous residual and
packet enclosures, would satisfy \cref{eq:pd-condition}.

\subsection{One-sided baseline and budget gain}

Without the dual term, the computed correction is \(-E\widehat Y\), the
remainder is \(-EA_z^{-1}r\), and a corresponding coefficient is
\begin{equation}
 c_{\rm p}(z)=\norm{F_J(z)^{-1}E}_2\,\norm{r(z)}_2.
 \label{eq:one-coefficient}
\end{equation}
Writing \(\eta_{\rm p}=\norm{F_J^{-1}E\widehat Y}_2\), its budget is
\begin{equation}
 M_*^{\rm p}(z)=\frac{1-\eta_{\rm p}(z)}{c_{\rm p}(z)}.
 \label{eq:one-budget}
\end{equation}
The gain \(M_*^{\rm pd}/M_*^{\rm p}\) measures how much the adjoint
residual relaxes the still-missing inverse estimate.  It is not an estimate
of the inverse itself.

If validated residual upper bounds inflate \(\norm r\) and \(\norm s\) by
factors \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\), respectively, then the primal--dual
coefficient grows by at most \(\alpha\beta\).  This simple product law makes
the budget a useful stress margin for a later interval implementation.

\section{Why inverse information cannot disappear completely}
\label{sec:nogo}

The dual residual can make \(c_{\rm pd}\) extremely small, but no theorem
can bound \cref{eq:pd-remainder} from the two residual norms alone.

\begin{theorem}[Inverse-information no-go]
\label{thm:no-go}
For every \(L>0\) and every dimension \(n\geq2\), there exist an invertible
matrix \(A\in\C^{n\times n}\) and unit vectors \(r,s\in\C^n\) such that
\begin{equation}
 \norm A_2=1,
 \qquad |s^*A^{-1}r|>L.
 \label{eq:no-go}
\end{equation}
Consequently, no finite upper bound for the bilinear remainder can depend
only on \(\norm A_2\), \(\norm r_2\), and \(\norm s_2\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Take \(r=s=e_1\) and
\[
 A_\varepsilon=\operatorname{diag}(\varepsilon,1,\ldots,1)
\]
with \(0<\varepsilon<L^{-1}\).  Then \(\norm{A_\varepsilon}_2=1\), while
\[
 s^*A_\varepsilon^{-1}r=\varepsilon^{-1}>L.
\]
\end{proof}

The theorem is deliberately elementary and sharp.  Residual squaring is a
conditioning strategy, not magic: it replaces the need for a sharp inverse
bound by the need for any bound below a vastly enlarged budget.  An exact
primal or dual solve is the only residual-only escape, as
\cref{cor:exact-side} shows.

\section{Shift-invariant primal and dual realization}
\label{sec:realization}

\subsection{The primal increment}

The RH-25 base and deep approximations are \(X_J\) and \(X_{J+16}\).  In
\cref{thm:primal-dual}, choose
\begin{equation}
 R=R_J,
 \qquad
 \widehat Y=X_{J+16}-X_J.
 \label{eq:primal-choice}
\end{equation}
Then, in exact arithmetic,
\begin{equation}
 r=R_J-A_z(X_{J+16}-X_J)=R_{J+16}.
 \label{eq:deep-residual}
\end{equation}
Thus the first computed correction is exactly the depth increment
\begin{equation}
 -E\widehat Y=F_{J+16}-F_J.
 \label{eq:depth-correction}
\end{equation}
In the implementation both sides are reconstructed independently; their
maximum matrix 2-norm defect is reported.

\subsection{Adjoint Arnoldi families}

The dual source is the fixed block \(E^*=[e_1,\ldots,e_m]\).  For each
column construct an Arnoldi basis for \(B^*\),
\begin{equation}
 B^*U_{i,K}^{\rm d}
 =U_{i,K}^{\rm d}H_{i,K}^{\rm d}
 +h_{i,K+1,K}^{\rm d}u_{i,K+1}^{\rm d}e_K^*.
 \label{eq:dual-arnoldi}
\end{equation}
For every contour shift, the dual FOM approximation is
\begin{equation}
 \widehat z_{i,K}(z)=
 U_{i,K}^{\rm d}
 (\overline z\Id_K-H_{i,K}^{\rm d})^{-1}
 \norm{e_i}_2 e_1.
 \label{eq:dual-fom}
\end{equation}
Collecting these columns gives \(\widehat Z_K(z)\).  One basis therefore
serves the entire contour; independent pointwise adjoint solves are not
required \citep{Saad1980,Saad2003}.

The dual depths are \(K=J,J+8,J+16\).  Fresh applications of \(B^*\)
evaluate
\[
 s_K=E^*-(\overline z\Id-B^*)\widehat Z_K,
\]
including floating Arnoldi recurrence defects.  The Euclidean adjoint of
the ambient \(QAQ\) realization is implemented explicitly and checked by
an inner-product identity.

\section{Numerical protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

The finite matrices, packet pairs, base depths, and circular contours are
inherited unchanged from RH-24 and RH-25
\citep{WangContourFeshbach2026,WangDirectionalRouche2026}.  The noise
scales are
\[
 10^{-2},\ 4\times10^{-3},\ 2\times10^{-3},\ 10^{-3},\
 5\times10^{-4},\ 2\times10^{-4},\ 10^{-4},
\]
with \(n\sigma=20.48\).  Packet ranks increase from four to nine, base
depths from 36 to 62, and maximum primal and dual depths from 52 to 78.

For each scale the calculation performs the following steps.

\begin{enumerate}
 \item Rebuild the physical sparse matrix, packet pair, and primal Arnoldi
 family through depth \(J+16\).
 \item Form the adjoint action matrix-free, build independent dual Arnoldi
 families from the columns of \(E^*\), and retain their ambient bases.
 \item On 32 equally spaced contour nodes, reconstruct \(X_J\),
 \(X_{J+16}\), and \(\widehat Z_K\) at all three dual depths.
 \item Evaluate true primal and dual residuals by new sparse operator calls,
 then compute \(\widehat\Delta\), \(c_{\rm p}\), \(c_{\rm pd}\), and both
 budgets using small dense packet algebra.
 \item Record determinant phases of \(F_J+\widehat\Delta\), the RH-25
 direct-correction comparison, and the earlier RH-23 physical-eigenmode
 resolvent lower bound.
\end{enumerate}

The RH-23 lower bound is evaluated at a physical eigenmode, not uniformly
on the present contour.  Its comparison with \(M_*^{\rm pd}\) is only a
conditioning scale: a lower bound can never replace the upper bound required
by \cref{thm:pd-rouche}.

All calculations use complex double precision with NumPy and SciPy; figures
use Matplotlib \citep{HarrisEtAl2020,VirtanenEtAl2020,Hunter2007}.  Seven
unit tests cover the exact identity, exact primal and dual edge cases,
budget algebra, the matrix-free residual interface, the norm majorant, and
the no-go construction.

\subsection{Residuals, depth, and winding}

The maximum deep primal true residual relative to \(C\) ranges from
\(4.13\times10^{-15}\) to \(2.91\times10^{-13}\).  The maximum deep dual
true residual relative to \(E^*\) ranges from \(1.82\times10^{-15}\) to
\(2.63\times10^{-14}\).  The primal correction reconstructed as
\(-E(X_{J+16}-X_J)\) agrees with \(F_{J+16}-F_J\) to a maximum absolute
2-norm defect \(3.07\times10^{-15}\).

Increasing the dual depth from \(J\) through \(J+8\) to \(J+16\) changes
the minimum budget by at most \(2.83\%\) at any scale.  The adjoint family
has therefore reached its floating residual floor by the base depth on this
finite range; it is not the observed depth bottleneck.

Every deepest-dual corrected determinant has sampled winding one.  The
largest adjacent phase step is \(2.998<\pi\), and the minimum sampled
singular value of a corrected packet matrix is \(2.14\times10^{-3}\).
As before, finite phase samples are not a rigorous enclosure of the arcs
between nodes.

\subsection{Cost}

At \(n=2048\), the primal and dual Arnoldi builds take 0.78 and 0.85
seconds.  At \(n=102400\), they take 145 and 177 seconds; at \(n=204800\),
336 and 403 seconds.  The adjoint calculation roughly doubles the basis
construction cost, but it is shift-invariant and serves all contour nodes.
The retained basis memory, rather than a global smallest-singular-value
iteration, is the principal computational cost of this audit.

\section{Status of the certificate}
\label{sec:status}

\subsection{What is exact}

The following conclusions are theorem-level finite-dimensional statements.

\begin{itemize}
 \item The decomposition \cref{eq:primal-dual-identity} is exact for any
 primal and dual approximations with explicitly defined residuals.
 \item The sufficient matrix-Rouch\'e condition
 \cref{eq:pd-condition} follows from a norm bound on the single bilinear
 remainder.
 \item The admissible budget \cref{eq:pd-budget} is an exact rearrangement
 of that sufficient condition.
 \item If either residual is zero, the goal correction is exact.
 \item Without inverse information, the bilinear remainder is unbounded in
 general by \cref{thm:no-go}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What is floating-point evidence}

For the seven specified finite sparse matrices, the data support the
following narrower observations.

\begin{itemize}
 \item Primal and dual shifted Arnoldi residuals are simultaneously small on
 the sampled contours.
 \item Their product enlarges the admissible inverse budget by at least
 \(5.48\times10^{13}\) relative to the one-sided deep-residual estimate.
 \item The computed packet correction remains below
 \(4.98\times10^{-11}\), agrees with the independent RH-25 correction at
 the informative scales, and preserves sampled winding one.
 \item The base dual depth is already sufficient at floating precision on
 the tested range.
\end{itemize}

The recorded residuals are ordinary double-precision evaluations.  The
very large budgets partly reflect residuals near the floating recurrence
floor.  They must be recomputed using outward-rounded bounds before entering
a proof.

\subsection{The next rigorous gate}

Primal--dual squaring changes the character of the missing estimate.  A
future validated calculation can proceed as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
 \item Enclose the sparse primal and dual residual blocks, including matrix
 construction, oblique projection, Arnoldi recurrence, and dot-product
 rounding errors.
 \item Enclose the small matrices \(F_J^{-1}\widehat\Delta\) and
 \(F_J^{-1}s^*\) on adaptive contour arcs.
 \item Obtain any rigorous upper bound for \(\norm{A_z^{-1}}\) below the
 residual-inflated minimum budget.  Because the present uninflated minimum
 is \(3.35\times10^{24}\), this bound need not be pseudospectrally sharp.
 \item Apply \cref{thm:pd-rouche}, then separately address finite-section
 and small-noise limits.
\end{enumerate}

Possible tools for the third step include a validated preconditioned
contraction, block interval solves, or a Grushin inverse with explicit
error estimates \citep{SjoestrandZworski2007,TrefethenEmbree2005}.  The
no-go theorem prevents deleting this step altogether; the numerical budgets
make it substantially less demanding.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The directional Feshbach error has a natural primal--dual structure.  A
shifted adjoint solve converts the remaining one-sided error into a
dual-weighted correction plus a bilinear remainder containing both true
residuals.  This identity is exact, matrix-free, and tailored to the small
packet quantity of interest.

On seven finite physical discretizations, residual squaring raises the
minimum admissible complement-resolvent budget from at least
\(4.22\times10^{10}\) to at least \(3.35\times10^{24}\), while preserving
the sampled one-root winding and the previously observed directional
correction.  The improvement is large enough that a future proof need not
begin with a sharp global pseudospectral calculation.

There is also a firm boundary.  Nonzero primal and dual residuals cannot be
controlled with no inverse information at all.  The next gate is therefore
not gone; it has been widened from a tight global-resolvent problem to a
coarse validated inverse bound coupled to rigorous residual and contour
enclosures.
