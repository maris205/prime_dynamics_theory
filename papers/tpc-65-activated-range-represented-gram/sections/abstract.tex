\begin{abstract}
The terminal-activation calculus for a fixed-shift multiscale incidence leaves
one precise question: how much of the activated energy survives the represented
output map?  We answer the finite-dimensional part on the \emph{actual range}
generated by the physical row coefficients.  The optimal represented-to-
terminal constant is the smallest eigenvalue of an activated row Gram; in the
non-descending case it is instead the shorted generalized Gram already required
by the null directions.  We give an explicit residual-fiber formula for every
matrix entry and prove that terminal-faithful pair repair leaves the activated
Gram exactly unchanged whenever the downstream map factors through the literal
terminal carrier.  Orthogonally labelled blocks satisfy a kernel-fusion law:
each block may be singular, while their sum is positive precisely when their
kernels have trivial intersection.  A weighted support-projection bound turns
this into a sharp spectral-capacity certificate.  The main obstruction is also
exact: rowwise activation capacities do not transfer through a coherent
represented map unless a reducing or basis-transport theorem is supplied.
Thus parent inflation is charged once at activation, whereas coherent row
collision is a separate loss.  All results are exact finite-scale statements.
They identify the next arithmetic task---control of the actual residual-fiber
column geometry---but prove neither a polynomial physical lower bound nor a
prime-pair conclusion.
\end{abstract}
