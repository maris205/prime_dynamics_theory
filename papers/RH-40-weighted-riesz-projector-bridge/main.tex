\title{Gauge-Free Weighted Riesz Terms for Nystr\"om Peripheral Extraction\\
\large A Second-Order Continuum Bridge and Exact Stored Haar Ledgers}
\author{Liang Wang}
\date{July 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
A nested-grid spectral-count program subtracts a rank-two Perron/parity term
from folded-Gaussian Markov matrices.  Smooth-kernel Haar theory predicts
second-order consistency and detail blocks and first-order cross blocks, but
the missing projector bridge was previously phrased in terms of individual
left and right eigenvectors.  Those vectors have unavoidable scaling, sign,
phase, and ordering ambiguities.  We replace them by the intrinsic weighted
Riesz term
\[
 \cQ(A;\Gamma)=A\Pi(A;\Gamma)
 =\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_\Gamma z(z-A)^{-1}\,dz.
\]

First, for two bounded operators sharing an isolating contour, we prove the
exact perturbation estimate
\[
 \norm{\cQ(A;\Gamma)-\cQ(B;\Gamma)}
 \le \frac{\len(\Gamma)}{2\pi}
       \max_{z\in\Gamma}|z|\,M_A M_B\norm{A-B},
\]
where $M_A$ and $M_B$ are the contour resolvent suprema.  Second, for a
$C^2$ integral kernel and a simple isolated nonzero eigenvalue, midpoint
Nystr\"om approximation of the eigenvalue and of the right and transposed
left eigenfunctions yields an intrinsic matrix estimate
\[
 \norm{Q_h-Q_h^\circ}_2=O(h^2).
\]
Here $Q_h$ is the finite-dimensional weighted Riesz term and $Q_h^\circ$ is
the midpoint matrix of the smooth continuum kernel
$\lambda r(x)\ell(y)$.  The argument is independent of every eigenvector
gauge.  Strong positivity makes the Perron instance unconditional for the
full folded-Gaussian kernel.  The negative parity instance remains
conditional on the existence of a simple isolated continuum resonance.
The adaptive cutoff bridge from the preceding paper transfers to $Q_h$ under
one additional, explicit uniform Euclidean contour-resolvent bound.

At $\sigma=10^{-2}$ we then audit the exact stored binary64 rank-two factors
at dimensions $2048,4096,8192$.  A 224-bit Arb calculation evaluates the
low-rank Frobenius identities without materializing the dense matrices.  The
second-to-first Haar ratios are rigorously enclosed by
\[
 0.249816736,\quad0.500028226,\quad0.500040220,\quad0.250029234
\]
for the consistency, coarse-to-detail, detail-to-coarse, and detail blocks.
The exact-stored biorthogonality defect is below
$2.885\times10^{-15}$.  Floating eigen-residuals and an observed radial gap
of $0.31188$ support, but do not validate, the parity-isolation hypothesis.
Thus the Perron bridge is closed analytically; continuum parity isolation is
the remaining analytic gate.  No zero-noise, zeta-zero, Hilbert--P\'olya, or
Riemann-hypothesis conclusion is made.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The stored continuation certificates of
\citet{WangNested2026,WangIterated2026} use a folded-Gaussian Markov matrix
$P_h$, remove its Perron and negative parity contributions, and square the
remaining one-step operator.  A subsequent deterministic theorem
\citep{WangHaar2026} explained the observed dyadic law
\begin{equation}
 \norm{E_h}_2,\norm{D_h}_2=O(h^2),\qquad
 \norm{C_h}_2,\norm{B_h}_2=O(h).
 \label{eq:quarter-half-intro}
\end{equation}
One Haar difference extracts one derivative, while two differences extract a
mixed second derivative.  Discrete row normalization, subtraction of a
smooth finite-rank kernel, and exact squaring preserve these powers.

That result isolated two bridges between the smooth continuum theorem and
the sparse stored family.  The hard support window has now been treated
separately \citep{WangCutoff2026}: a fixed eight-sigma window is negligible
at the archived dimensions but has a nonzero full-kernel continuum defect,
whereas a slowly growing support gives an $O(h^2)$ Euclidean perturbation.
The remaining bridge concerns the peripheral term.

A direct comparison of eigenvectors is the wrong invariant.  If $r$ and
$\ell$ are a biorthogonal right-left pair, then
\[
 r\longmapsto c r,\qquad \ell\longmapsto c^{-1}\ell
\]
changes both vectors but leaves the spectral contribution unchanged.  Signs
and complex phases are special cases, and several modes introduce ordering
and basis ambiguities.  Numerical continuation can impose conventions, but
an analytic theorem should not depend on them.

The matrix actually subtracted in the stored construction is
\begin{equation}
 Q_h=R_h\Lambda_hL_h^T,
 \qquad L_h^TR_h=I,
 \label{eq:stored-weighted-term}
\end{equation}
not the unweighted projector $R_hL_h^T$.  Intrinsically, it is the part of
$P_h$ selected by holomorphic functional calculus \citep{Kato1995}:
\begin{equation}
 \cQ(P_h;\Gamma)=P_h\Pi(P_h;\Gamma).
 \label{eq:intro-riesz}
\end{equation}
This observation turns the missing bridge into a resolvent and Nystr\"om
question.

The contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
 \item a gauge-free definition of the weighted peripheral term for a simple
       eigenvalue or a finite cluster;
 \item an explicit resolvent-Lipschitz estimate and a Neumann-series contour
       persistence criterion;
 \item a second-order Euclidean matrix theorem for smooth midpoint
       Nystr\"om weighted terms, proved by treating the transposed kernel on
       the same footing as the original kernel;
 \item an unconditional application to the full-kernel Perron branch and a
       sharply stated conditional application to the negative parity branch;
 \item transfer of an $O(h^2)$ Markov cutoff defect to the weighted Riesz
       term under uniform contour conditioning; and
 \item an exact-stored Arb ledger for the rank-two factors and all four
       dyadic Haar blocks at the three archived dimensions.
\end{enumerate}

Three evidence levels are kept distinct.
\begin{description}[leftmargin=3.0cm,style=nextline]
 \item[Analytic theorem]
 Functional-calculus perturbation bounds and smooth Nystr\"om convergence.
 \item[Exact-stored ledger]
 Arb enclosures after every archived binary64 factor is treated as an exact
 real input.
 \item[Floating diagnostic]
 Sparse eigen-residuals and a leading-spectrum isolation audit; these are not
 interval enclosures of the eigensolver or of the full matrix.
\end{description}

The result closes an analytic mechanism, not an arithmetic identification.
The resonances studied here are eigenvalues of a non-self-adjoint noisy
transfer operator at fixed positive noise.

\section{Weighted Riesz terms}
\label{sec:riesz}

Let $A$ be a bounded operator on a complex Banach space, and let $\Gamma$ be
a positively oriented rectifiable Jordan contour contained in the resolvent
set of $A$.  Assume that its interior contains a finite isolated spectral
cluster.

\begin{definition}[Riesz projector and weighted term]
\label{def:weighted-riesz}
Define
\begin{align}
 \Pi(A;\Gamma)
 &=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_\Gamma(z-A)^{-1}\,dz,
 \label{eq:riesz-projector}\\
 \cQ(A;\Gamma)
 &=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_\Gamma z(z-A)^{-1}\,dz.
 \label{eq:weighted-riesz}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Intrinsic spectral contribution]
\label{prop:intrinsic}
The weighted term satisfies
\[
 \cQ(A;\Gamma)=A\Pi(A;\Gamma)=\Pi(A;\Gamma)A.
\]
If the contour encloses one simple eigenvalue $\lambda$, with
$Ar=\lambda r$, $\ell A=\lambda\ell$, and $\ell(r)=1$, then
\begin{equation}
 \cQ(A;\Gamma)=\lambda\,r\otimes\ell.
 \label{eq:simple-term}
\end{equation}
This expression is invariant under $r\mapsto cr$ and
$\ell\mapsto c^{-1}\ell$ for every $c\ne0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The identity $z(z-A)^{-1}=I+A(z-A)^{-1}$ and the vanishing contour integral
of the constant operator give the first formula.  The standard simple Riesz
projector is $r\otimes\ell$, which proves the second.  The two gauge factors
cancel exactly.
\end{proof}

For a semisimple cluster, one may write $\cQ=R\Lambda L^T$ in any
biorthogonal basis.  A change of basis alters the factors but not $\cQ$.
The contour formula also remains valid for nonsemisimple clusters, where an
eigenvector-only description would need Jordan data.

\begin{theorem}[Resolvent-Lipschitz bound]
\label{thm:resolvent-lipschitz}
Let $A$ and $B$ be bounded operators on the same Banach space, and suppose
$\Gamma\subset\rho(A)\cap\rho(B)$.  Put
\[
 M_A=\max_{z\in\Gamma}\norm{(z-A)^{-1}},\qquad
 M_B=\max_{z\in\Gamma}\norm{(z-B)^{-1}},
\]
and $R_\Gamma=\max_{z\in\Gamma}|z|$.  Then
\begin{equation}
 \boxed{
 \norm{\cQ(A;\Gamma)-\cQ(B;\Gamma)}
 \le
 \frac{\len(\Gamma)}{2\pi}R_\Gamma M_AM_B\norm{A-B}.}
 \label{eq:resolvent-lipschitz}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The first resolvent identity gives
\[
 (z-A)^{-1}-(z-B)^{-1}
 =(z-A)^{-1}(A-B)(z-B)^{-1}.
\]
Insert this into \eqref{eq:weighted-riesz}, take norms along the contour, and
bound the Bochner integral by its arclength integral.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Contour persistence from one resolvent bound]
\label{cor:contour-persistence}
Let
$\delta=\norm{A-B}$ and assume $M_A\delta<1$.  Then
$\Gamma\subset\rho(B)$,
\begin{equation}
 M_B\le\frac{M_A}{1-M_A\delta},
 \label{eq:perturbed-resolvent}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\cQ(A;\Gamma)-\cQ(B;\Gamma)}
 \le
 \frac{\len(\Gamma)}{2\pi}R_\Gamma
 \frac{M_A^2\delta}{1-M_A\delta}.
 \label{eq:one-sided-transfer}
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Factor
\[
 z-B=\{I-(B-A)(z-A)^{-1}\}(z-A).
\]
The first factor is invertible by a uniformly convergent Neumann series.
This proves \eqref{eq:perturbed-resolvent}; then apply
\cref{thm:resolvent-lipschitz}.
\end{proof}

The factor $M_AM_B$ is essential in a nonnormal problem.  A geometric
distance from $\Gamma$ to the spectrum does not, by itself, replace it.
This is exactly where a visually stable eigenvalue gap stops being a
validated projector theorem.

\section{A second-order Nystr\"om bridge}
\label{sec:nystrom}

Let $I=[0,1]$ and $k\in C^2(I\times I)$.  Define
\begin{equation}
 (\cK f)(x)=\int_0^1k(x,y)f(y)\,dy.
 \label{eq:continuum-operator}
\end{equation}
For $h=1/n$, use midpoint nodes
$x_i=(i+\tfrac12)h$, $0\le i<n$, and the matrix
\begin{equation}
 (K_h)_{ij}=h k(x_i,x_j).
 \label{eq:nystrom-matrix}
\end{equation}
Here $T$ denotes algebraic transpose: $l_h^T$ is the row eigenfunctional.
For the archived real branches this agrees with the usual real left-vector
notation; no Hermitian gauge convention is being imposed.

Suppose $\lambda\ne0$ is a simple isolated eigenvalue of $\cK$.  Its right
eigenfunction and left eigenfunctional have $C^2$ representatives $r$ and
$\ell$ because
\[
 r=\lambda^{-1}\cK r,
 \qquad
 \ell=\lambda^{-1}\cK^T\ell,
\]
where
$(\cK^Tg)(y)=\int_0^1k(x,y)g(x)\,dx$.
Choose the bilinear normalization
\begin{equation}
 \int_0^1\ell(x)r(x)\,dx=1.
 \label{eq:continuum-normalization}
\end{equation}
The continuum weighted Riesz kernel is
\begin{equation}
 q(x,y)=\lambda r(x)\ell(y),
 \label{eq:continuum-weighted-kernel}
\end{equation}
and its midpoint matrix is
\begin{equation}
 (Q_h^\circ)_{ij}=h q(x_i,x_j).
 \label{eq:continuum-sampled-term}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Gauge-free second-order simple branch]
\label{thm:nystrom-weighted}
For all sufficiently small $h$, $K_h$ has a simple eigenvalue $\lambda_h$
inside a fixed isolating contour for $\lambda$.  Let $r_h,l_h\in\C^n$ be
any corresponding right and left vectors normalized by $l_h^Tr_h=1$, and
put
\begin{equation}
 Q_h=\lambda_h r_hl_h^T=\cQ(K_h;\Gamma).
 \label{eq:discrete-weighted-term}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
 \norm{Q_h-Q_h^\circ}_2=O(h^2).
 \label{eq:nystrom-weighted-rate}
\end{equation}
The estimate is unchanged by every admissible rescaling of $r_h,l_h$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Collectively compact Nystr\"om theory gives spectral exactness for the
nonzero isolated eigenvalue.  Composite midpoint quadrature is second order
on the smooth eigenfunctions.  Applied to $k$ and to the transposed kernel
$k^T(x,y)=k(y,x)$, the standard simple-branch estimates
\citep{Atkinson1967,Anselone1971,Atkinson1997,Chatelin1983} give compatible
node vectors $\rho_h,\eta_h$ such that
\begin{align}
 |\lambda_h-\lambda|&=O(h^2),\label{eq:eigenvalue-rate}\\
 \max_i|\rho_{h,i}-r(x_i)|
 +\max_i|\eta_{h,i}-\ell(x_i)|&=O(h^2),
 \label{eq:mode-rate}
\end{align}
with $K_h\rho_h=\lambda_h\rho_h$ and
$K_h^T\eta_h=\lambda_h\eta_h$.

Set
\[
 a_h=h\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\eta_{h,i}\rho_{h,i}.
\]
By \eqref{eq:continuum-normalization}, \eqref{eq:mode-rate}, and the midpoint
rule, $a_h=1+O(h^2)$.  Hence we may choose
\[
 r_h=\rho_h,\qquad l_h=\frac{h}{a_h}\eta_h,
\]
which gives $l_h^Tr_h=1$.  Uniform boundedness of $r,\ell$ and their
approximants now implies, entrywise,
\[
 \left|
 \lambda_h(r_h)_i(l_h)_j
 -h\lambda r(x_i)\ell(x_j)
 \right|=O(h^3).
\]
For an $n\times n$ matrix with maximum entry $O(h^3)$, both the $1$- and
$\infty$-norms are $O(nh^3)=O(h^2)$; their geometric mean bounds the
spectral norm.  This proves \eqref{eq:nystrom-weighted-rate}.  Finally,
$Q_h$ itself is invariant under the right-left gauge by
\cref{prop:intrinsic}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Finite simple cluster]
\label{cor:simple-cluster}
If a fixed finite cluster consists of simple isolated nonzero eigenvalues
with disjoint isolating contours, the sum of their weighted terms satisfies
the same $O(h^2)$ matrix estimate.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply \cref{thm:nystrom-weighted} to each branch and add finitely many
estimates.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why the result is stronger than vector tracking]
The proof chooses one convenient normalization only to obtain an entrywise
bound.  The conclusion concerns $Q_h$, which is independent of that choice.
Thus no sign-alignment, phase-alignment, or mode-ordering algorithm appears
in the theorem statement.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Discretely normalized smooth kernels}

The archived full Markov matrix uses a discrete row normalizer.  Let a
positive $C^2$ raw kernel $g$ have continuum normalizer
\[
 Z(x)=\int_0^1g(x,y)\,dy>0
\]
and normalized kernel $k(x,y)=g(x,y)/Z(x)$.  Its full midpoint Markov matrix
is
\begin{equation}
 (P_h)_{ij}=\frac{g(x_i,x_j)}{\sum_m g(x_i,x_m)}.
 \label{eq:discrete-markov}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, $P_h$ is the Nystr\"om matrix of the $h$-dependent smooth
kernel $g(x,y)/Z_h(x)$, where
$Z_h(x)=h\sum_mg(x,x_m)$.  Midpoint quadrature gives
$Z_h-Z=O(h^2)$ uniformly.

\begin{proposition}[Discrete row normalization]
\label{prop:discrete-normalization}
If $\lambda\ne0$ is a simple isolated eigenvalue of the continuum-normalized
operator with kernel $k=g/Z$, then its weighted term for the matrices $P_h$
in \eqref{eq:discrete-markov} is $O(h^2)$ close in spectral norm to the
midpoint matrix of the continuum weighted kernel.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Positivity and compactness separate $Z$ uniformly from zero.  Composite
midpoint quadrature and $g\in C^2$ give
\[
 \norm{Z_h-Z}_\infty=O(h^2),\qquad
 \norm{g/Z_h-g/Z}_\infty=O(h^2).
\]
The $h$-dependent kernels $g/Z_h$ and their transposes remain uniformly
smooth and collectively compact.  The simple right and transposed-left
Nystr\"om branches therefore satisfy the estimates
\eqref{eq:eigenvalue-rate}--\eqref{eq:mode-rate}; repeating the normalization
and entrywise argument in \cref{thm:nystrom-weighted} proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Full folded-Gaussian Perron bridge]
\label{cor:perron}
Fix $\sigma>0$ and a smooth map $f$.  On $I=[0,1]$, let
\begin{equation}
 g_\sigma(x,y)=
 \exp\!\left[-\frac{(y-f(x))^2}{2\sigma^2}\right]
 +
 \exp\!\left[-\frac{(y+f(x))^2}{2\sigma^2}\right].
 \label{eq:folded-kernel}
\end{equation}
The continuum-normalized operator is compact, Markov, and strongly positive.
Its eigenvalue $1$ is algebraically simple and isolated.  Consequently the
weighted Perron term of the full discretely normalized midpoint matrices
satisfies \eqref{eq:nystrom-weighted-rate} without an additional spectral
hypothesis.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The normalized kernel is strictly positive and smooth on the compact square.
Strong positivity and Krein--Rutman theory make $1$ algebraically simple and
exclude every other peripheral spectral value
\citep{Schaefer1974,WangContinuum2026}.  Apply
\cref{prop:discrete-normalization}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Conditional parity bridge]
\label{cor:parity}
If the full folded-Gaussian continuum operator has a simple isolated nonzero
negative resonance $\lambda_-$ continuing the archived parity branch, then
its weighted Nystr\"om term is $O(h^2)$ close in spectral norm to the
midpoint matrix of $\lambda_-r_-(x)\ell_-(y)$.  The combined Perron/parity
rank-two term has the same rate.
\end{corollary}

The hypothesis in \cref{cor:parity} is not proved by three convergent-looking
matrix eigenvalues.  Establishing it requires either a validated continuum
contour resolvent or an equivalent analytic isolation argument.

\section{Transfer through a Gaussian support cutoff}
\label{sec:cutoff}

Let $P_h$ denote the full discretely normalized folded-Gaussian matrix and
$\widehat P_h$ a support-truncated and renormalized version of the same
dimension.  Suppose
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\widehat P_h-P_h}_2\le\varepsilon_h.
 \label{eq:markov-bridge}
\end{equation}
For a fixed cluster contour $\Gamma$, put
\[
 M_h=\max_{z\in\Gamma}\norm{(z-P_h)^{-1}}_2.
\]

\begin{proposition}[Markov-to-Riesz transfer]
\label{prop:cutoff-transfer}
If $\sup_hM_h\le M<\infty$ and $M\varepsilon_h<1$ for all sufficiently
small $h$, then
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\cQ(\widehat P_h;\Gamma)-\cQ(P_h;\Gamma)}_2
 \le
 \frac{\len(\Gamma)}{2\pi}R_\Gamma
 \frac{M^2\varepsilon_h}{1-M\varepsilon_h}.
 \label{eq:cutoff-riesz-bound}
\end{equation}
In particular, an $O(h^2)$ Markov bridge gives an $O(h^2)$ weighted-Riesz
bridge.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Apply \cref{cor:contour-persistence} at each dimension.
\end{proof}

The adaptive support schedule of \citet{WangCutoff2026},
\begin{equation}
 L(h)=\max\{5,2\sqrt{\log(1/h)}\},
 \label{eq:adaptive-cutoff}
\end{equation}
gives
\[
 \varepsilon_h=O\!\left(h^2(\log(1/h))^{-1/4}\right).
\]
Thus it transfers through \cref{prop:cutoff-transfer} under the displayed
uniform resolvent condition.  A fixed eight-sigma window is extraordinarily
small at the archived grids, but it does not converge to the full kernel in
row-operator norm when held fixed forever.  The finite-grid observation and
the all-grid theorem are therefore different statements.

For the Perron branch, \cref{cor:perron} closes the full smooth continuum
bridge unconditionally.  For the parity branch, two logically separate
conditions remain: simple continuum isolation for \cref{cor:parity}, and
uniform Euclidean contour conditioning for transfer through the nonsmooth
cutoff.  The latter may follow from a sufficiently strong validated version
of the former, but it is not inferred from eigenvalue locations alone.

\section{Exact-stored rank-two Haar ledger}
\label{sec:ledger}

We now study the binary64 factors archived in the two dyadic continuation
papers.  This section does not assert that they are exact eigenvectors of an
exact-real sparse matrix.  Instead, every stored binary64 number is treated
as an exact real input and all subsequent low-rank identities are enclosed by
Arb interval arithmetic \citep{Johansson2017,Rump2010}.

At dimension $n$, the stored term is
\begin{equation}
 Q_n=R_n\Lambda_nL_n^T,
 \qquad R_n,L_n\in\mathbb R^{n\times2}.
 \label{eq:stored-Q}
\end{equation}
For a fine level $2n$, define paired right averages and differences
\[
 \overline R_i=\frac{(R_{2n})_{2i}+(R_{2n})_{2i+1}}2,
 \qquad
 \delta R_i=\frac{(R_{2n})_{2i}-(R_{2n})_{2i+1}}2,
\]
and paired left sums and differences
\[
 \Sigma L_i=(L_{2n})_{2i}+(L_{2n})_{2i+1},
 \qquad
 \Delta L_i=(L_{2n})_{2i}-(L_{2n})_{2i+1}.
\]
The exact unnormalized Haar blocks are then
\begin{align}
 E_n&=\overline R\Lambda_{2n}(\Sigma L)^T
       -R_n\Lambda_nL_n^T,
 \label{eq:E-factor}\\
 C_n&=\delta R\Lambda_{2n}(\Sigma L)^T,
 \label{eq:C-factor}\\
 B_n&=\overline R\Lambda_{2n}(\Delta L)^T,
 \label{eq:B-factor}\\
 D_n&=\delta R\Lambda_{2n}(\Delta L)^T.
 \label{eq:D-factor}
\end{align}
Thus $E_n$ has rank at most four and the other blocks rank at most two.
No dense $n\times n$ block is needed.

For real low-rank factors $A,B$,
\begin{equation}
 \norm{AB^T}_F^2
 =\operatorname{tr}\!\left((A^TA)(B^TB)\right).
 \label{eq:low-rank-frobenius}
\end{equation}
The implementation forms both small Gram matrices using exact binary64
inputs inside 224-bit Arb balls.  This matters most for $E_n$, whose two
rank-two contributions nearly cancel; a binary64 evaluation of the small
Gram identity loses visible digits even though no large dense matrix is
formed.

\section{What is closed and what remains}
\label{sec:boundary}

The projector bridge can now be stated without conflating evidence levels.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
 \item \textbf{Weighted Riesz stability is analytic.}
       \Cref{thm:resolvent-lipschitz} is an exact operator theorem and
       includes the nonnormal conditioning explicitly.
 \item \textbf{The full smooth Nystr\"om bridge is analytic.}
       A simple isolated nonzero branch gives
       $\norm{Q_h-Q_h^\circ}_2=O(h^2)$ without choosing a persistent
       eigenvector gauge.
 \item \textbf{The Perron instance is unconditional.}
       Strong positivity supplies simplicity and isolation for the full
       folded-Gaussian continuum operator.
 \item \textbf{The parity instance is conditional.}
       The same conclusion follows if the archived negative branch is shown
       to continue a simple isolated continuum resonance.  The current
       eigenvalue table does not prove that premise.
 \item \textbf{The cutoff transfer is conditional on contour conditioning.}
       The adaptive $O(h^2)$ matrix bridge propagates to $Q_h$ once a uniform
       Euclidean contour-resolvent bound is supplied.
 \item \textbf{The stored Haar ledger is rigorous for its stated inputs.}
       The Arb intervals prove exact algebraic properties of the stored
       binary64 factors.  Their status as spectral factors of the stored
       sparse matrices is still supported only by floating residuals.
\end{enumerate}

Accordingly, the single remaining \emph{analytic} gate in the rank-two
continuum bridge is parity simplicity and isolation, preferably in a form
that also controls the contour resolvent needed by
\cref{prop:cutoff-transfer}.  A fully validated finite-grid statement would
add two computational gates: interval enclosure of the sparse eigensolver
and outward-rounded construction of the binary64 Gaussian matrices.

Only two dyadic transitions are archived.  They strongly identify the
quarter--half mechanism but cannot prove a uniform all-level constant by
themselves.  The present paper also does not address the later hierarchical
Schur-resolvent recursion, a zero-noise limit, a self-adjoint generator, or
any arithmetic spectral identification.

\section{Conclusion}

The appropriate continuum object for peripheral subtraction is not a chosen
pair of eigenvectors but the weighted Riesz term $A\Pi$.  This replacement
removes every gauge convention and exposes the only perturbative quantity
that a nonnormal theorem genuinely needs: the contour resolvent.

For a smooth simple Nystr\"om branch, the weighted term is second-order close
in Euclidean matrix norm to the midpoint sample of its continuum kernel.
Strong positivity closes this statement for the Perron branch of the full
folded-Gaussian operator.  The same theorem is ready for the archived
negative branch as soon as its continuum simplicity and isolation are
established.  Under uniform contour conditioning, the adaptive Gaussian
cutoff passes through the weighted Riesz calculus at the same order.

The exact-stored rank-two factors independently obey the predicted dyadic
quarter--half law, with Arb-enclosed ratios within $2\times10^{-4}$ of their
ideal values and biorthogonality defects below $3\times10^{-15}$.  The maze
has therefore narrowed again: vector normalization is no longer an
obstruction, and the next mathematically meaningful target is a validated
continuum contour for the negative resonance.
