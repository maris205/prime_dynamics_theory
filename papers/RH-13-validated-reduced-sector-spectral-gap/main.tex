\begin{abstract}
At the algebraic band-merging parameter of $f_u(x)=1-u x^2$, let
$\uc^3-2\uc^2+2\uc-2=0$, put $r=\uc-1$, and set
$\lambda=2\uc r=1.678573510428\ldots$.  A preceding analytic-circle-lift
construction proved the exact component-zeta factorization
\[
 \cZ(z)=\frac{D_{2,-}(z)}{D_{1,+}(z)}
 \frac{1-z/\lambda}{1-z/\lambda^2}.
\]
After removing the Perron factor $1-z$ from $D_{1,+}$, the only unresolved
point was the reduced-sector disk bound
\[
 r_1<\lambda^{-2},\qquad r_2<\lambda^{-2},
\]
where $r_1$ is the non-Perron spectral radius in the even $\beta=1$ sector and
$r_2$ is the odd $\beta=2$ spectral radius.  That bound would prevent
cancellation of both explicit postcritical factors.  We prove it by a
computer-assisted argument with complete analytic tail estimates.

The two inverse branches have common square
\[
 t(x)=\frac{1-\sqrt{(1-x)/\uc}}{\uc}.
\]
Deck parity turns the circle operators into coefficient-decimating
Wiener--Taylor operators on the disk $|x|<R$, $R=0.7$:
\begin{align*}
 (\cT_1v)(x)&=2a(x)\sum_{k\ge0}c_{2k}\frac{t(x)^k}{R^{2k}},\\
 (\cT_2v)(x)&=b(x)\sum_{k\ge0}c_{2k+1}\frac{t(x)^k}{R^{2k+1}},
 \qquad v(x)=\sum_{j\ge0}c_j(x/R)^j,
\end{align*}
with explicit analytic weights $a,b$.  The exact circle-mean functional
removes the Perron mode from $\cT_1$.

Using dimension $N=50$, 100 Taylor coefficients, a Cauchy radius $0.9$, and
100-decimal Arb ball arithmetic, we certify
\[
 \|\cT_{1,0}^3\|\le0.03582019310742
 <\lambda^{-6}=0.0447051759618781\ldots,
\]
\[
 \|\cT_{2}^2\|\le0.06463843745472
 <\lambda^{-4}=0.125961707473977\ldots.
\]
Hence
\[
 r_1\le0.3296420762932<\lambda^{-2},
 \qquad
 r_2\le0.2542409043697<\lambda^{-2}.
\]
The inequalities remain certified for dimensions $30,40,50,60,70$.

It follows unconditionally that the centered weighted zeta function has an
uncanceled simple zero at $z=\lambda$, an uncanceled simple pole at
$z=\lambda^2$, and a zero-free residual factor on $|z|<3.03359$.  Moreover
\[
 q_n=1-\lambda^{-n}+\lambda^{-2n}+O(3^{-n}).
\]
Floating-point eigenvalues near $0.20788030$ and $0.15252824$ are reported
only as diagnostics; the theorem uses the larger validated norm bounds.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} computer-assisted proof; dynamical zeta function;
transfer operator; Wiener algebra; Taylor approximation; interval arithmetic;
postcritical orbit; quadratic map.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

An explicit factor in a meromorphic identity is not automatically an actual
zero or pole.  A zero of the remaining denominator can cancel a proposed
numerator, and a zero of the remaining numerator can cancel a proposed pole.
For a dynamical zeta function this distinction is spectral: cancellation is
equivalent to a transfer-operator eigenvalue at one prescribed reciprocal
location.

The preceding postcritical-factorization paper studied the central component
of the square of the quadratic map $f_u(x)=1-u x^2$ at an algebraic
band-merging parameter \citep{WangPostcritical2026}.  A two-sheeted cosine
cover removed the critical point and produced an analytic expanding circle
map.  Ordinary and deck-twisted flat traces gave the exact identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:prior-trace}
 q_n=E_{1,n}-O_{2,n}-\lambda^{-n}+\lambda^{-2n},
\end{equation}
and hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:prior-factorization}
 \cZ(z)=\frac{D_{2,-}(z)}{D_{1,+}(z)}
 \frac{1-z/\lambda}{1-z/\lambda^2}.
\end{equation}
The odd second-order determinant was proved nonzero at $z=\lambda$ by a
strict pressure estimate.  Thus the claim that the centered zeta has a simple
zero at $\lambda$ was reduced to excluding the single non-Perron eigenvalue
$\lambda^{-1}$ from the even first-order operator.

Numerically, the situation looked much stronger.  The leading reduced
resonances stabilized near
\begin{equation}\label{eq:numerical-resonances-intro}
 0.20788030,
 \qquad
 0.15252824,
\end{equation}
both far below
\begin{equation}\label{eq:target-intro}
 \lambda^{-2}=0.3549108444\ldots.
\end{equation}
The open conjecture was therefore the disk bound
\begin{equation}\label{eq:conjecture-intro}
 r_1<\lambda^{-2},
 \qquad
 r_2<\lambda^{-2}.
\end{equation}
Unlike exclusion at one point, this stronger statement simultaneously
controls the numerator at $\lambda$, the denominator at $\lambda^2$, and the
remainder in the periodic coefficient expansion.

The aim here is to prove \eqref{eq:conjecture-intro}.  The argument is
computer-assisted, but its infinite-dimensional part is not delegated to a
black-box eigensolver.  We derive exact Taylor formulas for both transfer
sectors, choose a concrete Wiener algebra, remove the Perron mode by an exact
linear functional, and bound the omitted rows and columns by geometric and
Cauchy estimates.  Arb ball arithmetic is used only for the finite matrices,
the algebraic parameter, and directed evaluation of explicit inequalities
\citep{Johansson2017}.

\subsection*{Main results and logical status}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{R\arabic*.},leftmargin=3.2em]
 \item The even $\beta=1$ and odd $\beta=2$ circle operators are conjugated
 to two explicit coefficient-decimating operators on a Wiener--Taylor space.

 \item The normalized circle mean is an exact left eigenfunctional of the
 first operator.  Eliminating the constant Taylor coefficient gives a
 concrete realization $\cT_{1,0}$ of the entire non-Perron sector.

 \item A finite-rank projection at dimension $50$ has rigorously bounded
 operator errors
 \[
  \varepsilon_1\le5.237746723\times10^{-4},
  \qquad
  \varepsilon_2\le2.407483932\times10^{-4}.
 \]

 \item Arb interval matrix products and analytic tails prove
 \[
  \|\cT_{1,0}^3\|<\lambda^{-6},
  \qquad
  \|\cT_2^2\|<\lambda^{-4}.
 \]
 This is the computer-assisted theorem.  The stored certificate contains
 every interval, software version, parameter, and source hash.

 \item The reduced-sector conjecture is therefore unconditional.  The
 residual Fredholm quotient is holomorphic and nonzero on a certified disk
 of radius $3.03359>\lambda^2$.

 \item Consequently $z=\lambda$ is an actual simple zero,
 $z=\lambda^2$ is an actual simple pole, and
 \[
  q_n=1-\lambda^{-n}+\lambda^{-2n}+O(3^{-n}).
 \]

 \item The floating-point resonance estimates in
 \eqref{eq:numerical-resonances-intro} are not used in any certified
 inequality.  They only cross-check the operator representation and explain
 why the validated bounds have a comfortable margin.
\end{enumerate}

\section{The lifted dynamics and the remaining spectral problem}
\label{sec:background}

Let $\uc$ be the real root in $(1,2)$ of
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cubic}
 u^3-2u^2+2u-2=0,
\end{equation}
and set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:constants}
 r=\uc-1,
 \qquad
 \lambda=2\uc r.
\end{equation}
Numerically,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:constants-numerical}
 \uc=1.54368901269207636157\ldots,
 \qquad
 \lambda=1.67857351042832226510\ldots.
\end{equation}
Let
\begin{equation}\label{eq:S}
 S=f_{\uc}^2|_{[-r,r]},
 \qquad
 S(x)=-r+2\uc^2x^2-\uc^3x^4.
\end{equation}
The two half intervals are full branches onto $[-r,r]$.

The cosine cover
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cover}
 \pi(\theta)=-r\cos\theta,
 \qquad
 \pi:\T\to[-r,r]
\end{equation}
lifts $S$ to an analytic, orientation-preserving degree-two expanding circle
map $F$ satisfying
\begin{equation}\label{eq:semiconjugacy}
 \pi\circ F=S\circ\pi,
 \qquad
 F(\theta+\pi)=F(\theta),
 \qquad
 \min F'=\lambda.
\end{equation}
The equality in the middle is on the circle; a real lift gains $2\pi$.

For $\beta=1,2$, let
\begin{equation}\label{eq:circle-transfer}
 (\cL_\beta\varphi)(\theta)
 =\sum_{F(\eta)=\theta}\frac{\varphi(\eta)}{F'(\eta)^\beta}.
\end{equation}
The deck involution $\theta\mapsto-\theta$ commutes with $F$ and splits each
operator into even and odd sectors.  Denote their Fredholm determinants by
$D_{\beta,+}$ and $D_{\beta,-}$.  Standard analytic expanding-map theory
identifies their zeros with reciprocals of the corresponding Ruelle
resonances \citep{Ruelle1976,Baladi2000,Baladi2018}.

The $\beta=1$ even sector has a simple Perron eigenvalue one.  Write
\begin{equation}\label{eq:perron-removal}
 D_{1,+}(z)=(1-z)\widetilde D_{1,+}(z).
\end{equation}
Let $r_1$ be the spectral radius after this Perron mode is removed, and let
$r_2$ be the spectral radius of $\cL_{2,-}$.  The factorization
\eqref{eq:prior-factorization} can then be written
\begin{equation}\label{eq:centered-factorization}
 H(z):=(1-z)\cZ(z)
 =\frac{1-z/\lambda}{1-z/\lambda^2}G(z),
 \qquad
 G(z)=\frac{D_{2,-}(z)}{\widetilde D_{1,+}(z)}.
\end{equation}
Proving \eqref{eq:conjecture-intro} makes both determinants in $G$ zero-free
on a disk strictly larger than $|z|\le\lambda^2$.

\section{Exact Wiener--Taylor sector representations}
\label{sec:taylor-representation}

The full-branch symmetry makes the transfer operators much simpler than the
piecewise arccosine formula for $F$ suggests.  Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:s-t}
 s(x)=\sqrt{\frac{1-x}{\uc}},
 \qquad
 t(x)=\frac{1-s(x)}{\uc}.
\end{equation}
For real $x\in[-r,r]$, the two inverse branches of $S$ are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inverse-branches}
 g_\pm(x)=\pm\sqrt{t(x)}.
\end{equation}
The function $t=g_+^2=g_-^2$ is analytic on $|x|<1$ even though the
individual square roots branch at the endpoint preimage.

Put
\begin{align}
 a(x)
 &=\frac{\sqrt{(1+s(x))(r+s(x))}}{4s(x)},
 \label{eq:a-weight}\\
 b(x)
 &=\frac{a(x)}{2\uc^2s(x)}.
 \label{eq:b-weight}
\end{align}
For $|x|<1$, the quantity $1-x$ lies in the right half-plane and the chosen
square root has positive real part.  Hence $s$, $a$, and $b$ are analytic on
that disk; in particular none of the displayed denominators vanishes there.
The derivative formula for the circle lift gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a-derivative}
 a(x)=\frac1{F'(\eta)}
 =\frac{\sqrt{\uc(2-\uc t(x))(1-t(x))}}
 {4(1-\uc t(x))}
\end{equation}
at either lift preimage $\eta$ over $g_+(x)$ or $g_-(x)$.

\subsection{Parity conjugacies}

Every analytic even deck function can be written
\begin{equation}\label{eq:even-coordinate}
 \varphi_+(\theta)=v(-r\cos\theta),
\end{equation}
while every analytic odd deck function can be written
\begin{equation}\label{eq:odd-coordinate}
 \varphi_-(\theta)=\sin\theta\,v(-r\cos\theta).
\end{equation}
The division by $\sin\theta$ in the odd case is analytic at $0$ and $\pi$.
Indeed, deck oddness gives locally
$\varphi_-(\theta)=\theta h(\theta^2)$ at $0$ and the analogous expansion in
$\theta-\pi$ at $\pi$.  Dividing by the corresponding expansion of
$\sin\theta$ leaves an even analytic germ, hence an analytic germ in
$x=-r\cos\theta$ at each branch value.

\begin{proposition}[Exact sector operators]
\label{prop:sector-operators}
Under \eqref{eq:even-coordinate}, the even $\beta=1$ operator is conjugate to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:T1-branches}
 (\cT_1v)(x)=a(x)\bigl[v(\sqrt{t(x)})+v(-\sqrt{t(x)})\bigr].
\end{equation}
Under \eqref{eq:odd-coordinate}, the odd $\beta=2$ operator is conjugate to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:T2-branches}
 (\cT_2v)(x)=
 \frac{v(\sqrt{t(x)})-v(-\sqrt{t(x)})}
 {S'(\sqrt{t(x)})F'(\eta)}.
\end{equation}
At $t(x)=0$, the quotient is understood by its removable analytic
continuation.
If $v(x)=\sum_{j\ge0}v_jx^j$, these formulas become
\begin{align}
 (\cT_1v)(x)
 &=2a(x)\sum_{k\ge0}v_{2k}t(x)^k,
 \label{eq:T1-series}\\
 (\cT_2v)(x)
 &=b(x)\sum_{k\ge0}v_{2k+1}t(x)^k.
 \label{eq:T2-series}
\end{align}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a target lift $\theta$, the two $F$-preimages project to
$\pm\sqrt{t(x)}$.  Their circle derivatives are equal because
$F'(\eta)^2$ depends only on the square of the interval coordinate.  Thus
\eqref{eq:T1-branches} follows immediately from \eqref{eq:circle-transfer}.

For the odd sector, differentiating the semiconjugacy gives the signed
identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sine-derivative}
 F'(\eta)=S'(y)\frac{\sin\eta}{\sin\theta},
 \qquad y=\pi(\eta).
\end{equation}
After dividing the output by $\sin\theta$, each $\beta=2$ term becomes
$v(y)/[S'(y)F'(\eta)]$.  Since $S'$ is odd and $F'$ is equal on the two
preimages, their sum is \eqref{eq:T2-branches}.  Finally
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Sprime-g}
 S'(\sqrt t)=4\uc^2\sqrt t(1-\uc t)
\end{equation}
and the odd Taylor difference contributes a factor $2\sqrt t$.  This gives
the weight \eqref{eq:b-weight} and proves
\eqref{eq:T1-series} and \eqref{eq:T2-series}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Wiener algebra and Perron removal}

Fix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:R-choice}
 R=\frac7{10}>r
\end{equation}
and let $\cA_R$ be the Wiener algebra
\begin{equation}\label{eq:wiener-space}
 \cA_R=\left\{
 v(x)=\sum_{j\ge0}c_j(x/R)^j:
 \|v\|_R:=\sum_{j\ge0}|c_j|<\infty
 \right\}.
\end{equation}
In these scaled coefficients,
\begin{align}
 (\cT_1v)(x)
 &=2a(x)\sum_{k\ge0}c_{2k}\frac{t(x)^k}{R^{2k}},
 \label{eq:T1-scaled}\\
 (\cT_2v)(x)
 &=b(x)\sum_{k\ge0}c_{2k+1}\frac{t(x)^k}{R^{2k+1}}.
 \label{eq:T2-scaled}
\end{align}

The Taylor coefficients of $t$ are nonnegative.  Indeed,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:t-positive}
 t(x)=\frac1\uc-\uc^{-3/2}\sqrt{1-x},
\end{equation}
its constant coefficient is positive, and every nonconstant coefficient of
$-\sqrt{1-x}$ is positive.  Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:t-norm}
 \|t\|_R=t(R)=0.362223396291970\ldots,
 \qquad
 \tau:=\frac{\|t\|_R}{R^2}
 =0.739231421004020\ldots<1.
\end{equation}
It follows directly from \eqref{eq:T1-scaled} and \eqref{eq:T2-scaled} that the operators
are nuclear on $\cA_R$, not merely compact.  In fact their nonzero column
norms are bounded respectively by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:full-column-decay}
 2\|a\|_R\tau^k,
 \qquad
 \frac{\|b\|_R}{R}\tau^k,
 \qquad k\ge0,
\end{equation}
and these bounds are summable because $\tau<1$.

The normalized circle mean of the even lift is the explicit functional
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mean-functional}
 \ell(v)=\frac1{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}v(-r\cos\theta)\,d\theta
 =\sum_{k\ge0}m_{2k}c_{2k},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:moments}
 m_{2k}=\binom{2k}{k}\left(\frac{r}{2R}\right)^{2k},
 \qquad m_{2k+1}=0.
\end{equation}
Since $\cL_1$ preserves Lebesgue integral,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mean-invariance}
 \ell(\cT_1v)=\ell(v).
\end{equation}
Every eigenfunction with eigenvalue different from one therefore belongs to
$\ker\ell$.

Parameterize $\ker\ell$ by $d=(c_1,c_2,\ldots)\in\ell^1$ and eliminate
\begin{equation}\label{eq:c0-elimination}
 c_0=-\sum_{j\ge1}m_jd_j.
\end{equation}
The resulting reduced operator $\cT_{1,0}$ has columns
\begin{equation}\label{eq:T10-columns}
 C^{(1)}_{2k}(x)
 =2a(x)\left[\frac{t(x)^k}{R^{2k}}-m_{2k}\right],
 \qquad k\ge1,
\end{equation}
and $C^{(1)}_{2k+1}=0$.  Output coordinates are the nonconstant scaled Taylor
coefficients.  The odd second-order operator has columns
\begin{equation}\label{eq:T2-columns}
 C^{(2)}_{2k+1}(x)=b(x)\frac{t(x)^k}{R^{2k+1}},
 \qquad
 C^{(2)}_{2k}=0.
\end{equation}
The reduced first-sector columns are summable as well, since
$m_{2k}\le(r/R)^{2k}$ and $r/R<1$.

\begin{corollary}[Spectral identification]
\label{cor:spectral-identification}
The nonzero spectrum of $\cT_{1,0}$ is the non-Perron even $\beta=1$
spectrum, and the nonzero spectrum of $\cT_2$ is the odd $\beta=2$ spectrum.
Moreover
\begin{equation}\label{eq:det-identification}
 \det(I-z\cT_{1,0})=\widetilde D_{1,+}(z),
 \qquad
 \det(I-z\cT_2)=D_{2,-}(z).
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Because $R>r$, the maps
\[
 J_+v(\theta)=v(-r\cos\theta),
 \qquad
 J_-v(\theta)=\sin\theta\,v(-r\cos\theta)
\]
send $\cA_R$ continuously and injectively into functions holomorphic on a
complex strip about the circle.  The endpoint argument preceding
\cref{prop:sector-operators} shows that these are precisely the two local
parity coordinates, and that proposition gives the intertwining identities
$J_+\cT_1=\cL_{1,+}J_+$ and
$J_-\cT_2=\cL_{2,-}J_-$.

The summable geometric column estimates \eqref{eq:full-column-decay} make both
Wiener operators nuclear of order zero.  The analytic transfer-operator trace
theorem applied to these admissible holomorphic realizations identifies every
nuclear trace with the same local inverse-branch fixed-point sum as in the
circle realization.  Equivalently, this is the usual independence of the
analytic Ruelle determinant from the admissible holomorphic realization
\citep{Ruelle1976,Baladi2000,Baladi2018}.  Thus the two realizations have the
same Fredholm determinants and the same nonzero spectra, including algebraic
multiplicity.

For the first sector, define the bounded isomorphism
\[
 I_0:\ell^1(\{1,2,\ldots\})\longrightarrow\ker\ell,
 \qquad
 I_0d=\left(-\sum_{j\ge1}m_jd_j,d_1,d_2,\ldots\right).
\]
Its inverse drops the constant coordinate.  Mean invariance implies
$\cT_1I_0=I_0\cT_{1,0}$.  The Perron eigenvalue is simple, so this similarity
removes exactly its one-dimensional factor and gives
\eqref{eq:det-identification}.
\end{proof}

\section{Finite matrices and analytic tail bounds}
\label{sec:certificate-method}

Let $P_N$ retain the first $N$ reduced coordinates.  For $\cT_{1,0}$ these
are $d_1,\ldots,d_N$; for $\cT_2$ these are $c_0,\ldots,c_{N-1}$.  Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:finite-matrices}
 M_{1,N}=P_N\cT_{1,0}P_N,
 \qquad
 M_{2,N}=P_N\cT_2P_N,
\end{equation}
extended by zero on the omitted coordinates.

The proof needs a rigorous operator-norm estimate for
$\cT_{j}-M_{j,N}$.  Choose the larger Cauchy radius
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cauchy-radius}
 \rho=\frac9{10},
 \qquad
 q=\frac R\rho=\frac79.
\end{equation}
If $f$ is analytic on $|x|\le\rho$ with $|f|\le M_\rho$, Cauchy's estimate
gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cauchy-tail}
 \sum_{j\ge K}|f_j|R^j
 \le M_\rho\frac{q^K}{1-q}.
\end{equation}

At $\rho$, the same positive-coefficient argument gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigma}
 t(\rho)=0.482921793913694\ldots,
 \qquad
 \sigma:=\frac{t(\rho)}{R^2}
 =0.985554681456517\ldots<1.
\end{equation}
For $|x|\le\rho$, put
\begin{equation}\label{eq:s-bounds}
 s_{\min}=\sqrt{\frac{1-\rho}{\uc}},
 \qquad
 s_{\max}=\sqrt{\frac{1+\rho}{\uc}}.
\end{equation}
Equations \eqref{eq:a-weight} and \eqref{eq:b-weight} imply the explicit sup bounds
\begin{align}
 A_\rho
 &=\frac{\sqrt{\uc(1+s_{\max})(1+t(\rho))}}
 {4s_{\min}}
 \ge\sup_{|x|\le\rho}|a(x)|,
 \label{eq:A-rho}\\
 B_\rho
 &=\frac{A_\rho}{2\uc^2s_{\min}}
 \ge\sup_{|x|\le\rho}|b(x)|.
 \label{eq:B-rho}
\end{align}
The certified values are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cauchy-values}
 A_\rho\le2.15844363868010,
 \qquad
 B_\rho\le1.77938939382276.
\end{equation}

The first $K=100$ Taylor coefficients, enclosed by Arb, together with
\eqref{eq:cauchy-tail}, give
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weight-norms}
 \|a\|_R\le0.675431518461580,
 \qquad
 \|b\|_R\le0.321477794530791.
\end{equation}
The omitted portions of these two weight norms are below
$1.19\times10^{-10}$ and $9.76\times10^{-11}$, respectively.

\begin{lemma}[Finite-rank operator errors]
\label{lem:finite-rank-errors}
For even $N$, define
\begin{align}
 \varepsilon_{1,N}
 =\max\Bigg\{&
 2A_\rho(\sigma+m_2)\frac{q^{N+1}}{1-q},\nonumber\\[-0.2em]
 &2\|a\|_R\left(\tau^{N/2+1}+m_{N+2}\right)
 \Bigg\},
 \label{eq:epsilon-one}\\
 \varepsilon_{2,N}
 =\max\Bigg\{&
 \frac{B_\rho}{R}\frac{q^N}{1-q},
 \frac{\|b\|_R}{R}\tau^{N/2}
 \Bigg\}.
 \label{eq:epsilon-two}
\end{align}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:operator-errors}
 \|\cT_{1,0}-M_{1,N}\|_1\le\varepsilon_{1,N},
 \qquad
 \|\cT_2-M_{2,N}\|_1\le\varepsilon_{2,N}.
\end{equation}
For $N=50$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:error-values}
 \varepsilon_{1,50}\le0.000523774672228,
 \qquad
 \varepsilon_{2,50}\le0.000240748393117.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For a retained column, the finite matrix omits only Taylor rows above the
projection.  In the first sector, every nonzero retained column has index
$2k$, $k\ge1$.  Its supremum on the Cauchy disk is bounded by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:low-column-one}
 2A_\rho(\sigma^k+m_{2k})
 \le2A_\rho(\sigma+m_2),
\end{equation}
because $\sigma<1$ and $m_{2k}$ decreases.  Apply
\eqref{eq:cauchy-tail} starting at row $N+1$.  In the second sector, the
largest retained-column Cauchy bound occurs at $k=0$ and is $B_\rho/R$;
the omitted rows start at $N$.

For an omitted input column, use the Wiener algebra inequality.  The first
nonzero omitted first-sector column is $N+2$, hence $k=N/2+1$, and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:high-column-one}
 \|C^{(1)}_{2k}\|_1
 \le2\|a\|_R(\tau^k+m_{2k}).
\end{equation}
Both terms decrease with $k$.  The first nonzero omitted second-sector column
is $N+1$, giving
\begin{equation}\label{eq:high-column-two}
 \|C^{(2)}_{2k+1}\|_1
 \le\frac{\|b\|_R}{R}\tau^k,
 \qquad k=N/2.
\end{equation}
Taking the larger of the retained-row tail and omitted-column bound proves
the formulas.  Arb evaluates every endpoint outward.
\end{proof}

\section{The validated spectral-gap theorem}
\label{sec:main-theorem}

All numbers in this section are interval enclosures, although only outward
decimal endpoints are displayed.  The cubic root is isolated directly from
the integer polynomial \eqref{eq:cubic}.  Taylor coefficients, matrix
products, absolute column sums, and the scalar inequalities in
\cref{lem:finite-rank-errors} are evaluated at 100 decimal places with
python-flint/Arb \citep{Johansson2017,PythonFlint2026}.

Write $\cT=M+E$ with $\|E\|\le\varepsilon$.  For the first sector, the exact
noncommutative expansion gives
\begin{align}
 \|\cT^3\|
 \le{}&\|M^3\|
 +\varepsilon\left(2\|M^2\|+\|M\|^2\right)\nonumber\\
 &+3\|M\|\varepsilon^2+\varepsilon^3.
 \label{eq:cube-perturbation}
\end{align}
For the second sector,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:square-perturbation}
 \|\cT^2\|
 \le\|M^2\|+2\|M\|\varepsilon+\varepsilon^2.
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Validated reduced-sector disk bound]
\label{thm:validated-gap}
For the postcritical circle lift at \eqref{eq:cubic},
\begin{align}
 \|\cT_{1,0}^3\|_1
 &\le0.035820193107412
 <\lambda^{-6}=0.0447051759618781\ldots,
 \label{eq:certified-one}\\
 \|\cT_2^2\|_1
 &\le0.064638437454714
 <\lambda^{-4}=0.125961707473977\ldots.
 \label{eq:certified-two}
\end{align}
Consequently
\begin{equation}\label{eq:radius-bounds}
 \boxed{
 r_1\le0.329642076293171<\lambda^{-2},
 \qquad
 r_2\le0.254240904369682<\lambda^{-2}.}
\end{equation}
In fact both radii are strictly smaller than $1/3$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Insert \cref{tab:certificate} and \eqref{eq:error-values} into
\eqref{eq:cube-perturbation} and \eqref{eq:square-perturbation}.  Arb returns the upper
endpoints in \eqref{eq:certified-one} and \eqref{eq:certified-two}; the lower threshold
endpoints are computed from the isolated algebraic interval for $\lambda$.
The spectral-radius formula gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:power-radius}
 r(\cT_{1,0})\le\|\cT_{1,0}^3\|^{1/3},
 \qquad
 r(\cT_2)\le\|\cT_2^2\|^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
Taking outward roots yields \eqref{eq:radius-bounds}.  Direct interval
comparison also gives $0.329642076293171<1/3$.
\end{proof}

This proves the reduced-sector disk-bound conjecture posed in the preceding
paper.  The argument uses power norms, not approximate eigenvalues.  In
particular, no spectral diagonalization or assumption of normality enters the
proof.

\section{Consequences for the weighted zeta function}
\label{sec:consequences}

Let
\begin{equation}\label{eq:certified-disk-radius}
 R_{\mathrm{cert}}
 =\frac1{0.329642076293171}
 =3.0335933180\ldots.
\end{equation}
Since
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lambda-square}
 \lambda^2=2.8176090299\ldots<R_{\mathrm{cert}},
\end{equation}
\cref{thm:validated-gap,cor:spectral-identification} show that both
$\widetilde D_{1,+}$ and $D_{2,-}$ are zero-free throughout
$|z|<R_{\mathrm{cert}}$.

\begin{corollary}[Uncanceled postcritical zero and pole]
\label{cor:zero-pole}
The residual quotient
\begin{equation}\label{eq:G-again}
 G(z)=\frac{D_{2,-}(z)}{\widetilde D_{1,+}(z)}
\end{equation}
is holomorphic and nonzero for $|z|<R_{\mathrm{cert}}$.  Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H-final}
 H(z)=\frac{1-z/\lambda}{1-z/\lambda^2}G(z)
\end{equation}
has an actual simple zero at $z=\lambda$ and an actual simple pole at
$z=\lambda^2$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Fredholm zeros are reciprocals of nonzero eigenvalues.  The certified spectral
radii therefore make both determinants zero-free on the stated disk.
Equation \eqref{eq:H-final} is the exact factorization
\eqref{eq:centered-factorization}; nonvanishing of $G$ prevents either
explicit factor from canceling.
\end{proof}

The periodic coefficient remainder is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:e-n}
 e_n=q_n-1+\lambda^{-n}-\lambda^{-2n}
 =\operatorname{tr}(\cT_{1,0}^n)-\operatorname{tr}(\cT_2^n).
\end{equation}
Both operators are nuclear.  Since their spectral radii are strictly below
$1/3$, the logarithmic derivative of their residual determinant quotient is
analytic on a disk of radius strictly greater than $3$.

\begin{corollary}[Sharp postcritical coefficient law]
\label{cor:sharp-law}
The component Perron periodic weight satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sharp-law}
 \boxed{
 q_n=1-\lambda^{-n}+\lambda^{-2n}+O(3^{-n}).}
\end{equation}
For the standard weighted trace $Q_m$ of the original quadratic map,
\begin{align}
 Q_{2n}
 &=2-2\lambda^{-n}+\lambda^{-2n}+O(3^{-n}),
 \label{eq:Q-even-sharp}\\
 Q_{2n+1}
 &=\lambda^{-(2n+1)}.
 \label{eq:Q-odd-exact}
\end{align}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By the Fredholm trace expansion,
\[
 z\frac{G'(z)}{G(z)}=\sum_{n\ge1}e_nz^n.
\]
Choose any $\rho_0$ with $3<\rho_0<R_{\mathrm{cert}}$.  The left-hand side is
holomorphic on $|z|\le\rho_0$, so Cauchy's coefficient estimate gives
$e_n=O(\rho_0^{-n})=O(3^{-n})$.  Substitution into \eqref{eq:e-n} proves the
first formula.  The exact component-pairing identity from the flat-trace
completion theorem is
$Q_{2n}=2q_n-\lambda^{-2n}$, while odd iterates fix only the shared endpoint
\citep{WangFlatTrace2026}.  Substitution gives the result.
\end{proof}

\section{Conclusion}

The deck symmetry of the analytic circle lift does more than split a
determinant.  It decimates Taylor coefficients: the even first-order sector
uses only even input coefficients, while the odd second-order sector uses
only odd ones.  After the exact Perron mean is removed, both operators become
nuclear on a concrete Wiener algebra.  This structure permits a
small finite matrix and an elementary analytic tail bound to control the full
infinite-dimensional spectrum.

The resulting validated inequalities resolve the sole noncancellation
condition left by the postcritical factorization.  The algebraic factors at
$\lambda$ and $\lambda^2$ are now actual, respectively a simple zero and a
simple pole, and the observed three-term periodic law is a theorem with the
explicit remainder $O(3^{-n})$.  No unproved spectral assumption remains in
these conclusions.
