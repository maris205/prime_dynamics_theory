\begin{abstract}
Let $S$ be a nonzero finite matrix and
\[
  \mathcal N(S)=\frac{S^*S}{\|S\|_F^2}.
\]
This trace-one positive matrix is the normalized snapshot consumed by the
moving-memory construction of RH-130--RH-139.  We prove a sharp perturbation
theorem for this nonlinear normalization.  If $S,H\ne0$ and
\[
 \delta=\min\!\left\{1,\frac{\|S-H\|_F}
 {\max(\|S\|_F,\|H\|_F)}\right\},
\]
then
\[
 \|\mathcal N(S)-\mathcal N(H)\|\leq\delta,\qquad
 \|\mathcal N(S)-\mathcal N(H)\|_F\leq\sqrt2\,\delta,
 \qquad
 \|\mathcal N(S)-\mathcal N(H)\|_1\leq2\delta.
\]
All three constants are simultaneously attained by one-row matrices.  If
$H$ is an exact orthogonal singular-value truncation and
$q=\|S-H\|_F/\|S\|_F$, the same bounds improve quadratically to
$q^2,\sqrt2q^2,2q^2$, with equality in trace norm.  The degeneration as
$\delta\uparrow1$ is unavoidable because normalization is discontinuous at
the zero matrix.

We then transport the 160-bit Arb residuals of RH-77 through the universal
theorem at all five archived scales and both source channels.  Every
rank-four postblock snapshot lies in a certified operator ball of radius at
most $2.824\times10^{-4}$; eight of ten radii are below $1.34\times10^{-6}$.
The direct floating proxies exhibit the expected quadratic cancellation, but
we do not treat that cancellation as interval-validated.  Thus snapshot
normalization is no longer an uncontrolled finite interface.  Spectral
projector stability, packet construction, threshold updates, and every
all-level statement remain open.
\end{abstract}

\section{The interface problem}
The source model produces rectangular postblock states, whereas the later
directional machinery consumes trace-normalized right Gramians.  A small
absolute state error alone does not control this map near zero: both numerator
and denominator change, and the denominator can vanish.  RH-77 supplied Arb
balls for the state residual after rank compression, but those balls had not
been converted into snapshot balls.  Consequently RH-138 validated a frozen
matrix assembly rather than the intended source model.

The correct geometry is projective Hilbert--Schmidt geometry.  Regard a
matrix as a vector in the tensor product of its row and column spaces.  The
rank-one density matrix of the normalized vector has $\mathcal N(S)$ as a
partial trace.  Contractivity of trace distance under partial trace then
gives constants independent of either matrix dimension
\citep{NielsenChuang2010}.  This dimension-free feature is essential at the
five scale-dependent discretizations.

\section{Sharp normalized-snapshot enclosure}
Write $\|\cdot\|$, $\|\cdot\|_F$, and $\|\cdot\|_1$ for operator,
Hilbert--Schmidt, and trace norm.  The following theorem is valid over real or
complex matrices.

\begin{theorem}[Sharp projective snapshot bound]\label{thm:main}
For nonzero matrices $S,H$ of the same shape, set
\[
 d=\|S-H\|_F,
 \qquad
 \delta=\min\left\{1,\frac d{\max(\|S\|_F,\|H\|_F)}\right\}.
\]
Then
\begin{align}
 \|\mathcal N(S)-\mathcal N(H)\|&\leq\delta,\label{eq:op}\\
 \|\mathcal N(S)-\mathcal N(H)\|_F&\leq\sqrt2\,\delta,\label{eq:fro}\\
 \|\mathcal N(S)-\mathcal N(H)\|_1&\leq2\delta.\label{eq:trace}
\end{align}
For every $0\leq\delta<1$, one pair of one-row matrices attains equality in
all three inequalities.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $u=S/\|S\|_F$ and $v=H/\|H\|_F$, interpreted as unit vectors in
Hilbert--Schmidt space.  If $\theta$ is their projective angle, projection
orthogonal to either endpoint gives
\[
 \|S\|_F\sin\theta\leq d,
 \qquad
 \|H\|_F\sin\theta\leq d.
\]
Hence $\sin\theta\leq\delta$.  The trace distance between the pure states
$uu^*$ and $vv^*$ equals $2\sin\theta$.  Since partial trace is positive and
trace preserving on Hermitian matrices, it contracts trace norm, while
partial tracing the row factor gives $\mathcal N(S)$ and $\mathcal N(H)$.
This proves \eqref{eq:trace}.

The difference is Hermitian and traceless.  Its positive and negative parts
therefore each have trace at most $\delta$.  Thus its operator norm is at
most $\delta$, and the sum of the squared eigenvalues is at most
$\delta^2+\delta^2$, proving \eqref{eq:op}--\eqref{eq:fro}.

For sharpness take
\[
 H=(1,0),\qquad
 S=(1-\delta^2,\delta\sqrt{1-\delta^2}).
\]
Then $\|H\|_F=1$, $\|S-H\|_F=\delta$, and the normalized rows meet at angle
$\arcsin\delta$.  Their snapshot difference has eigenvalues $\pm\delta$.
\end{proof}

An immediate consequence is the outward Loewner enclosure
\[
 \mathcal N(H)-\delta I\preceq\mathcal N(S)
 \preceq\mathcal N(H)+\delta I.
\]
No factor depending on row count, column count, or rank occurs.

\section{Quadratic cancellation for singular truncation}
The general theorem cannot exploit the special geometry of a singular-value
truncation.  That geometry gives one extra order.

\begin{proposition}[Orthogonal split]\label{prop:svd}
Suppose $S=H+R$, $H^*R=0$, $HR^*=0$, and
$q=\|R\|_F/\|S\|_F$.  Then
\[
 \|\mathcal N(S)-\mathcal N(H)\|\leq q^2,
 \quad
 \|\mathcal N(S)-\mathcal N(H)\|_F\leq\sqrt2q^2,
 \quad
 \|\mathcal N(S)-\mathcal N(H)\|_1=2q^2.
\]
In particular this applies to an exact truncated SVD.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Put $A=H^*H/\|H\|_F^2$ and, when $R\ne0$,
$B=R^*R/\|R\|_F^2$.  The cross terms vanish, $A$ and $B$ have orthogonal
supports, and
\[
 \mathcal N(S)=(1-q^2)A+q^2B,
 \qquad \mathcal N(H)=A.
\]
Thus the difference is $q^2(B-A)$.  Its positive and negative parts have
trace $q^2$, proving the trace identity and the other two bounds.
\end{proof}

The distinction between Theorem~\ref{thm:main} and
Proposition~\ref{prop:svd} matters computationally.  A numerically returned
SVD is not automatically an exact orthogonal split of the Arb state.  The
linear theorem is therefore the certified statement used below; the
quadratic formula is recorded as a diagnostic and as a target for a later
interval projector calculation.  This avoids silently turning backward
error into a spectral theorem \citep{Wedin1972,Bhatia1997}.

\section{The zero-state obstruction}
The strict nonzero condition cannot be removed.  As $\delta\uparrow1$ in the
sharp witness, $S\to0$ while its normalized row tends to a direction
orthogonal to $H$.  At relative radius one, arbitrarily small matrices can
produce snapshot operator distance arbitrarily close to one.  For any radius
larger than one, a ball around $H$ contains small matrices in every
orthogonal direction.  Hence no reference-sensitive modulus below the full
state-space diameter survives across zero.

This is also the correct practical gate.  A state enclosure must certify a
positive norm before normalization.  RH-77 does so at the five anchors; an
all-level theorem would have to do so uniformly.

\section{Consequence and claim boundary}
The finite source interface can now be written without an implicit
normalization assumption:
\[
 S\in B_F(H,\varepsilon)
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 \mathcal N(S)\in
 [\mathcal N(H)-\delta I,\mathcal N(H)+\delta I].
\]
This supplies the first component of the source-to-packet enclosure $P_E$
identified by RH-139.  It does not supply the whole packet.  Eigenprojectors
can be unstable when a selected cluster has no gap; polar frames can be
unstable at rank loss; threshold decisions can change when a singular value
meets the cutoff.  The next layer must compare the RH-140 radii with the
actual packet spectral gaps and state the corresponding obstruction sharply.

No all-level source norm lower, uniform rank-four compression theorem,
controlled-tail tube, normalized-base liminf, Stage A theorem,
Hilbert--Polya operator, zeta-zero identification, or Riemann-hypothesis
statement is proved here.  The result is a rigorous finite bridge from
validated source-state residuals to normalized snapshot norm balls.
