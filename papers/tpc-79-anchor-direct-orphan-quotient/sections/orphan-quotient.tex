\section{The free orphan quotient floor}
\label{sec:orphan}

Before charging for anchor coefficients, declare all directions in
\(V_A\) free.

\begin{definition}[Orphan quotient gauge]\label{def:orphan-gauge}
For \(\pi k\in Q_{\rm orph}\), define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:orph}
 \delta_{\rm orph}(\pi k)
 =
 \inf_{v\in V_A}\delta(k-v).
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The value is independent of the representative \(k\) and is the
quotient norm induced by \(\delta\).

\begin{theorem}[Exact orphan dual]\label{thm:orphan-dual}
For every \(k\in K\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:orphan-dual}
 \boxed{\displaystyle
 \delta_{\rm orph}(\pi k)
 =
 \max\left\{
 [\lambda,k]_{\R}:
 \lambda\in\cB_D,\ S_A^*\lambda=0
 \right\}.}
\end{equation}
Both extrema are attained.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The annihilator of \(V_A=\Ran S_A\) is
\[
 V_A^\perp=\{\lambda:S_A^*\lambda=0\}.
\]
The dual unit ball of a quotient norm is the intersection of the
original dual unit ball with the annihilator of the quotient
subspace.  Applying this to \(\delta\) proves
\eqref{eq:orphan-dual}.  Direct finite-dimensional minimax duality gives
the same result: the free variable \(v\in V_A\) forces its dual
coefficient to vanish on \(V_A\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Exact orphan zero test]\label{cor:orphan-zero}
\[
 \delta_{\rm orph}(\pi k)=0
 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
 k\in V_A.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
It is a norm on \(K/V_A\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[A floor, not an attainable hybrid price]
The minimization in \eqref{eq:orph} charges nothing for \(v\).  It is a
lower floor on any model that assigns a nonnegative cost to anchor
synthesis.  It is not the actual joint cost unless the required anchor
directions are free in that model.
\end{remark}
