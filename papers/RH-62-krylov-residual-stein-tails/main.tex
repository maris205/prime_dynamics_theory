\title{Krylov Residual Certificates for Directional Stein Tails\\
\large A First Arnoldi Replacement for the Norm Envelope}
\author{Liang Wang}
\date{July 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
RH-61 showed that the finite phase-aware tail of a physical packet can be
orders of magnitude smaller than the geometric envelope obtained from the
largest normalized operator norm.  This paper introduces the first
directional replacement: an Arnoldi residual certificate for the power
$S^Lz$ entering a Stein tail.  If
\[
 AV=VH+ge_m^*,
 \qquad z=V\beta,
\]
then we prove the exact identity
\[
 A^Lz=VH^L\beta+
 \sum_{j=0}^{L-1}A^{L-1-j}g\,e_m^*H^j\beta.
\]
Propagating the single Arnoldi defect with an ordinary norm gives a
rigorous upper depending on the actual Krylov coefficients.  It is exact at
Arnoldi breakdown and extends columnwise to Hilbert--Schmidt sources.

Three deterministic models test the mechanism.  In a slow/fast surrogate
calibrated to the RH-61 endpoint, the ordinary norm envelope is about
$2000$ times the exact power at $L=32$, while a one-vector Krylov
certificate is essentially exact.  In a nonnormal four-step chain, the
one-vector gain improves from about $220$ to $84$, and full Krylov
termination is exact.  The RH-60 two-block model exposes the remaining
problem: a crude one-vector residual propagation can be worse than the
ordinary envelope.  Krylov structure is therefore a viable route, but the
residual propagator must itself become directional or weighted.  No
production physical-family theorem, Stage A1 closure, self-adjoint operator,
arithmetic trace formula, or Hilbert--Polya conclusion is claimed.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Arnoldi method; Krylov residual; Stein tail;
directional Hardy energy; nonnormal matrix; small noise.

\section{Introduction}

The current small-noise spectral route requires directional
Hilbert--Schmidt Hardy bounds rather than a global contraction estimate
\citep{WangHardy2026}.  RH-60 retained a finite phase Gram and completed its
tail with a positive Stein metric.  RH-61 then showed that the metric's
ordinary norm envelope can be extremely pessimistic even when the actual
directional tail is tiny.

The natural next object is the Krylov range generated by the actual tail
source.  Arnoldi methods provide a finite-dimensional relation between the
full operator and its projected Hessenberg matrix
\citep{Saad2003,GolubVanLoan2013}.  The relation is especially useful here
because the source is fixed and the question is a power applied to that
source, not a worst-case resolvent norm.

This paper is deliberately a first layer.  We prove the algebraic residual
identity, formulate a computable upper, and test it on small models.  The
propagation of the Arnoldi defect is still bounded by an ordinary operator
norm.  A failure of that final step is recorded as a route boundary rather
than hidden by increasing the Krylov dimension.

\subsection*{Contributions and boundary}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
 \item We prove the Arnoldi power identity in a form directly usable inside
       a Stein tail.
 \item We derive a positive directional power and Stein-tail certificate.
 \item We prove exact termination at Krylov breakdown and a columnwise
       Hilbert--Schmidt extension.
 \item We audit slow/fast, two-block, and nonnormal-chain models and isolate
       the residual-propagation bottleneck.
\end{enumerate}

All production-looking numbers in this paper are finite-dimensional
binary64 model computations.  The RH-61-calibrated model matches a stored
contraction gap only; it is not the folded-Gaussian physical operator.

\section{Arnoldi power identity}
\label{sec:identity}

Let $A\in\mathbb C^{n\times n}$ and let $z\ne0$.  Run modified
Gram--Schmidt Arnoldi from $v_1=z/\norm z$ for $k$ steps.  Write
$V=[v_1,\ldots,v_k]$, let $H=V^*AV$ be the projected Hessenberg matrix,
and let $g$ be the final Arnoldi residual.  Thus
\begin{equation}
 AV=VH+ge_k^*,
 \qquad z=V\beta,
 \qquad \beta=\norm z\,e_1.
 \label{eq:arnoldi}
\end{equation}
At exact or certified breakdown, $g=0$.  Otherwise $g$ is the unnormalized
next Arnoldi vector multiplied by its subdiagonal coefficient.

\begin{theorem}[Arnoldi power identity]
\label{thm:identity}
Under \eqref{eq:arnoldi}, for every integer $L\ge0$,
\begin{equation}
 A^Lz=VH^L\beta+
 \sum_{j=0}^{L-1}A^{L-1-j}g\,e_k^*H^j\beta.
 \label{eq:power-identity}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For every vector $u$,
\[
 AVu=VHu+g e_k^*u.
\]
Apply this relation successively to $u=H^j\beta$ and telescope:
\[
 A^{j+1}V\beta=A^jVH\beta+A^jg e_k^*\beta.
\]
Summing the resulting one-step identities from $j=0$ to $L-1$ gives
\eqref{eq:power-identity}.  The case $L=0$ is immediate.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Directional power certificate]
\label{prop:certificate}
Let $\rho\ge\norm A$.  Then
\begin{equation}
\norm{A^Lz}
\le
\norm{VH^L\beta}
 +\norm g\sum_{j=0}^{L-1}
 \rho^{L-1-j}\left|e_k^*H^j\beta\right|.
\label{eq:power-upper}
\end{equation}
If $g=0$, the right side equals $\norm{H^L\beta}$ and the identity is
exact.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Apply the triangle inequality to \eqref{eq:power-identity} and use
$\norm{A^u g}\le\rho^u\norm g$ for each $u\ge0$.
\end{proof}

The bound is directional in two distinct ways.  The projected power follows
the actual starting vector through $H$, and the residual contribution is
weighted by the scalar coefficients $e_k^*H^j\beta$.  A common replacement
of all coefficients by $\norm{H}^j\norm\beta$ would erase the first
advantage.

\section{Stein-tail form and extensions}
\label{sec:stein}

Suppose a normalized packet tail has the form
\begin{equation}
 t_L=\sqrt{\kappa}\norm{S^Lz}_{\HS},
 \qquad \kappa\ge0.
\label{eq:stein-tail}
\end{equation}
Applying \cref{prop:certificate} to each source column and summing the
resulting bounds gives a valid Hilbert--Schmidt tail certificate.

\begin{corollary}[Krylov Stein tail]
\label{cor:krylov-stein}
Let $C_\ell$ be the power certificate in \cref{prop:certificate} for a
column $z_\ell$ of a source matrix $Z$.  Then
\begin{equation}
 \sqrt{\kappa}\norm{S^LZ}_{\HS}
 \le \sqrt{\kappa}\sum_\ell C_\ell.
\label{eq:columnwise}
\end{equation}
If the Arnoldi process for every column breaks down, the right side is
exact.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The Hilbert--Schmidt norm is bounded by the sum of the column Euclidean
norms.  Apply \cref{prop:certificate} column by column.  Breakdown makes
each residual term vanish.
\end{proof}

The column sum is conservative.  A block Arnoldi implementation can retain
cross-column Gram information and replace it by a Frobenius residual.  That
is a later layer; the present theorem keeps the certificate scalar and
transparent.

\begin{remark}[Boundary]
\label{rem:boundary}
The residual term in \eqref{eq:power-upper} still uses $\rho^{L-1-j}\norm g$.
If $\rho$ is close to one, or exceeds one because of nonnormal transient
growth, the residual certificate may remain large.  Thus Arnoldi projection
alone is not a physical-family tail theorem.  It exposes exactly which
factor must be replaced next.
\end{remark}

\section{Finite-model audit}
\label{sec:audit}

The pilot uses three deterministic models.  The first is a diagonal
slow/fast surrogate with slow contraction
$0.993861320134583$, matching the left RH-61 endpoint gap, and a source
whose slow component is only $5\times10^{-4}$.  The second is the
two-scalar-block model used by the RH-60 Arb audit.  The third is an
upper-triangular four-step chain designed to expose nonnormal transient
growth.

For each model we compare the ordinary upper
$\norm A^L\norm z$ with the Krylov bound at $L=32$.  The full Krylov
dimension is allowed to terminate exactly.

The first row demonstrates the desired mechanism: the ordinary norm sees a
slow mode with tiny source amplitude, while the Krylov residual records that
directional fact.  The second row is a genuine warning.  With only one
Arnoldi vector, the residual is propagated using a norm bound and the result
is worse than the baseline.  The third row shows partial improvement even
when the ordinary norm exceeds one; spectral stability alone is not enough
to make the residual propagator useful.

\section{Route consequence}
\label{sec:consequence}

RH-62 establishes an intermediate certificate:
\begin{equation}
\text{physical source}
\longrightarrow
\text{Krylov projection}
\longrightarrow
\text{Arnoldi residual}
\longrightarrow
\text{Stein tail}.
\label{eq:route}
\end{equation}
It removes the need to use the largest norm on the projected component.
However, the remaining residual propagation is still a worst-case step.

The next minimal theorem is therefore not simply to take a larger Krylov
space.  It is to replace $\rho^{L-1-j}\norm g$ by a second directional
certificate for $A^u g$.  Two natural choices are a nested Arnoldi
residual recursion and an observability-weighted residual Gramian.  Either
one must preserve a positive upper and admit an a posteriori stopping test.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
 \item A nested residual certificate should be tested first on the RH-60
       two-block model, where the one-step bound fails.
 \item A block version should then retain cross-column phase information
       rather than summing columns absolutely.
 \item Only after those finite-dimensional tests pass should the construction
       be transferred to the physical folded-Gaussian family.
\end{enumerate}

\section{No arithmetic or Hilbert--Polya conclusion}

This paper constructs no self-adjoint operator, no $T\log T$ counting law,
no von Mangoldt or prime-power trace formula, and no completed-zeta
spectral identity.  It makes no Hilbert--Polya or Riemann-hypothesis claim.
The TPC branch is not used.

\section{Reproducibility}

The directory contains the Arnoldi algebra, tests, three-model pilot,
256-bit Arb audit, figures, hashes, and publication artifacts.  The main
commands are shown below.
\begin{verbatim}
pytest -q -p no:cacheprovider
python experiments/run_krylov_tail_pilot.py
python experiments/run_arb_krylov_audit.py
MPLBACKEND=Agg python experiments/make_figures.py
latexmk -pdf -interaction=nonstopmode -halt-on-error main.tex
\end{verbatim}

The finite-dimensional identity and certificate are analytic.  The model
tables are deterministic floating-point evidence, and the Arb scope is the
displayed two-block example only.  Stage A1, Stage A4, a physical-family
directional tail theorem, a self-adjoint Hilbert--Polya operator, a
prime-power trace formula, and a zeta-zero identity remain open.
