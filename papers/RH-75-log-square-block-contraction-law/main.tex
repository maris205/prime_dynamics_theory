\title{Log-Square Horizons and the Square-Root Block-Contraction Law\\
\large A Sufficient Dyadic Criterion for Polylogarithmic Directional Hardy Energy}
\author{Liang Wang}
\date{July 2026}

\begin{abstract}
RH-74 closed the folded-Gaussian production chain at five fixed noise scales.
The remaining Stage A1 problem is no longer finite-scale validation but a
uniform theorem over the small-noise dyadic family.  This paper isolates a
sharp sufficient scaling law for that theorem.

Let $\sigma_k=\sigma_0 2^{-k}$ and let $(A_k,X_k,Y_k)$ be a directional bulk
triple.  Suppose there are block horizons $M_k\le(k+a)^2$ such that
\[
 \norm{A_k^{M_k}}_2\le C_q\sqrt{\sigma_k},\qquad
 \sigma_k\norm{Y_k}_{\F}^2\le C_y,
\]
and suppose the one-block source energy and finite transfer prefix obey
\[
 \sum_{r<M_k}\norm{A_k^rX_k}_{\F}^2
 \le C_s(k+a)^s,qquad
 \sum_{r<M_k}\norm{Y_kA_k^rX_k}_{\F}^2
 \le C_f(k+a)^f.
\]
Then the infinite block tail satisfies
\[
 T_k^2\le
 \frac{C_yC_q^2C_s}{1-C_q^2\sigma_0}(k+a)^s,
\]
and the full Hardy energy squared is
$O((k+a)^{\max\{s,f\}})$.  Thus a log-square growing horizon is fully
compatible with the required polylogarithmic Stage A1 input.  The
$\sqrt{\sigma_k}$ factor is the exact scale that cancels the
$\norm{Y_k}_{\F}^2=O(\sigma_k^{-1})$ mesh growth.

A 256-bit Arb audit combines the analytic-upstream block contractions of
RH-74 with RH-70's source, observation, prefix, and tail balls.  At the five
levels $k=0,\dots,4$, all ten channels satisfy common constants
\[
 a=2,quad C_q=0.086,quad C_y=2.561,quad C_s=3.1,quad
 s=0,quad C_f=0.552,quad f=1.
\]
The common tail envelope is $0.058788$, while the largest certified tail is
$0.049421$.

This identifies the correct all-level target but does not prove it beyond the
five anchors.  The next open mechanism is phase compression or effective-rank
decay sufficient to imply the square-root block law.  Stage A1, unconditional
Stage A4, Hilbert--Polya, and the Riemann Hypothesis remain open.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} block contraction; Hardy energy; dyadic family;

\section{Introduction}

The earlier directional-Hardy program deliberately separated finite matrix
identities from small-noise family statements.  RH-61 showed that a worst-case
one-step geometric envelope may require a power-law horizon when the norm gap
closes \citep{WangHorizon2026}.  RH-68 then proved that stability, spectral
radius, and even a perfectly conditioned Lyapunov metric cannot force a
universal fixed Krylov depth \citep{WangBarrier2026}.  These are genuine
barriers to fixed-depth arguments, not barriers to a controlled growing depth.

RH-70 found production block horizons
\[
 4,9,16,25,32
\]
at dyadic noise scales $0.16,0.08,0.04,0.02,0.01$ and certified the frozen
tails \citep{WangFrozen2026}.  RH-74 transported those contractions to the
analytic upstream triples and closed the complete finite-scale bridge
\citep{WangBridge2026}.  The horizon list suggests the log-square schedule
$(k+2)^2$, but a schedule alone is not enough: the observation Frobenius norm
grows with dimension.

The decisive empirical quantity is instead
\begin{equation}
 \frac{\norm{A_k^{M_k}}}{\sqrt{\sigma_k}}.
 \label{eq:ratio}
\end{equation}
It remains bounded by $0.086$ in every validated channel.  This paper explains
why \eqref{eq:ratio}, rather than a fixed contraction constant, is the natural
uniform target.

\section{Dyadic block setting}

Let
\[
 \sigma_k=\sigma_0 2^{-k},\qquad k\ge0,
\]
and consider finite directional triples $(A_k,X_k,Y_k)$.  Define
\begin{align}
 S_k(M)&=\sum_{r=0}^{M-1}\norm{A_k^rX_k}_{\F}^2,
 \label{eq:source-block}\\
 F_k(M)&=\sum_{r=0}^{M-1}\norm{Y_kA_k^rX_k}_{\F}^2,
 \label{eq:finite-block}\\
 H_k^2&=\sum_{r\ge0}\norm{Y_kA_k^rX_k}_{\F}^2.
 \label{eq:hardy}
\end{align}
The block-power theorem gives, whenever $q_k=\norm{A_k^M}_{\F}<1$,
\begin{equation}
 H_k^2\le F_k(M)
 +\norm{Y_k}_{\F}^2\frac{q_k^2}{1-q_k^2}S_k(M).
 \label{eq:block-tail}
\end{equation}

For the mesh-locked production family, the fine dimension obeys
$n_k\sigma_k=5.12$ and the observation rank is proportional to $n_k$.
Consequently $\norm{Y_k}_{\F}^2=O(\sigma_k^{-1})$.  A merely uniform
$q_k<q<1$ would leave a power divergence in \eqref{eq:block-tail}.  The block
contraction must improve with the mesh.

\section{The square-root block law}

\begin{theorem}[Log-square square-root block criterion]
\label{thm:criterion}
Fix constants $a>0$, $C_q,C_y,C_s,C_f\ge0$ and exponents $s,f\ge0$.
Suppose for every $k\ge0$ there is an integer $M_k$ such that
\begin{align}
 M_k&\le(k+a)^2,
 \label{eq:horizon-law}\\
 q_k:=\norm{A_k^{M_k}}_{\F}&\le C_q\sqrt{\sigma_k},
 \label{eq:sqrt-law}\\
 \sigma_k\norm{Y_k}_{\F}^2&\le C_y,
 \label{eq:observation-law}\\
 S_k(M_k)&\le C_s(k+a)^s,
 \label{eq:source-law}\\
 F_k(M_k)&\le C_f(k+a)^f.
 \label{eq:finite-law}
\end{align}
If $C_q^2\sigma_0<1$, then
\begin{equation}
 H_k^2\le C_f(k+a)^f
 +\frac{C_yC_q^2C_s}{1-C_q^2\sigma_0}(k+a)^s.
 \label{eq:uniform-hardy}
\end{equation}
In particular,
$H_k=O((k+a)^{\max\{s,f\}/2})$, equivalently a polylogarithmic bound in
$1/\sigma_k$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Insert \eqref{eq:sqrt-law}--\eqref{eq:finite-law} into
\eqref{eq:block-tail}.  Since $\sigma_k\le\sigma_0$,
\[
 \frac{\norm{Y_k}_{\F}^2q_k^2}{1-q_k^2}
 \le\frac{(C_y/\sigma_k)C_q^2\sigma_k}
 {1-C_q^2\sigma_0}.
\]
Multiplication by \eqref{eq:source-law} proves
\eqref{eq:uniform-hardy}.  Finally
$k=\log_2(\sigma_0/\sigma_k)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why the exponent $1/2$ is critical]
If $q_k=O(\sigma_k^\theta)$ while
$\norm{Y_k}_{\F}^2=O(\sigma_k^{-1})$, the raw tail prefactor scales as
$\sigma_k^{2\theta-1}$.  Thus $\theta=1/2$ is the threshold between a power
loss and a bounded prefactor.  Additional source growth may still be
polylogarithmic.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[No fixed-depth claim]
The theorem permits $M_k=O(k^2)=O(\log^2(1/\sigma_k))$.  It is therefore
consistent with both RH-61's geometric-horizon warning and RH-68's exact
fixed-depth no-go theorem.
\end{remark}

\section{What remains to prove}

The theorem converts the vague phrase ``uniform horizon control'' into four
specific all-level inequalities: \eqref{eq:sqrt-law}--\eqref{eq:finite-law}.
The five interval anchors show that these inequalities are compatible with the
physical production family and have nontrivial margin.  They do not establish
an induction in $k$ or a continuum estimate.

The next plausible mechanisms are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
 \item phase support compressed to arcs whose width shrinks along the dyadic
       family, giving polynomial approximation at depth $O(k^2)$;
 \item weighted effective-rank decay, allowing a small exceptional phase tail;
 \item a direct strong--weak block estimate for the noisy transfer operator.
\end{enumerate}
RH-76 addresses the first mechanism.  If phase compression fails, RH-77 will
test whether effective-rank decay can replace it.

No all-dyadic law is proved here, so uniform Stage A1 and unconditional Stage
A4 remain open.  This paper does not construct a renormalized determinant,
self-adjoint Hilbert--Polya operator, $T\log T$ counting law, prime-power trace
formula, zeta-zero identity, or proof of the Riemann Hypothesis.

\section{Conclusion}

A universal fixed horizon is neither necessary nor expected.  The correct
target is a log-square horizon paired with a square-root-in-noise block
contraction.  That factor precisely neutralizes the observation's mesh growth
and leaves a polylogarithmic Hardy bound.  Five validated levels satisfy a
single conservative constant ledger.  The route remains open, but its next
mathematical obligation is now explicit.
