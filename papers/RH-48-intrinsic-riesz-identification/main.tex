\title{Quadratic Schur Transport for Intrinsic Peripheral Riesz Identification\\
\large A Dyadic Closure Theorem and the Remaining Directional Resolvent Gate}
\author{Liang Wang}
\date{July 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
For the small-noise folded-Gaussian transfer family, the continuum Perron
and negative-parity weighted Riesz terms are known intrinsically, and their
cell compression preserves the strict $n\sigma^2\to\infty$ bulk-square mesh
law.  The remaining defect compares that compressed continuum term with the
finite Galerkin matrix's own spectral identification:
\[
 \cI_{n,\sigma}
 =\cQ_{\rm per}(E_n\cK_\sigma E_n)
  -E_n\cQ_{\rm per}(\cK_\sigma)E_n.
\]
We isolate its exact mechanism.

Relative to $L^2=V_n\oplus V_n^\perp$, write
\[
 \cK_\sigma=\begin{pmatrix}A_n&B_n\\C_n&D_n\end{pmatrix},
 \qquad
 \Sigma_n(z)=B_n(z-D_n)^{-1}C_n.
\]
For either peripheral contour, the compressed fine weighted term minus the
coarse weighted term is exactly
\[
 \frac1{2\pi i}\int_\Gamma
 z\,(z-A_n-\Sigma_n(z))^{-1}
 B_n(z-D_n)^{-1}C_n(z-A_n)^{-1}\,dz.
\]
Thus intrinsic identification starts quadratically in the two
coarse--detail couplings.  A Hilbert--Schmidt estimate requires only the
directional actions
\[
 (z-A_n-\Sigma_n(z))^{-1}B_n,
 \qquad C_n(z-A_n)^{-1},
\]
not two global $L^2$ resolvent norms.

For nested dyadic Galerkin spaces, the continuum defect is the convergent
telescoping sum of adjacent defects.  Gaussian derivative estimates give
\[
 \norm{B_n}_{\HS},\norm{C_n}_{\HS}
 =O(n^{-1}\sigma^{-3/2}),
 \qquad
 \norm{D_n}=O(n^{-2}\sigma^{-5/2}).
\]
If the product of the normalized directional gains is
$O(\sigma^{-\gamma})$ uniformly over dyadic refinements, then
\[
 \norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 =O(n^{-2}\sigma^{-3-\gamma}).
\]
Consequently every schedule $n\sigma^2\to\infty$ remains sufficient when
$\gamma\le1/2$.  Uniform reduced directional resolvents would give only a
polylogarithmic loss, because the already known residues grow like
$\sqrt{\log(1/\sigma)}$; however that reduced-resolvent statement is not
proved here.

A binary64 audit uses exact Haar compression of sparse row-stochastic
matrices at six noise levels, reaching dimension $204800$ and
$133873007$ nonzero entries.  Eighteen adjacent defects have joint fit
\[
 0.01917\,n^{-1.99524}\sigma^{-1.97197},
\]
and collapse to within nine percent on the candidate clock
$C(n\sigma)^{-2}$.  This sharper law is numerical evidence, not an analytic
claim.  The paper therefore closes the algebraic and mesh-power reduction
while leaving one explicit directional small-noise gate.  No arithmetic
trace formula, self-adjoint spectral realization, zeta-zero identification,
or Riemann-hypothesis conclusion is asserted.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} weighted Riesz projection; Schur complement;
Feshbach map; Galerkin approximation; Hilbert--Schmidt operator; small
noise; transfer operator.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The fixed-noise spectral construction for the quadratic band-merging map
now has two intrinsic peripheral kernels.  The Perron term has kernel
$\pi_\sigma(y)$ and the negative branch has kernel
$\lambda_-(\sigma)h_\sigma(x)g_\sigma(y)$.  Their sum
\begin{equation}
 \Qper
 =\cQ_{+,\sigma}+\cQ_{-,\sigma}
 \label{eq:qper-intro}
\end{equation}
is independent of eigenvector gauge and can be subtracted before forming
the trace-class bulk square
\citep{WangWeightedKernel2026,WangRankTwo2026}.

The first small-noise analysis separated spatial resolution from the
deterministic singular limit.  For the raw folded-Gaussian kernel,
\begin{align}
 \norm{\cK_\sigma}_{\HS}&=O(\sigma^{-1/2}),
 \label{eq:raw-size-intro}\\
 \norm{E_n\cK_\sigma E_n-\cK_\sigma}_{\HS}
 &=O(n^{-1}\sigma^{-3/2}),
 \label{eq:raw-mesh-intro}
\end{align}
and therefore $n\sigma^2\to\infty$ suffices for two-step trace-norm
convergence \citep{WangSmallNoiseMesh2026}.  The subsequent endpoint theorem
showed that the peripheral residues are not uniformly bounded in $L^2$:
\begin{equation}
 \norm{P_{+,\sigma}},\norm{P_{-,\sigma}}
 =\Theta\!\left(\sqrt{\log(1/\sigma)}\right).
 \label{eq:residue-log-intro}
\end{equation}
Hence a fixed-circle global resolvent cannot be $O(1)$ in $L^2$.
Nevertheless direct differentiation of the rank-two kernel gives
\begin{equation}
 \norm{E_n\Qper E_n-\Qper}_{\HS}
 =O(n^{-1}\sigma^{-3/2}),
 \label{eq:qper-compression-intro}
\end{equation}
so the continuum-anchored bulk retains the raw $p>2$ threshold
\citep{WangLogConditioning2026}.

The actual finite matrix does not use the continuum factors.  Put
\begin{equation}
 G_{n,\sigma}=E_n\cK_\sigma E_n
 \label{eq:galerkin-intro}
\end{equation}
on the $n$-cell space.  Its intrinsic bulk is
\begin{equation}
 B_{n,\sigma}^{\rm int}
 =G_{n,\sigma}-\cQ_{\rm per}(G_{n,\sigma}),
 \label{eq:intrinsic-bulk-intro}
\end{equation}
whereas the continuum-anchored compression is
\begin{equation}
 \widetilde B_{n,\sigma}
 =G_{n,\sigma}-E_n\Qper E_n.
 \label{eq:anchored-bulk-intro}
\end{equation}
Their difference is exactly
\begin{equation}
 B_{n,\sigma}^{\rm int}-\widetilde B_{n,\sigma}
 =-\cI_{n,\sigma},
 \qquad
 \cI_{n,\sigma}
 :=\cQ_{\rm per}(G_{n,\sigma})-E_n\Qper E_n.
 \label{eq:identification-intro}
\end{equation}
This was the single unresolved arrow in the preceding paper.

The naive resolvent identity writes the weighted-term error as a product of
two complete contour resolvents and the one-step Galerkin defect.  It is
valid, but it discards the geometry of an orthogonal compression.  The
correct block calculation starts and ends in $V_n$.  An excursion into
$V_n^\perp$ must therefore leave the coarse space and return.  The first
nonzero term is quadratic in the two couplings.

That observation has two consequences.  First, the global logarithmic
resolvent obstruction is no longer the relevant quantity.  Only resolvent
action on the coupling ranges appears.  Second, an adjacent dyadic defect
has order $n^{-2}$ rather than the $n^{-1}$ order of direct kernel
compression.  Summing adjacent defects over all refinements then recovers
the continuum identification defect.

\subsection*{Main results}

The paper proves four statements.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
 \item \textbf{Exact top-left Schur identity.}
 For a block operator
 $T=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}A&B\\C&D\end{smallmatrix}\right)$, the
 difference between the compressed weighted Riesz term of $T$ and that of
 $A$ is the contour integral of
 \[
  zSBR_DCR_A,
  \quad
  R_A=(z-A)^{-1},\quad R_D=(z-D)^{-1},\quad
  S=(z-A-BR_DC)^{-1}.
 \]

 \item \textbf{Directional Hilbert--Schmidt bound.}
 The integral is bounded by the product of
 $\norm{SB}_{\HS}$ and $\norm{CR_A}$, or by the symmetric placement of the
 Hilbert--Schmidt norm.  No global bound for $S$ or $R_A$ is required.

 \item \textbf{Dyadic telescoping.}
 If $\Delta_{n,\sigma}$ denotes the exact adjacent defect between $n$ and
 $2n$, then
 \[
  \cI_{n,\sigma}
  =\sum_{j\ge0}E_n\Delta_{2^jn,\sigma}E_n.
 \]
 A quadratic $4^{-j}$ ledger therefore costs only the geometric factor
 $4/3$.

 \item \textbf{Conditional small-noise closure.}
 If the product of the normalized directional gains is
 $O(\sigma^{-\gamma})$, then
 \[
  \norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
  =O(n^{-2}\sigma^{-3-\gamma}).
 \]
 For $\gamma\le1/2$, every strict $p>2$ schedule remains sufficient for the
 intrinsic bulk square.
\end{enumerate}

The fourth statement is deliberately conditional.  The paper does not
derive a uniform small-noise upper for the reduced directional resolvents.
It replaces the vague request for ``uniform contour control'' by a precise
two-range condition with an exact allowable exponent.  Positive and
negative future results can now be compared against the threshold
$\gamma=1/2$.

\subsection*{Logical layers}

The evidence is separated as follows.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=3.8cm,style=nextline]
 \item[Unconditional operator theory]
 The Schur identity, directional Schatten bound, residue--reduced
 decomposition, and dyadic telescoping theorem.

 \item[Previously proved analytic input]
 Gaussian derivative scales, continuum peripheral factor growth, direct
 rank-two compression, and the anchored bulk-square theorem
 \citep{WangSmallNoiseMesh2026,WangLogConditioning2026}.

 \item[Explicit remaining condition]
 A dyadically uniform bound on two normalized directional resolvent actions.

 \item[Floating diagnostic]
 Exact Haar compression of binary64 sparse matrices.  ``Exact'' here refers
 to the algebraic nesting of the displayed finite matrices, not to interval
 validation of the continuum operator.
\end{description}

\section{Folded-Gaussian family and weighted peripheral terms}
\label{sec:setup}

Let $I=[0,1]$ and let $u_{\rm c}$ be the first band-merging parameter.  In
folded coordinates the deterministic map is
\begin{equation}
 f(x)=1-u_{\rm c}x^2.
 \label{eq:map}
\end{equation}
For $\sigma>0$, define the conditioned folded-Gaussian kernel
\begin{equation}
 k_\sigma(x,y)
 =\frac{\phi_\sigma(y-f(x))+\phi_\sigma(-y-f(x))}
 {Z_\sigma(x)},
 \qquad
 \phi_\sigma(t)=\frac{e^{-t^2/(2\sigma^2)}}
 {\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma},
 \label{eq:kernel}
\end{equation}
where the row normalizer makes
$\int_0^1k_\sigma(x,y)\,dy=1$.  The Markov operator on observables is
\begin{equation}
 (\cK_\sigma v)(x)=\int_0^1k_\sigma(x,y)v(y)\,dy.
 \label{eq:operator}
\end{equation}
It is compact and strongly positive on $L^2(I)$ for every fixed
$\sigma>0$.

The Perron branch has eigenvalue $1$, right observable $\mathbf1$, and
stationary density $\pi_\sigma$.  The negative branch has a simple real
eigenvalue $\lambda_-(\sigma)\to-1$, right observable $h_\sigma$, and left
density $g_\sigma$, normalized by
\begin{equation}
 \cK_\sigma h_\sigma=\lambda_-h_\sigma,
 \quad
 \cK_\sigma^*g_\sigma=\lambda_-g_\sigma,
 \quad
 \int_Ih_\sigma g_\sigma=1.
 \label{eq:parity-factors}
\end{equation}
The weighted Riesz terms are
\begin{align}
 (\cQ_{+,\sigma}v)(x)
 &=\int_I\pi_\sigma(y)v(y)\,dy,
 \label{eq:perron-term}\\
 (\cQ_{-,\sigma}v)(x)
 &=\lambda_-(\sigma)h_\sigma(x)
   \int_Ig_\sigma(y)v(y)\,dy.
 \label{eq:parity-term}
\end{align}

For a positively oriented contour $\Gamma$ in the resolvent set of a
bounded operator $T$, define
\begin{equation}
 \cQ_\Gamma(T)
 :=\frac1{2\pi i}\int_\Gamma z(z-T)^{-1}\,dz.
 \label{eq:weighted-riesz}
\end{equation}
If $\Gamma$ encloses one simple eigenvalue $\lambda$, then
$\cQ_\Gamma(T)=\lambda P_\lambda$.  We use two disjoint fixed-geometry
contours $\Gamma_+$ and $\Gamma_-$ around the Perron and parity branches and
put
\begin{equation}
 \cQ_{\rm per}(T)
 =\cQ_{\Gamma_+}(T)+\cQ_{\Gamma_-}(T).
 \label{eq:peripheral-functional}
\end{equation}

Let $E_n$ be orthogonal averaging on the $n$ equal cells of $I$, and write
$V_n=\operatorname{Ran}E_n$.  All finite Galerkin operators are understood
as operators on $V_n$ or as zero-extended operators on $L^2(I)$, according
to context.

\section{The exact top-left Schur identity}
\label{sec:schur}

We first work on an arbitrary Hilbert space.  Let $P$ be an orthogonal
projection, $Q=\Id-P$, and decompose
\begin{equation}
 T=\begin{pmatrix}A&B\\C&D\end{pmatrix}
 \quad\text{on}\quad
 H=PH\oplus QH,
 \label{eq:block-T}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
 A=PTP,
 \quad B=PTQ,
 \quad C=QTP,
 \quad D=QTQ.
 \label{eq:block-definitions}
\end{equation}

For $z$ in the resolvent set of $D$, put
\begin{equation}
 R_D(z)=(z-D)^{-1},
 \quad
 \Sigma(z)=BR_D(z)C,
 \quad
 S(z)=(z-A-\Sigma(z))^{-1}.
 \label{eq:schur-objects}
\end{equation}
The familiar block inverse is
\begin{equation}
 (z-T)^{-1}
 =\begin{pmatrix}
 S&SBR_D\\
 R_DCS&R_D+R_DCSBR_D
 \end{pmatrix}.
 \label{eq:block-resolvent}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Exact intrinsic Schur identity]
\label{thm:exact-schur}
Let $\Gamma$ lie in the resolvent sets of $T$, $A$, and $D$.  Then
\begin{equation}
 P\cQ_\Gamma(T)P-\cQ_\Gamma(A)
 =\frac1{2\pi i}\int_\Gamma
 zS(z)B R_D(z)C(z-A)^{-1}\,dz.
 \label{eq:exact-schur-identity}
\end{equation}
In particular, the top-left weighted-Riesz defect starts quadratically in
$B$ and $C$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The top-left block of \eqref{eq:block-resolvent} is $S$.  With
$R_A=(z-A)^{-1}$, the resolvent identity gives
\begin{equation}
 S-R_A=S\Sigma R_A=SBR_DCR_A.
 \label{eq:schur-resolvent-difference}
\end{equation}
Insert this identity into the contour definition
\eqref{eq:weighted-riesz}.
\end{proof}

The theorem is stronger than applying the ordinary resolvent identity to
$T$ and $A\oplus0$.  The latter sees the off-diagonal perturbation
$\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&B\\C&D\end{smallmatrix}\right)$ linearly and
pays for two complete resolvents.  The top-left Schur identity records that
an excursion contributing to $PH\to PH$ must contain both $C$ and $B$.

\subsection{Directional Schatten bound}

The exact formula naturally places the Schatten norm on one composed
directional factor.

\begin{theorem}[One-sided directional Schur bound]
\label{thm:directional-bound}
Under the hypotheses of \cref{thm:exact-schur}, suppose $B,C\in\HS$.  Put
\begin{align}
 L_B^{(2)}
 &:=\sup_{z\in\Gamma}\norm{S(z)B}_{\HS},
 &
 L_C^{(\infty)}
 &:=\sup_{z\in\Gamma}\norm{C(z-A)^{-1}},
 \label{eq:left-right-directional}\\
 L_B^{(\infty)}
 &:=\sup_{z\in\Gamma}\norm{S(z)B},
 &
 L_C^{(2)}
 &:=\sup_{z\in\Gamma}\norm{C(z-A)^{-1}}_{\HS}.
 \label{eq:left-right-directional-symmetric}
\end{align}
Then
\begin{align}
 \norm{P\cQ_\Gamma(T)P-\cQ_\Gamma(A)}_{\HS}
 \le{}&
 \frac{\operatorname{length}(\Gamma)}{2\pi}
 \max_{z\in\Gamma}|z|
 \sup_{z\in\Gamma}\norm{R_D(z)}
 \notag\\
 &\times
 \min\!\left\{
 L_B^{(2)}L_C^{(\infty)},
 L_B^{(\infty)}L_C^{(2)}
 \right\}.
 \label{eq:directional-schur-bound}
\end{align}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Use \eqref{eq:exact-schur-identity}, the ideal inequalities
$\norm{XY}_{\HS}\le\norm{X}_{\HS}\norm{Y}$ and
$\norm{XY}_{\HS}\le\norm{X}\norm{Y}_{\HS}$, and then integrate the uniform
majorant around $\Gamma$.
\end{proof}

The quantities in \eqref{eq:left-right-directional} ask only how the
coarse resolvents act after entrance through $B$ and before exit through
$C$.  They may stay small even when the global resolvent is large on an
unrelated direction.  This is the same goal-oriented principle that appears
in directional Feshbach and primal--dual residual estimates, now applied to
the weighted Riesz term itself.

\subsection{Residues versus reduced directional resolvents}

The logarithmic lower bound in \eqref{eq:residue-log-intro} comes from the
residue.  It should not be confused with an upper for the reduced
complement.  The Schur formula permits an exact separation.

Assume $\Gamma$ encloses one simple eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $T$ and one
simple eigenvalue $\mu$ of $A$.  Let $\Pi_T$ and $\Pi_A$ be their Riesz
projections.  The top-left resolvent residue is
\begin{equation}
 \widehat\Pi_T=P\Pi_TP.
 \label{eq:compressed-residue}
\end{equation}
On $\Gamma$ write
\begin{align}
 S(z)&=\frac{\widehat\Pi_T}{z-\lambda}+S^\circ(z),
 \label{eq:S-reduced}\\
 (z-A)^{-1}&=\frac{\Pi_A}{z-\mu}+R_A^\circ(z).
 \label{eq:RA-reduced}
\end{align}

\begin{proposition}[Residue--reduced directional split]
\label{prop:residue-reduced}
Let
\begin{equation}
 d_T=\min_{z\in\Gamma}|z-\lambda|,
 \qquad
 d_A=\min_{z\in\Gamma}|z-\mu|.
 \label{eq:pole-distances}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{align}
 L_B^{(2)}
 &\le
 \sup_\Gamma\norm{S^\circ(z)B}_{\HS}
 +d_T^{-1}\norm{\widehat\Pi_TB}_{\HS},
 \label{eq:left-reduced-bound}\\
 L_C^{(\infty)}
 &\le
 \sup_\Gamma\norm{CR_A^\circ(z)}
 +d_A^{-1}\norm{C\Pi_A}.
 \label{eq:right-reduced-bound}
\end{align}
The symmetric bounds hold with the Schatten norm interchanged.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Multiply \eqref{eq:S-reduced} by $B$ and
\eqref{eq:RA-reduced} by $C$, then apply the triangle inequality.
\end{proof}

Suppose the contours have fixed geometry, the fine and coarse projection
norms are both $O(\sqrt{\log(1/\sigma)})$, and the normalized reduced
directional actions are $O(1)$.  Then the product in
\eqref{eq:directional-schur-bound} is at
most $O(\log(1/\sigma))$ after the two coupling norms are factored out.  The
global reduced resolvent may be much larger; \cref{prop:residue-reduced}
does not ask for it.  Conversely, this paper does not prove even the weaker
directional $O(1)$ statement.  It identifies it as the exact next analytic
gate.

\section{Dyadic telescoping of the continuum defect}
\label{sec:dyadic}

The Schur identity compares one fine operator with one coarse block.  We now
show that adjacent comparisons sum exactly to the continuum identification
defect.

Let the cell spaces be nested dyadically:
\begin{equation}
 V_n\subset V_{2n}\subset V_{4n}\subset\cdots,
 \qquad
 E_nE_{2n}=E_n.
 \label{eq:nested-spaces}
\end{equation}
For fixed $\sigma$, put
\begin{equation}
 A_{n,\sigma}=E_n\cK_\sigma E_n.
 \label{eq:An}
\end{equation}
All weighted terms below use the same pair of peripheral contours.

\begin{definition}[Adjacent intrinsic defect]
\label{def:adjacent-defect}
Define
\begin{equation}
 \Delta_{n,\sigma}
 :=\cQ_{\rm per}(A_{n,\sigma})
 -E_n\cQ_{\rm per}(A_{2n,\sigma})E_n.
 \label{eq:adjacent-defect}
\end{equation}
The continuum defect is
\begin{equation}
 \cI_{n,\sigma}
 =\cQ_{\rm per}(A_{n,\sigma})
 -E_n\cQ_{\rm per}(\cK_\sigma)E_n.
 \label{eq:continuum-defect}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Exact dyadic recursion]
\label{lem:dyadic-recursion}
Whenever the displayed weighted terms are defined,
\begin{equation}
 \cI_{n,\sigma}
 =\Delta_{n,\sigma}+E_n\cI_{2n,\sigma}E_n.
 \label{eq:dyadic-recursion}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Add and subtract
$E_n\cQ_{\rm per}(A_{2n,\sigma})E_n$ in
\eqref{eq:continuum-defect}, and use $E_nE_{2n}=E_n$.
\end{proof}

For each fixed $\sigma>0$, the kernel is smooth and compact.  Standard
collectively compact Galerkin theory gives convergence of isolated Riesz
terms once the contours are fixed in the resolvent set
\citep{Chatelin1983,Kato1995}.  Thus
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\cI_{2^mn,\sigma}}_{\HS}\longrightarrow0
 \qquad(m\to\infty).
 \label{eq:fixed-sigma-tail}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Dyadic identification series]
\label{thm:dyadic-series}
For fixed $\sigma$, suppose the two peripheral contours remain admissible
along the dyadic chain and \eqref{eq:fixed-sigma-tail} holds.  Then
\begin{equation}
 \cI_{n,\sigma}
 =\sum_{j=0}^\infty
 E_n\Delta_{2^jn,\sigma}E_n,
 \label{eq:dyadic-series}
\end{equation}
where each adjacent defect is naturally extended to $L^2(I)$.  In
particular, if
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\Delta_{2^jn,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 \le M_{n,\sigma}4^{-j},
 \label{eq:quadratic-dyadic-ledger}
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 \le\frac43M_{n,\sigma}.
 \label{eq:four-thirds}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Iterate \eqref{eq:dyadic-recursion} $m$ times.  Orthogonal compression is a
contraction in $\HS$, so the finite sum is dominated by the sum of the
adjacent norms.  Let $m\to\infty$ and use
\eqref{eq:fixed-sigma-tail}.  Under
\eqref{eq:quadratic-dyadic-ledger}, sum the geometric series
$\sum_{j\ge0}4^{-j}=4/3$.
\end{proof}

This theorem is useful both analytically and computationally.  A future
validated proof need not compare one finite matrix directly with an
infinite-dimensional contour integral.  It may certify a sequence of
adjacent Schur steps and sum their explicit tails.

\section{Gaussian block scales}
\label{sec:block-scales}

Take the adjacent split
\begin{equation}
 V_{2n}=V_n\oplus W_n,
 \label{eq:haar-split}
\end{equation}
where $W_n$ is the Haar detail space, and write
\begin{equation}
 A_{2n,\sigma}
 =\begin{pmatrix}
 A_{n,\sigma}&B_{n,\sigma}\\
 C_{n,\sigma}&D_{n,\sigma}
 \end{pmatrix}.
 \label{eq:adjacent-blocks}
\end{equation}

Let
\begin{equation}
 K_x(\sigma)=\norm{\partial_xk_\sigma}_{L^2(I^2)},
 \quad
 K_y(\sigma)=\norm{\partial_yk_\sigma}_{L^2(I^2)},
 \quad
 K_{xy}(\sigma)=\norm{\partial_x\partial_yk_\sigma}_{L^2(I^2)}.
 \label{eq:derivative-envelopes}
\end{equation}
The one-dimensional Poincar\'e--Wirtinger inequality applied in the
appropriate cell variable gives
\begin{align}
 \norm{B_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 &\le\frac{K_y(\sigma)}{\pi n},
 \label{eq:B-bound}\\
 \norm{C_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 &\le\frac{K_x(\sigma)}{\pi n},
 \label{eq:C-bound}\\
 \norm{D_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 &\le\frac{K_{xy}(\sigma)}{\pi^2n^2}.
 \label{eq:D-bound}
\end{align}
These are the infinite-complement and adjacent-Haar estimates used in the
validated fixed-noise Schur constructions
\citep{WangWeightedKernel2026,WangRankTwo2026}.

For the normalized folded Gaussian, differentiation of the kernel and its
row normalizer gives, for fixed derivative order,
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\partial_x^a\partial_y^b k_\sigma}_{L^2(I^2)}
 =O(\sigma^{-a-b-1/2}).
 \label{eq:gaussian-all-derivatives}
\end{equation}
In particular,
\begin{align}
 K_x(\sigma)+K_y(\sigma)&=O(\sigma^{-3/2}),
 \label{eq:first-derivatives}\\
 K_{xy}(\sigma)&=O(\sigma^{-5/2}).
 \label{eq:mixed-derivative}
\end{align}
Thus
\begin{align}
 \norm{B_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 +\norm{C_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 &=O(n^{-1}\sigma^{-3/2}),
 \label{eq:coupling-clock}\\
 \norm{D_{n,\sigma}}
 &=O(n^{-2}\sigma^{-5/2}).
 \label{eq:detail-clock}
\end{align}

Let $\rho_\Gamma=\min_{z\in\Gamma}|z|$.  If
$\norm{D_{n,\sigma}}<\rho_\Gamma$, then
\begin{equation}
 \sup_{z\in\Gamma}\norm{(z-D_{n,\sigma})^{-1}}
 \le\frac1{\rho_\Gamma-\norm{D_{n,\sigma}}}.
 \label{eq:detail-resolvent}
\end{equation}
Under $n\sigma^2\to\infty$,
\begin{equation}
 n^{-2}\sigma^{-5/2}
 =(n\sigma^2)^{-2}\sigma^{3/2}\longrightarrow0,
 \label{eq:detail-vanishes}
\end{equation}
so the detail resolvent is uniformly $O(1)$ on either fixed peripheral
contour.  The unresolved growth lies entirely in the two coarse
directional actions.

\section{The directional small-noise condition}
\label{sec:directional-condition}

For each branch $s\in\{+,-\}$, let $\Gamma_s$ be its contour.  Relative to
the adjacent split \eqref{eq:adjacent-blocks}, define
\begin{align}
 R_{A,s}(z)&=(z-A_{n,\sigma})^{-1},
 \notag\\
 R_{D,s}(z)&=(z-D_{n,\sigma})^{-1},
 \notag\\
 S_s(z)&=
 \left(z-A_{n,\sigma}
 -B_{n,\sigma}R_{D,s}(z)C_{n,\sigma}\right)^{-1}.
 \label{eq:branch-schur-objects}
\end{align}

When the denominator coupling norm is nonzero, put
\begin{align}
 \ell_{B,s}^{(2)}(n,\sigma)
 &:=\sup_{z\in\Gamma_s}
 \frac{\norm{S_s(z)B_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}}
 {\norm{B_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}},
 \label{eq:normalized-left-hs}\\
 \ell_{C,s}^{(\infty)}(n,\sigma)
 &:=\sup_{z\in\Gamma_s}
 \frac{\norm{C_{n,\sigma}R_{A,s}(z)}}
 {\norm{C_{n,\sigma}}},
 \label{eq:normalized-right-op}
\end{align}
and define the symmetric pair by interchanging the Schatten norm.  Zero
couplings are assigned zero gain.  The combined directional gain is
\begin{equation}
 \cL_{n,\sigma}
 :=\sum_{s\in\{+,-\}}
 \min\!\left\{
 \ell_{B,s}^{(2)}\ell_{C,s}^{(\infty)},
 \ell_{B,s}^{(\infty)}\ell_{C,s}^{(2)}
 \right\}.
 \label{eq:combined-gain}
\end{equation}
Fixed contour length, maximum modulus, and the bounded detail resolvent are
absorbed into constants below.

\begin{condition}[Dyadic directional gain of order $\gamma$]
\label{cond:directional-gain}
There are $C<\infty$, $\sigma_0>0$, and $n_0(\sigma)$ such that the two
contours lie in the required resolvent sets and
\begin{equation}
 \sup_{j\ge0}\cL_{2^jn,\sigma}
 \le C\sigma^{-\gamma}
 \label{eq:gain-condition}
\end{equation}
for $0<\sigma<\sigma_0$ and $n\ge n_0(\sigma)$.
\end{condition}

This condition is weaker than a global reduced-resolvent bound.  It does not
control the resolvent on vectors orthogonal to the two coupling ranges.  It
also retains the full fine Schur inverse on the left, so no perturbative
replacement by the coarse resolvent is hidden in the definition.

\begin{theorem}[Quadratic adjacent identification bound]
\label{thm:adjacent-bound}
Under \cref{cond:directional-gain} and the Gaussian derivative estimates,
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\Delta_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 =O(n^{-2}\sigma^{-3-\gamma}).
 \label{eq:adjacent-small-noise}
\end{equation}
The same bound holds uniformly at level $2^jn$ with an additional factor
$4^{-j}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Apply \cref{thm:directional-bound} to each contour and use
\eqref{eq:coupling-clock}, \eqref{eq:detail-resolvent}, and
\eqref{eq:gain-condition}.  The two coupling norms contribute
$n^{-2}\sigma^{-3}$; replacing $n$ by $2^jn$ contributes $4^{-j}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Intrinsic small-noise identification theorem]
\label{thm:intrinsic-identification}
Under the hypotheses of \cref{thm:adjacent-bound} and the fixed-$\sigma$
Galerkin convergence in \cref{thm:dyadic-series},
\begin{equation}
 \boxed{
 \norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 =O(n^{-2}\sigma^{-3-\gamma}).}
 \label{eq:intrinsic-identification-bound}
\end{equation}
If $\gamma\le1/2$ and
\begin{equation}
 n(\sigma)\sigma^2\longrightarrow\infty,
 \label{eq:p-two-again}
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 =o(n^{-1}\sigma^{-3/2}).
 \label{eq:identification-lower-order}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The first statement follows from \cref{thm:dyadic-series} and the factor
$4/3$.  Divide \eqref{eq:intrinsic-identification-bound} by the anchored
one-step clock:
\begin{equation}
 \frac{n^{-2}\sigma^{-3-\gamma}}
 {n^{-1}\sigma^{-3/2}}
 =\frac{\sigma^{1/2-\gamma}}{n\sigma^2}.
 \label{eq:relative-clock}
\end{equation}
For $\gamma\le1/2$, the numerator is bounded and the denominator tends to
infinity.
\end{proof}

For a pure power $n(\sigma)\asymp\sigma^{-p}$, the intrinsic defect has
exponent
\begin{equation}
 2p-3-\gamma,
 \label{eq:identification-power}
\end{equation}
while its ratio to the anchored one-step error has exponent
\begin{equation}
 p-\frac32-\gamma.
 \label{eq:relative-power}
\end{equation}
Combining this with the anchored trace threshold $p>2$ gives the sufficient
condition
\begin{equation}
 p>\max\!\left\{2,\frac32+\gamma\right\}.
 \label{eq:general-p-threshold}
\end{equation}
Thus $\gamma=1/2$ is the precise boundary at which all strict $p>2$
schedules survive.

\begin{corollary}[Slowly varying directional closure]
\label{cor:polylog-closure}
If, for some fixed $m$, the dyadic directional gain satisfies
\begin{equation}
 \sup_{j\ge0}\cL_{2^jn,\sigma}
 =O((\log(1/\sigma))^m),
 \label{eq:polylog-gain}
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
 \norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 =O\!\left(
 n^{-2}\sigma^{-3}(\log(1/\sigma))^m
 \right),
 \label{eq:polylog-identification}
\end{equation}
and every schedule \eqref{eq:p-two-again} satisfies
\eqref{eq:identification-lower-order}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Every fixed power of $\log(1/\sigma)$ is
$o(\sigma^{-1/2})$.  Repeat \eqref{eq:relative-clock}.
\end{proof}

By \cref{prop:residue-reduced}, uniformly bounded reduced directional
resolvents together with $O(\sqrt{\log})$ bounds for both the fine and
coarse residues would give \cref{cor:polylog-closure} with $m=1$.  The
continuum residue law is known; its dyadically uniform coarse counterpart
and the reduced directional bounds remain part of the premise.  The
implication is rigorous, but that premise is not proved here.

\section{Transfer to the actual intrinsic bulk square}
\label{sec:bulk-transfer}

The continuum-anchored theorem gives
\begin{align}
 \norm{\widetilde B_{n,\sigma}-\cB_\sigma}_{\HS}
 &=O(n^{-1}\sigma^{-3/2}),
 \label{eq:anchored-hs-recall}\\
 \norm{\widetilde B_{n,\sigma}^2-\cB_\sigma^2}_{\TC}
 &=O(n^{-1}\sigma^{-2})+O(n^{-2}\sigma^{-3}),
 \label{eq:anchored-trace-recall}\\
 \norm{\widetilde B_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 &=O(\sigma^{-1/2}).
 \label{eq:anchored-size-recall}
\end{align}
The exact relation \eqref{eq:identification-intro} now transfers those
bounds.

\begin{corollary}[Conditional intrinsic bulk-square closure]
\label{cor:intrinsic-bulk-square}
Under \cref{cond:directional-gain} with $\gamma\le1/2$ and
$n\sigma^2\to\infty$,
\begin{align}
 \norm{B_{n,\sigma}^{\rm int}-\cB_\sigma}_{\HS}
 &=O(n^{-1}\sigma^{-3/2}),
 \label{eq:intrinsic-hs-final}\\
 \norm{(B_{n,\sigma}^{\rm int})^2-\cB_\sigma^2}_{\TC}
 &\longrightarrow0.
 \label{eq:intrinsic-trace-final}
\end{align}
For $n\asymp\sigma^{-p}$, every $p>2$ is sufficient.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Equation \eqref{eq:identification-lower-order} and
\eqref{eq:identification-intro} preserve
\eqref{eq:anchored-hs-recall}.  For the square difference between intrinsic
and anchored bulks,
\begin{align}
 \norm{(B_{n,\sigma}^{\rm int})^2
       -\widetilde B_{n,\sigma}^2}_{\TC}
 &\le
 \norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
 \left(2\norm{\widetilde B_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}
       +\norm{\cI_{n,\sigma}}_{\HS}\right).
 \label{eq:intrinsic-square-difference}
\end{align}
The leading additional clock is
$n^{-2}\sigma^{-7/2-\gamma}$.  Relative to the leading anchored trace clock
$n^{-1}\sigma^{-2}$, its ratio is again
$n^{-1}\sigma^{-3/2-\gamma}$, equal to the right-hand side of
\eqref{eq:relative-clock}.  It tends to zero for $\gamma\le1/2$ under
$n\sigma^2\to\infty$.  Combine with
\eqref{eq:anchored-trace-recall}.
\end{proof}

The conclusion is important but conditional: the finite matrix's actual
weighted-Riesz subtraction inherits the anchored theorem if and only if the
new directional gate is met.  The paper has not silently replaced that gate
by the observed finite-matrix scaling.

\section{Exact-Haar floating audit}
\label{sec:numerics}

The analytic theorem identifies a quadratic clock.  We test that clock on
the same row-normalized eight-sigma sparse folded-Gaussian family used in
the preceding small-noise studies.

\subsection{Nested construction}

For each $\sigma$, build one finest row-stochastic matrix $K_N$ with
\begin{equation}
 N\sigma\simeq40.96.
 \label{eq:finest-resolution}
\end{equation}
Every coarser matrix is then obtained by exact orthogonal Haar compression,
not by rebuilding a separate midpoint quadrature.  If $R$ averages each
pair of fine values and $L$ repeats a coarse value on both children, then
\begin{equation}
 K_{N/2}=RK_NL,
 \qquad RL=\Id.
 \label{eq:exact-haar-compression}
\end{equation}
Thus each displayed adjacent comparison is an exact finite-dimensional
instance of \cref{thm:exact-schur}, up to floating eigensolver error.

Let a weighted rank-two term be represented as
\begin{equation}
 Q_N=U_NV_N^{\mathsf T},
 \label{eq:factorization}
\end{equation}
where the columns of $U_N$ are the right value vectors and the columns of
$V_N$ are the left cell-mass vectors, with the parity column multiplied by
$\lambda_-$.  Under a compression ratio $r$, the exact coarse factors of
$RQ_NL$ are
\begin{equation}
 (U_N)_{\rm comp}=\operatorname{blockmean}_r(U_N),
 \qquad
 (V_N)_{\rm comp}=\operatorname{blocksum}_r(V_N).
 \label{eq:factor-compression}
\end{equation}
All Frobenius norms and singular values are evaluated from matrices of size
at most $4\times4$ using factor Gram matrices.  The dense rank-two operator
is never formed.

\section{What is closed and what remains}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The intrinsic identification problem has now separated into an algebraic
part, a mesh part, and a genuinely spectral part.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
 \item \textbf{Algebraic part: closed.}
 The exact top-left difference is a Schur self-energy integral and begins
 quadratically in the coarse--detail couplings.

 \item \textbf{Trace-ideal part: closed.}
 The Hilbert--Schmidt estimate needs only two directional resolvent actions.
 Adjacent quadratic defects telescope with geometric factor $4/3$.

 \item \textbf{Small-noise exponent reduction: closed.}
 If the directional product grows like $\sigma^{-\gamma}$, the exact mesh
 threshold is \eqref{eq:general-p-threshold}.  The previous $p>2$ theorem
 survives for every $\gamma\le1/2$ and, in particular, for every
 polylogarithmic loss.

 \item \textbf{Directional reduced-resolvent estimate: open.}
 The present paper does not prove a uniform or $\sigma^{-1/2}$ upper for the
 normalized directional gain.  The global $L^2$ residue obstruction neither
 proves nor disproves that estimate.

 \item \textbf{Sharper $(n\sigma)^{-2}$ law: open.}
 Exact-Haar data strongly support it, but the available Gaussian
 Hilbert--Schmidt derivative envelope proves only the more conservative
 $n^{-2}\sigma^{-3}$ self-energy clock before directional gain.
\end{enumerate}

The next useful theorem should therefore target one of two statements:
\begin{equation}
 \sup_{j\ge0}\cL_{2^jn,\sigma}
 =O((\log(1/\sigma))^m)
 \label{eq:next-polylog-target}
\end{equation}
for some fixed $m$, or at least
\begin{equation}
 \sup_{j\ge0}\cL_{2^jn,\sigma}
 =O(\sigma^{-1/2}).
 \label{eq:next-half-power-target}
\end{equation}
Possible tools include a reduced Grushin system built from the known left
and right peripheral factors, a primal--dual enclosure of the two coupling
ranges, or a mixed Banach/Hilbert norm that separates deterministic bulk
transport from endpoint residues.  A negative result with growth exponent
$\gamma>1/2$ would also be decisive: it would show that the strict $p>2$
route needs a stronger mesh schedule or a different intrinsic subtraction.

The scope remains operator-theoretic.  None of the results identifies a
dynamical trace with primes or prime powers, constructs a self-adjoint
Hilbert--P\'olya operator, derives a $T\log T$ counting law, locates a Riemann
zero, or proves the Riemann hypothesis.
